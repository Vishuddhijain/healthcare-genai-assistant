TOPIC: Bone Health — Osteoporosis and Calcium Deficiency
CATEGORY: Non-Communicable Disease (Musculoskeletal / Metabolic Bone Disease)
SOURCE: WHO + IOF + NHP India + ICMR - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/osteoporosis | nhp.gov.in | iofbonehealth.org
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Bones are living tissue that continuously break down and rebuild throughout life. Bone mass peaks around age 25–30, then gradually declines. Osteoporosis is a skeletal disease characterised by reduced bone density and deteriorating bone microarchitecture, leading to increased fracture risk. India has a very high and underrecognised burden of osteoporosis — estimated 50 million Indians are affected, with women disproportionately impacted after menopause. Indian populations also have a high prevalence of Vitamin D deficiency (60–90% in some studies), which — along with dietary calcium inadequacy — is a primary driver of poor bone health. Calcium deficiency, rickets in children, and osteomalacia in adults are also significant.

--- BONE HEALTH CONDITIONS ---

1. OSTEOPOROSIS:
- Low bone density leading to fragile, fracture-prone bones
- Often asymptomatic until a fracture occurs — called the "silent disease"
- Most common osteoporotic fractures in India: Hip (most serious), vertebral (spine), wrist (Colles' fracture)
- Hip fracture in elderly: ~20–30% die within a year; majority require assistance for daily activities thereafter

2. OSTEOPENIA:
- Bone density lower than normal but not yet at osteoporosis level
- Precursor stage — intervention can prevent progression to osteoporosis

3. OSTEOMALACIA (Adult Rickets):
- Softening of bones due to Vitamin D and/or calcium deficiency in adults
- Causes: Vitamin D deficiency (sunlight deprivation, malabsorption), low calcium diet
- Symptoms: Bone pain (especially back, hips, legs), muscle weakness, waddling gait

4. RICKETS (Children):
- Bone softening in growing children due to Vitamin D and/or calcium deficiency
- Causes bowing of legs, soft skull, delayed tooth eruption, widened wrists
- Common in infants and toddlers with limited sun exposure or pure breastfeeding without supplementation
- Treatment: Vitamin D and calcium supplementation under medical supervision

5. CALCIUM DEFICIENCY:
- Hypocalcaemia (low blood calcium) causes: Muscle cramps and spasms, tingling in hands/feet, numbness, convulsions (severe)
- Chronic dietary calcium insufficiency contributes to osteoporosis over time
- India-specific: Low dietary calcium intake is common due to limited dairy consumption in some populations and low bioavailability of plant-based calcium

--- SYMPTOMS ---

Osteoporosis (early stage — usually none):
- No symptoms until a fracture occurs

Signs suggesting bone loss or fracture:
- Back pain (sudden, severe — possible vertebral compression fracture)
- Loss of height over time (vertebral compression)
- Stooped posture (kyphosis / "dowager's hump")
- Fragility fracture: Fracture after minimal trauma (fall from standing height or less)
- Wrist fracture on falling forward (Colles' fracture)

Osteomalacia / Rickets:
- Diffuse bone pain (aching, dull) — especially back, hips, pelvis, feet
- Muscle weakness (difficulty climbing stairs, rising from sitting)
- Bone tenderness on pressure
- In children: Bowed legs, rachitic rosary (knobby ribs), delayed motor milestones

Calcium deficiency:
- Muscle cramps and spasms (especially calves)
- Tingling or numbness in fingers, toes, face
- Dental problems (weak/brittle teeth)
- Brittle nails
- Fatigue

--- RISK FACTORS ---

For Osteoporosis:
- Female sex (women lose bone faster after menopause due to oestrogen loss)
- Age >50 (men) or post-menopause (women)
- Low calcium and Vitamin D intake throughout life
- Vitamin D deficiency (extremely prevalent in India — even with abundant sunshine, due to skin coverage, indoor lifestyle, high melanin)
- Physical inactivity / sedentary lifestyle
- Low body weight (BMI <19 kg/m²) — less mechanical loading on bones
- Smoking
- Excessive alcohol use
- Family history of osteoporosis or fragility fractures
- Long-term medications: Corticosteroids (most common), proton pump inhibitors, antiepileptics, aromatase inhibitors, DMPA (injectable contraceptive)
- Medical conditions: Hyperthyroidism, hyperparathyroidism, coeliac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, rheumatoid arthritis, COPD, diabetes, chronic kidney disease
- Early menopause (<45 years) or surgical removal of ovaries
- Prolonged immobilisation

For Calcium Deficiency:
- Low dairy and calcium-rich food intake
- Vitamin D deficiency (needed for calcium absorption)
- High phytate diet (whole grains, legumes — reduces calcium absorption)
- Lactose intolerance reducing dairy intake
- Malabsorption syndromes

For Vitamin D Deficiency (India context):
- Paradoxically high in sunny India; due to: Indoor lifestyle, skin coverage by clothing, high melanin pigmentation, limited dietary sources, sunscreen use
- Exclusively breastfed infants (breast milk has low Vitamin D)
- Elderly (reduced skin synthesis and outdoor activity)
- People with malabsorption

--- PREVENTION ---
Calcium Intake (adequate dietary calcium throughout life):
- Adults: 1000 mg/day; women >50 years and men >70 years: 1200 mg/day
- Children and adolescents: 1000–1300 mg/day
- Calcium-rich foods in India:
  * Dairy: Milk (~300 mg/glass), curd/yoghurt (~150–200 mg/cup), paneer, cheese
  * Non-dairy (important for vegetarians): Ragi (finger millet) — one of the richest plant sources (~350 mg/100g), til (sesame seeds), rajma, chana, soyabean, tofu, dark green vegetables (drumstick leaves, amaranth, fenugreek), small fish eaten with bones (sardines)
  * Fortified foods: Fortified milk, fortified orange juice, fortified cereals

Vitamin D:
- Sun exposure: 15–30 minutes of midday sun on arms and face (without sunscreen) — 3–4 days per week
- Dietary sources (limited): Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel), egg yolk, fortified dairy products, mushrooms exposed to UV
- Vitamin D supplementation: 600–800 IU/day for adults (up to 2000 IU/day is safe); higher doses may be needed for deficiency — doctor prescribed

Physical Activity:
- Weight-bearing exercises (walking, jogging, dancing, aerobics, stair climbing): Stimulate bone remodelling and build bone density
- Resistance/strength training: Increases bone density and muscle strength (reduces fall risk)
- Balance and flexibility exercises (yoga, tai chi): Reduce fall risk in elderly
- Minimum: 30 minutes of moderate activity, 5 days a week

Lifestyle:
- Quit smoking
- Limit alcohol
- Prevent falls in elderly: Remove trip hazards at home, use grab bars in bathrooms, ensure adequate lighting, wear non-slip footwear, treat vision problems

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- DEXA Scan (Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry): Gold standard for bone density measurement; reports T-score
  * T-score ≥ -1.0: Normal
  * T-score -1.0 to -2.5: Osteopenia
  * T-score ≤ -2.5: Osteoporosis
- FRAX Tool: 10-year fracture probability calculator (WHO tool) — combines BMD and clinical risk factors
- Serum 25(OH) Vitamin D: Measures Vitamin D status (deficient <20 ng/mL; insufficient 20–29; sufficient ≥30 ng/mL)
- Serum calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase, PTH: Assess metabolic bone disease
- X-ray: Detects fractures and gross bone loss (not sensitive for early osteoporosis)

Who should have DEXA screening:
- All women ≥65 years
- All men ≥70 years
- Younger postmenopausal women and men 50–69 with risk factors
- Anyone who has had a fragility fracture

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- Adequate calcium and Vitamin D supplementation (if dietary intake is insufficient) — foundational
- Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate, zoledronic acid): First-line for osteoporosis — doctor prescribed
- Hormone replacement therapy (HRT): For postmenopausal women — doctor decision (risk-benefit discussion)
- Denosumab, teriparatide, romosozumab: Newer agents for high fracture risk — specialist prescribed
- Treat secondary causes: Correct Vitamin D deficiency, treat hyperthyroidism, etc.
- Hip fracture: Surgical repair (if fit for surgery) followed by physiotherapy and rehabilitation

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Fracture from minor fall or minimal trauma (fragility fracture)
- Back pain with loss of height or stooped posture
- Bone pain and muscle weakness
- Post-menopause women (bone density assessment)
- Long-term corticosteroid use
- Child with bowing of legs, delayed walking, or suspected rickets
- Tingling, numbness, or muscle spasms (calcium deficiency)
- Low Vitamin D level on blood test

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Ragi (finger millet) is the most calcium-rich staple grain in India — promoting its consumption is a key public health strategy
- Vitamin D deficiency is a national epidemic; supplementation is underutilised
- ICMR-NIN recommends daily Vitamin D supplementation for elderly and infants in India
- Few government hospitals have DEXA scanners outside major cities — private availability is growing
- Hip fracture care: Major challenge in rural India due to surgical infrastructure; many elderly with hip fractures do not receive surgery
- Ayushman Bharat PMJAY: Covers hip fracture surgery and orthopaedic procedures for eligible beneficiaries

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Do not self-supplement with high-dose calcium or Vitamin D without medical supervision — excess calcium can increase cardiovascular risk; excess Vitamin D causes toxicity. Osteoporosis medication must be taken exactly as prescribed. Always consult a qualified doctor or orthopaedic/endocrine specialist for diagnosis and management of bone health conditions.
