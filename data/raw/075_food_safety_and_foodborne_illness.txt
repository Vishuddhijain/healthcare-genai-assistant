--- OVERVIEW ---
Food safety refers to practices that prevent foodborne illness — diseases caused by eating contaminated food. Foodborne illnesses affect an estimated 600 million people globally every year, causing 420,000 deaths. In India, foodborne illness is a major public health problem, with contaminated food and water being leading causes of diarrhoea, typhoid, hepatitis A, and other conditions. Most foodborne illness is preventable through safe food handling.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- 600 million people (1 in 10) become ill from eating contaminated food each year globally.
- 420,000 people die from foodborne diseases annually.
- Children under 5 carry 40% of the global foodborne disease burden.
- In India, foodborne illness peaks during summer and monsoon seasons.
- Common food safety violations: street food from unhygienic sources, unclean water for cooking, improper food storage.

--- COMMON FOODBORNE ILLNESSES IN INDIA ---
| Disease | Common Source | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| Diarrhoea (bacterial) | Contaminated water, undercooked food | Loose stools, cramps |
| Typhoid | Contaminated water/food | Persistent fever, abdominal pain |
| Hepatitis A | Contaminated water/raw food | Jaundice, nausea, dark urine |
| Cholera | Contaminated water | Profuse watery diarrhoea, dehydration |
| Salmonellosis | Raw eggs, undercooked poultry | Diarrhoea, fever, vomiting |
| Food poisoning (Staphylococcus) | Cooked food left at room temperature | Vomiting, cramps (rapid onset) |
| Aflatoxin poisoning | Stored grains, peanuts with mould | Liver damage (long-term) |

--- WHO'S 5 KEYS TO SAFER FOOD ---
1. KEEP CLEAN
   - Wash hands before handling food, after using toilet, after handling raw meat/eggs
   - Wash and sanitise all surfaces and equipment used for food preparation
   - Protect kitchen areas from insects, pests, and animals

2. SEPARATE RAW AND COOKED
   - Separate raw meat, poultry, and seafood from other foods
   - Use separate utensils and cutting boards for raw and cooked foods
   - Store raw meat in sealed containers at the bottom of the fridge

3. COOK THOROUGHLY
   - Cook food to at least 70°C (internal temperature) — especially meat, eggs, poultry
   - Reheat cooked food to 70°C
   - Never eat undercooked eggs, chicken, or pork
   - Bring soups and gravies to boil when reheating

4. KEEP FOOD AT SAFE TEMPERATURES
   - Do not leave cooked food at room temperature for more than 2 hours
   - Refrigerate perishables promptly
   - Do not thaw frozen food at room temperature — thaw in refrigerator
   - Do not store hot food in refrigerator — cool to room temperature first, then refrigerate

5. USE SAFE WATER AND RAW MATERIALS
   - Use safe, treated water for cooking and washing
   - Wash fruits and vegetables under running water before eating
   - Choose fresh, unprocessed foods
   - Do not use food beyond its expiry date
   - Discard food with visible mould (do not just cut off mouldy portion — toxins spread)

--- STREET FOOD SAFETY IN INDIA ---
Street food is a major part of Indian food culture. To minimise risk:
- Choose vendors where food is cooked fresh and hot in front of you
- Avoid pre-cooked food sitting at room temperature for long periods
- Avoid cut fruits, chutneys, or pani puri water from unknown/unclean sources
- During monsoon and summer, extra caution is warranted
- Choose vendors who maintain visible cleanliness (clean hands, covered food)
- Avoid raw or partially cooked non-vegetarian items from street vendors

--- SPECIAL FOOD SAFETY CONCERNS ---
Milk and dairy:
- Always consume pasteurised milk
- Boil raw/unpackaged milk before drinking
- Fermented dairy (curd, buttermilk) is generally safer than raw milk

Grains and pulses:
- Store in dry, airtight containers — moisture promotes mould growth
- Discard grain that smells musty or shows mould — do not consume (aflatoxin risk)
- Wash rice and dal before cooking

Oils and fats:
- Do not reuse cooking oil multiple times — repeated heating produces harmful compounds
- Discard oil that has darkened, smokes at low temperature, or smells rancid

Packaged foods:
- Check expiry date before purchasing or consuming
- Look for FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority of India) licence mark on packaged foods
- Avoid damaged or swollen cans and packages

--- FSSAI (FOOD SAFETY IN INDIA) ---
FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority of India) regulates food safety in India.
- Look for the FSSAI logo on packaged foods — ensures regulatory compliance.
- To report food safety violations: 1800-11-2100 (FSSAI toll-free helpline)
- Eat Right India initiative: Promotes safe and healthy food choices

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
For suspected foodborne illness:
- Diarrhoea with blood
- High fever (above 38.5°C)
- Signs of dehydration (no urination, extreme weakness)
- Symptoms lasting more than 2 days
- Multiple people in a household falling ill simultaneously (possible common source — report to local health authority)
- Vomiting that prevents keeping fluids down

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is food kept in the refrigerator always safe?
A: No. Refrigeration slows bacterial growth but doesn't stop it. Cooked food should be consumed within 2–3 days. Raw meat within 1–2 days. Always reheat refrigerated cooked food thoroughly.

Q: Is it safe to eat food that has been out for 4 hours?
A: No. The danger zone for bacterial growth is 5–60°C. Food at room temperature for more than 2 hours should be discarded, especially in India's warm climate.

Q: Are preservatives in packaged food harmful?
A: FSSAI-approved preservatives in foods within regulated limits are generally considered safe. However, excessive consumption of highly processed foods with multiple additives is not recommended for overall health.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for food safety awareness. Suspected foodborne illness outbreaks (multiple people affected) should be reported to local health authorities or FSSAI (1800-11-2100). For severe symptoms, seek medical care immediately.
