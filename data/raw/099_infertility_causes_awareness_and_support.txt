TOPIC: Infertility — Causes, Awareness, and Support
CATEGORY: Reproductive Health / Non-Communicable
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/infertility
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Infertility is defined as the failure to achieve a clinical pregnancy after 12 months or more of regular, unprotected sexual intercourse. It affects both men and women equally. In India, infertility is a significant concern affecting millions of couples, and it carries immense social stigma — especially for women. Infertility has medical, social, and psychological dimensions. Many causes are treatable.

--- TYPES ---
Primary infertility: Person has never achieved a pregnancy.
Secondary infertility: Person has previously achieved pregnancy but is unable to do so again.
Male factor infertility: Due to sperm abnormalities.
Female factor infertility: Due to ovulatory, uterine, tubal, or cervical factors.
Unexplained infertility: No identifiable cause found in either partner after investigation (~10–15%).
Combined infertility: Both partners have contributing factors.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Infertility affects approximately 17.5% of adults globally (1 in 6 couples).
- In India, infertility affects approximately 10–15% of couples of reproductive age.
- Male factor infertility is equally common as female factor — responsible for 40–50% of cases.
- 50% of infertility cases in India remain undiagnosed due to social stigma and lack of access to care.
- Infertility is NOT a woman's problem alone — men must also be evaluated.
- Many causes of infertility are preventable (STI prevention, healthy weight, quit smoking).
- ART (Assisted Reproductive Technology) — including IVF — is available in India.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Infertility itself has no symptoms (the inability to conceive IS the symptom). However, underlying causes may have signs:

Female signs (suggesting possible infertility causes):
- Irregular or absent menstrual periods (possible PCOS or hypothalamic issues)
- Very painful periods (possible endometriosis)
- Recurrent miscarriages
- Previous pelvic infection or STI history
- Abnormal pelvic pain

Male signs (suggesting possible infertility causes):
- Varicocele (swelling in scrotum)
- History of undescended testes
- History of mumps in adulthood
- Previous STI history
- Erectile or ejaculatory dysfunction

--- CAUSES ---
Female causes:
- Ovulatory disorders: PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) — most common; hypothalamic dysfunction; premature ovarian failure.
- Tubal factors: Blocked or damaged fallopian tubes — due to previous STI (chlamydia, gonorrhoea), PID (pelvic inflammatory disease), or previous surgery.
- Uterine factors: Fibroids, polyps, congenital abnormalities.
- Endometriosis: Tissue from the uterus grows outside — causes pain and infertility.
- Age: Female fertility declines significantly after age 35.
- Cervical factors: Mucus problems, cervical surgery.

Male causes:
- Sperm disorders: Low sperm count (oligospermia), poor sperm motility (asthenospermia), abnormal sperm shape (teratospermia), no sperm (azoospermia).
- Varicocele: Swelling of veins in scrotum — most common reversible male infertility cause.
- Hormonal disorders: Low testosterone, pituitary problems.
- Obstruction: Blocked vas deferens — congenital or from prior infection.
- Sexual dysfunction: Erectile dysfunction, ejaculatory issues.

Shared/Environmental causes:
- Untreated STIs — especially chlamydia and gonorrhoea causing tubal/epididymal damage.
- Obesity (both men and women).
- Smoking — damages sperm and eggs.
- Excessive alcohol.
- Stress.
- Environmental toxins (pesticides, heavy metals).

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- STI history (especially chlamydia and gonorrhoea)
- Age >35 (women); >40 (men)
- Obesity or underweight
- Tobacco use
- Excessive alcohol
- Endometriosis
- Previous pelvic surgery
- Chemotherapy/radiation history
- Uncontrolled diabetes or thyroid disease
- History of undescended testis (men)

--- PREVENTION ---
- Prevent STIs: Use condoms; get tested and treated promptly for any STI.
- Maintain healthy weight (BMI 18.5–24.9) — both obesity and underweight affect fertility.
- Quit smoking — smoking reduces sperm count, egg quality, and ART success.
- Limit alcohol.
- Manage chronic conditions (diabetes, thyroid, PCOS).
- Avoid occupational exposure to toxins (pesticides, heavy metals, radiation).
- Do not delay childbearing beyond late 30s if possible — age is the single greatest female fertility factor.

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- STI prevention (condom use, timely treatment) is the most impactful measure to prevent infertility.
- Workplace protection from reproductive toxins.

--- VACCINATION ---
- HPV vaccination prevents HPV-related conditions that can affect fertility.
- Mumps vaccination (MMR) — mumps orchitis in men causes infertility.
- All routine vaccinations protect overall reproductive health.

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
For women:
- Hormone tests: FSH, LH, AMH (ovarian reserve), prolactin, thyroid.
- Pelvic ultrasound: PCOS, fibroids, ovarian cysts.
- Hysterosalpingography (HSG): X-ray to check if fallopian tubes are open.
- Laparoscopy: For endometriosis diagnosis.

For men:
- Semen analysis: Sperm count, motility, morphology (basic and most important test).
- Hormone tests: FSH, LH, testosterone.
- Scrotal ultrasound: For varicocele.

Infertility evaluation should assess BOTH partners simultaneously.

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- PCOS: Lifestyle change + ovulation-induction medications (doctor-prescribed).
- Tubal blockage: Laparoscopic surgery or IVF.
- Male factor: Varicocelectomy (surgery for varicocele); sperm retrieval for azoospermia; ICSI (intracytoplasmic sperm injection).
- IUI (Intrauterine Insemination): Affordable first-line ART.
- IVF (In Vitro Fertilisation): For complex cases; increasingly available in India.
- ART Regulation Act 2021: Regulates ART clinics in India — check for ICMR registration.
- Cost: IVF in government medical colleges is subsidised; private clinics charge ₹1–2 lakh per cycle.

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
- Pelvic pain with fever in women (possible PID — treats promptly to prevent tubal damage).
- Sudden testicular swelling/pain in men (possible epididymitis/orchitis — treat immediately).
- Recurrent miscarriages (>2) — should trigger investigation.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Couples under 35: After 12 months of unprotected intercourse without conception.
- Couples 35–40: After 6 months.
- Women over 40: Immediately if trying to conceive.
- Any woman with irregular periods, severe period pain, or known PCOS — earlier evaluation.
- Any man with previous STI, scrotal abnormality, or known hormonal issues.

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Infertility stigma in India disproportionately burdens women — socially, emotionally, and legally. Women are often blamed even when the cause is male factor.
- The ART (Regulation) Act 2021 regulates fertility clinics and protects patients' rights.
- Surrogacy (Regulation) Act 2021: Allows altruistic surrogacy by close relatives.
- Government medical college fertility clinics offer subsidised evaluation and treatment.
- ICMR guidelines on ART: icmr.gov.in

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: We have been trying for 8 months without success. Should we be worried?
A: Not yet — 8 months is within the normal range. Consult a doctor after 12 months (or 6 months if over 35). In the meantime, both partners should get a basic check-up.

Q: Is infertility always permanent?
A: No. Many causes are treatable. Even unexplained infertility often resolves. ART has helped millions achieve pregnancy.

Q: My husband refuses to get tested. What should I do?
A: Male factor is responsible in 40–50% of cases — it is essential both partners are tested. Share this information with your husband. Many men are unaware that infertility is equally a male issue. A doctor can counsel couples together.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for awareness. Infertility evaluation and treatment require a qualified gynaecologist and/or urologist. Avoid unproven treatments and unregulated fertility clinics. Check ICMR registration before choosing any ART clinic.
