--- OVERVIEW ---
Chikungunya is a viral disease transmitted to humans by Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus mosquitoes — the same vectors as dengue. It is caused by the chikungunya virus (CHIKV), an alphavirus. The name "chikungunya" derives from a Kimakonde language word meaning "to become contorted," describing the stooped posture of affected patients due to severe joint pain. India experienced major outbreaks in 2006–2007 and continues to report cases annually, particularly during monsoon season.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Acute phase (onset 2–12 days after bite):
- Sudden high fever (often above 39°C)
- Severe, debilitating joint pain (polyarthralgia) — especially in wrists, ankles, knees, fingers
- Joint swelling
- Muscle pain (myalgia)
- Headache
- Nausea and fatigue
- Skin rash (maculopapular, appears 2–5 days after fever)

Chronic phase:
- Joint pain and stiffness may persist for weeks to months, or in some cases, years
- Chronic arthritis-like symptoms — more common in older adults

--- DIFFERENTIATION FROM DENGUE ---
| Feature             | Chikungunya               | Dengue                        |
|---------------------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Joint pain          | Severe and prolonged      | Mild to moderate              |
| Platelet drop       | Mild/uncommon             | Common (key indicator)        |
| Haemorrhage risk    | Rare                      | Yes (severe dengue)           |
| Rash                | Common                    | Less common                   |
| Shock/organ failure | Very rare                 | Possible in severe dengue     |
| Duration of fever   | 2–3 days typically        | 5–7 days typically            |

Both can co-infect simultaneously — clinical overlap is common and lab testing is essential.

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Living in/travelling to tropical and subtropical regions
- Monsoon season and post-monsoon (stagnant water breeding)
- Urban and peri-urban settings with poor sanitation
- Previous chikungunya infection does confer lifelong immunity (unlike dengue)

--- PREVENTION ---
Same mosquito control measures as dengue:
- Eliminate stagnant water sources around the home
- Use insect repellents and wear protective clothing
- Use mosquito nets and screens
- Community-level fogging and larviciding during outbreaks

No licensed vaccine available as of 2024 (trials ongoing).

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- RT-PCR: Definitive, useful in first 5 days
- Chikungunya IgM/IgG antibody ELISA: Useful from day 5 onwards
- Clinical diagnosis often combined with CBC (to rule out dengue)

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
No specific antiviral drug available. Management is supportive:
- Rest and adequate fluid intake
- Paracetamol for fever and pain
- NSAIDs (under doctor's supervision) may be considered after dengue is ruled out
- Physiotherapy may help with prolonged joint stiffness
- Avoid self-medication

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Sudden high fever with severe joint pain
- Rash combined with fever
- Fever during a known outbreak in your area
- Persistent joint pain lasting more than 2 weeks after fever resolution
- Symptoms in elderly, pregnant women, or newborns (higher complication risk)

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Chikungunya is notifiable under India's Integrated Disease Surveillance Programme (IDSP)
- Major states affected: Maharashtra, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Kerala, Delhi, Tamil Nadu, Gujarat
- NVBDCP (nvbdcp.gov.in) provides surveillance and response guidance
- Free testing available at government district hospitals during outbreaks
- Report suspected outbreaks to NVBDCP or call the National Health Helpline: 104. For vector control and fogging requests, contact your local municipal corporation.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness purposes only. Do not self-diagnose or self-medicate. Always consult a qualified healthcare provider for diagnosis and treatment. Chikungunya symptoms closely resemble dengue — lab testing is necessary to distinguish them.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is chikungunya the same as dengue?
A: No, but they share the same mosquito vector (Aedes aegypti) and similar early symptoms. Key difference: chikungunya causes severe, prolonged joint pain (arthralgia) â often debilitating â which is much milder or absent in dengue. Blood tests differentiate them.

Q: Can chikungunya joint pain last for months?
A: Yes. Chikungunya arthritis can persist for months to years in some patients, especially older adults. This is the hallmark of chikungunya. Anti-inflammatory medication, physiotherapy, and rest help manage it. Consult a doctor for persistent joint pain.

Q: Is there a vaccine or specific treatment for chikungunya?
A: A chikungunya vaccine (IXCHIQ) was approved in 2023 in the US but is not yet widely available in India. Treatment is supportive: rest, fluids, paracetamol for fever and pain. Do NOT take aspirin or ibuprofen (can increase bleeding risk if dengue is also possible).

Q: How do I protect myself from chikungunya during monsoon?
A: Eliminate stagnant water around your home (coolers, pots, tyres), use mosquito repellent during daytime hours, wear long sleeves, and use bed nets. Aedes mosquitoes breed in clean stagnant water and bite primarily during daylight hours.
