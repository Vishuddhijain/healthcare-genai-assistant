--- OVERVIEW ---
Mental disorders are characterised by clinically significant disturbance in an individual's cognition, emotional regulation, or behaviour, reflecting dysfunction in psychological, biological, or developmental processes. WHO estimates that 1 in 8 people globally live with a mental disorder, with anxiety and depressive disorders being most common. India's National Mental Health Survey (NIMHANS) found approximately 10.6% of adults suffer from some mental disorder, with a treatment gap of 70–92% depending on disorder type — meaning most people with mental illness in India do not receive care. This overview complements the dedicated mental health, anxiety/autism, bipolar/depression, dementia, schizophrenia, and PTSD topics.

--- TYPES (Major Categories) ---
1. Anxiety disorders: Generalised anxiety, panic disorder, phobias, social anxiety (see Anxiety topic)
2. Depressive disorders: Major depressive disorder, dysthymia (see Bipolar/Depression topic)
3. Bipolar disorder: Alternating mania and depression (see Bipolar/Depression topic)
4. Schizophrenia and psychotic disorders: Hallucinations, delusions, disorganised thinking (see Schizophrenia topic)
5. Neurodevelopmental disorders: Autism spectrum disorder, ADHD, intellectual disability (see Anxiety/Autism topic)
6. Eating disorders: Anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder
7. Conduct/behavioural disorders: Especially in children and adolescents
8. Substance use disorders: Often co-occur with other mental disorders (see Alcohol Use topic)
9. Dementia: Neurocognitive disorders (see Dementia topic)
10. Stress-related disorders: PTSD, adjustment disorders (see PTSD topic)

--- SYMPTOMS (General Warning Signs Across Disorders) ---
- Persistent sadness, anxiety, or irritability
- Withdrawal from social activities and relationships
- Significant changes in sleep or appetite
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
- Loss of interest in previously enjoyed activities
- Excessive fears or worries
- Unusual thoughts, beliefs, or perceptions (hallucinations, delusions)
- Significant changes in energy levels or behaviour
- Difficulty functioning at work, school, or in relationships

--- WARNING SIGNS (Urgent) ---
- Thoughts of self-harm or suicide — see Suicide topic for crisis resources
- Inability to care for oneself (not eating, not maintaining hygiene)
- Psychotic symptoms (hearing voices, paranoid beliefs) causing distress or risk
- Severe agitation or aggression
- Sudden, severe changes in behaviour or personality

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Genetic and family history of mental illness
- Adverse childhood experiences (abuse, neglect, trauma)
- Chronic stress, poverty, unemployment
- Social isolation
- Substance use
- Chronic physical illness
- Major life transitions or losses
- Discrimination and stigma (compounds barriers to care)

--- PREVENTION / PROMOTION ---
- Mental health literacy and reducing stigma
- Strengthening social connections and support systems
- Stress management techniques (mindfulness, exercise, adequate sleep)
- Early identification and intervention programmes (especially in schools and workplaces)
- Addressing social determinants: Poverty reduction, employment programmes, safe housing
- Limiting access to means of self-harm in at-risk individuals

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- Workplace mental health policies (reducing burnout, harassment)
- School-based mental health programmes
- Community-based rehabilitation reduces institutionalisation and supports recovery

--- VACCINATION ---
- Not applicable

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- Clinical assessment by mental health professional (psychiatrist, clinical psychologist)
- Standardised screening tools (e.g. PHQ-9 for depression, GAD-7 for anxiety) — screening only, not diagnostic alone
- Comprehensive history including psychosocial context
- Rule out medical causes (thyroid disorders, vitamin deficiencies, substance effects) that can mimic psychiatric symptoms

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- Psychotherapy: Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT), counselling, and other evidence-based therapies
- Medication: Antidepressants, anti-anxiety medications, antipsychotics, mood stabilisers — doctor/psychiatrist prescribed
- Combination treatment: Often most effective for moderate-severe disorders
- Psychosocial support: Peer support groups, family psychoeducation, vocational rehabilitation
- Community mental health services: District Mental Health Programme (DMHP) provides services at district level in India

--- WHEN TO SEEK HELP ---
- Symptoms persisting for more than 2 weeks affecting daily functioning
- Thoughts of self-harm or suicide — seek immediate help (see Suicide topic for helplines)
- Significant changes in behaviour noticed by family/friends
- Difficulty managing work, relationships, or self-care due to emotional/psychological symptoms

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- National Mental Health Programme (NMHP) and District Mental Health Programme (DMHP): Provide mental health services at district hospitals and below
- Mental Healthcare Act 2017: Decriminalises suicide attempts, protects rights of persons with mental illness, mandates insurance coverage for mental health on par with physical health
- Tele-MANAS (Tele Mental Health Assistance and Networking Across States): 24x7 toll-free helpline — 14416 or 1800-891-4416 — provides counselling and referral across India in multiple languages
- NIMHANS (National Institute of Mental Health and Neurosciences), Bengaluru: Apex institute for mental health research, training, and tertiary care
- Significant treatment gap (70–92%) due to stigma, shortage of mental health professionals, and limited awareness

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Mental disorders are treatable medical conditions, not personal weaknesses. If you or someone you know is experiencing symptoms, reaching out to Tele-MANAS (14416) or a qualified mental health professional is an important first step. If there are thoughts of self-harm or suicide, please seek immediate help — this is a sensitive topic and support is available.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: How common are mental health conditions in India?
A: Approximately 10.6% of adults in India have a mental health condition (National Mental Health Survey 2016). Depression and anxiety are most common. Despite this, only 20-30% receive any treatment — the treatment gap is enormous due to stigma, lack of awareness, and shortage of mental health professionals.

Q: Are mental health conditions treatable?
A: Yes. Most mental health conditions are treatable. Depression responds well to therapy and medication. Schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and anxiety can be managed effectively. Recovery is possible. The key is early diagnosis and consistent treatment — not giving up.

Q: Are mental health services free in India?
A: Free psychiatric outpatient services are available at all government district hospitals and medical colleges. Tele MANAS (14416) provides free 24/7 telephone mental health support. The National Mental Health Programme funds district-level mental health services across India.

Q: What is the difference between a psychiatrist, psychologist, and counsellor?
A: Psychiatrist: Medical doctor who can prescribe medication and provide therapy for mental health conditions. Psychologist: Non-medical professional with psychology degree providing therapy and assessment; cannot prescribe medicines in India. Counsellor: Provides supportive talk therapy for milder issues. All three have important roles.
