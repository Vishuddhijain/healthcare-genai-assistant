--- OVERVIEW ---
Hepatitis means inflammation of the liver. Viral hepatitis — caused by hepatitis viruses A, B, C, D, and E — is a major global health concern. Hepatitis B and C are the most serious, causing chronic liver disease, cirrhosis, and liver cancer. Hepatitis B is vaccine-preventable. Hepatitis C is now curable. Together, they cause 1.1 million deaths per year globally.

--- HEPATITIS B ---

Key facts:
- Hepatitis B (HBV) affects approximately 296 million people globally.
- India has approximately 40 million people with chronic Hepatitis B.
- Hepatitis B causes liver cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma (liver cancer).
- Hepatitis B vaccine is safe, effective, and FREE under India's Universal Immunisation Programme.
- Effective treatment can suppress the virus and prevent liver damage.

How it spreads:
- Sexual contact with an infected person (unprotected)
- Sharing needles, syringes, or other drug-injection equipment
- Mother to child during childbirth (perinatal transmission) — the most common route in Asia
- Unsafe medical or dental procedures with contaminated instruments
- Blood transfusion (now rare — blood is screened in India)
- Sharing razors or toothbrushes with infected person

Does NOT spread through:
- Hugging, kissing, sharing food/water, coughing/sneezing, mosquito bites

Symptoms:
- Most people with acute Hepatitis B have mild or no symptoms.
- Acute symptoms (if present): fatigue, nausea, loss of appetite, yellowing of eyes/skin (jaundice), dark urine, abdominal pain.
- Chronic Hepatitis B: Often silent for years or decades. Symptoms appear only when liver is severely damaged.
- Chronic infection can lead to: cirrhosis (liver scarring), liver failure, liver cancer.

Who is at risk for chronic infection:
- Infants infected at birth: 90% develop chronic infection.
- Children infected 1–5 years: 25–50% develop chronic infection.
- Adults infected: Only 5–10% develop chronic infection.
→ This is why vaccinating infants at birth is critical.

Prevention:
- Hepatitis B vaccine: 3-dose series. FREE at birth under India's UIP (0-6-14 weeks schedule).
- Adults not vaccinated: 3-dose vaccine available at government hospitals.
- Use condoms during sexual contact.
- Never share needles, syringes, razors, or toothbrushes.
- Ensure medical/dental instruments are properly sterilised.
- Pregnant women should be tested — if positive, baby receives HBIG (Hepatitis B Immunoglobulin) at birth.

Diagnosis and treatment:
- HBsAg blood test (Hepatitis B surface antigen) detects infection.
- HBV DNA test measures viral load.
- Liver function tests and ultrasound assess liver health.
- Treatment: Antiviral medications (tenofovir, entecavir) — suppress virus, reduce liver damage.
- Treatment available at government hospitals; free for eligible patients under NVHCP.

--- HEPATITIS C ---

Key facts:
- Hepatitis C (HCV) affects approximately 50 million people globally.
- India has approximately 6–12 million people with HCV infection.
- 70–80% of people with acute HCV develop chronic infection.
- Hepatitis C is now CURABLE with direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) in 8–12 weeks (95%+ cure rate).
- There is currently no vaccine for Hepatitis C.

How it spreads:
- Sharing needles and equipment for injecting drugs (most common route globally)
- Unsafe medical/dental/tattoo/piercing procedures with contaminated instruments
- Blood transfusions before blood screening was implemented
- Mother to child (less common than HBV)
- Sexual contact (less common than HBV)

Symptoms:
- 80% of people with acute Hepatitis C have NO symptoms.
- Chronic HCV infection is often called the "silent epidemic" — most people are unaware.
- When symptoms occur: fatigue, nausea, jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes).
- Long-term effects: cirrhosis, liver failure, liver cancer.

Diagnosis and treatment:
- Anti-HCV antibody test: Initial screening test.
- HCV RNA PCR test: Confirms active infection.
- Cure: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs like sofosbuvir/daclatasvir) — 8–12 week course, 95%+ cure rate.
- Free treatment available in India: National Viral Hepatitis Control Programme (NVHCP) provides free DAA treatment at government hospitals.
- Find nearest NVHCP centre: mohfw.gov.in

--- NATIONAL VIRAL HEPATITIS CONTROL PROGRAMME (NVHCP) ---
India's NVHCP aims to eliminate viral hepatitis by 2030 (Sustainable Development Goal).
- Free testing for Hepatitis B and C at government hospitals.
- Free treatment for Hepatitis C with DAA drugs (cure rate >95%).
- Free Hepatitis B treatment for eligible patients.
- Call 1800-11-0123 (health helpline) for NVHCP information.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is Hepatitis B curable?
A: Not currently curable, but manageable. Antiviral treatment keeps the virus suppressed and prevents liver damage. With treatment, people with Hepatitis B can live normal lives.

Q: Is Hepatitis C curable?
A: Yes! DAA drugs cure Hepatitis C in 8–12 weeks with over 95% success rate. Free treatment is available at government hospitals in India under NVHCP.

Q: If I was vaccinated for Hepatitis B, do I need a booster?
A: The standard 3-dose course given in childhood provides long-lasting (likely lifelong) protection. Boosters are not routinely recommended for immunocompetent individuals.

Q: Can I donate blood if I have Hepatitis B or C?
A: No. You are permanently deferred from blood donation if positive for Hepatitis B or C.

Q: Can Hepatitis B spread from eating at the same table or sharing utensils?
A: No. Hepatitis B does not spread through food, water, shared utensils, or casual contact. It spreads through blood and bodily fluids only.
