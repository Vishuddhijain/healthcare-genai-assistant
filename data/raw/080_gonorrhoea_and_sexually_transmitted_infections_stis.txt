--- OVERVIEW ---
Gonorrhoea is a common sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Neisseria gonorrhoeae. It infects the genitals, rectum, and throat. If untreated, it causes serious complications including infertility. Drug-resistant gonorrhoea (multi-drug resistant) is an emerging global health crisis. STIs in general are extremely common, largely underreported in India, and largely preventable.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- 82 million new gonorrhoea infections occur globally each year.
- India is among countries with significant gonorrhoea burden, though data is limited.
- Gonorrhoea is the second most common bacterial STI globally (after chlamydia).
- Drug-resistant gonorrhoea (including extensively drug-resistant strains) is a growing crisis — some strains are untreatable with current antibiotics.
- Untreated gonorrhoea causes infertility, pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), and increased HIV transmission risk.
- Many gonorrhoea infections — especially in women — have no symptoms.
- Gonorrhoea can be passed from mother to newborn during delivery, causing neonatal eye infection (ophthalmia neonatorum) and blindness.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
In men:
- Burning sensation during urination
- White, yellow, or green penile discharge
- Painful or swollen testicles (epididymitis)
- Some men have no symptoms

In women (often absent or mild):
- Increased vaginal discharge (yellowish)
- Burning during urination
- Pelvic pain
- Bleeding between periods
- Many women have NO symptoms — infection discovered only on testing

Rectal infection (both sexes):
- Anal itching, discharge, or soreness
- Often no symptoms

Throat infection:
- Sore throat, but usually no symptoms

Neonatal gonorrhoea (newborn from infected mother):
- Eye redness and discharge within 2–5 days of birth
- Can cause permanent blindness if untreated
- Routine eye drops at birth prevent this

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Unprotected sexual intercourse (vaginal, anal, oral)
- Multiple sexual partners
- Previous STI
- Young age (15–24 years highest rates)
- Men who have sex with men (MSM)
- Partner with untreated gonorrhoea

--- PREVENTION ---
- Use condoms consistently and correctly — every time, for every sexual encounter.
- Regular STI testing for sexually active individuals, especially with new or multiple partners.
- Mutual monogamy with a tested, uninfected partner reduces risk.
- Avoid douching (women) — disrupts natural protective flora.
- Neonatal eye prophylaxis at birth (erythromycin or tetracycline eye drops) — routine in India.

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- Partner notification and treatment: When diagnosed with gonorrhoea, all recent sexual partners must be informed and tested.
- Contact tracing by health authorities reduces community spread.

--- VACCINATION ---
- No vaccine available for gonorrhoea currently.
- HPV and Hepatitis B vaccines protect against other STIs.

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- NAAT (Nucleic Acid Amplification Test) — most sensitive test; available at ICTC centres.
- Gram stain and culture of discharge — available at government hospitals.
- All people with an STI should also be tested for HIV and syphilis.
- Free STI testing at ICTCs (Integrated Counselling and Testing Centres) — all government hospitals.
- Testing is confidential.

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- Gonorrhoea is treated with dual antibiotic therapy (ceftriaxone + azithromycin) — doctor-prescribed.
- Drug-resistant strains may require different regimens — culture and sensitivity testing essential.
- Complete the full treatment course.
- Sexual partners must also be treated simultaneously.
- Retest 7 days after treatment to confirm cure.
- NEVER self-medicate for gonorrhoea — contributes to drug resistance.

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
- Penile discharge or burning urination in men — see a doctor promptly.
- Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) in women: severe lower abdominal pain, fever, discharge — emergency.
- Newborn with eye redness/discharge — emergency eye care needed.
- Any STI symptoms — do not delay, do not ignore.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Any discharge from genitals, rectum, or unusual sores
- Burning urination without urinary tract infection explanation
- Lower abdominal pain in women with sexual activity history
- After unprotected sex with unknown partner — get tested
- If a sexual partner has been diagnosed with gonorrhoea

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Stigma around STIs is a major barrier to testing and treatment in India.
- Free STI diagnosis and treatment at all ICTCs and STI/RTI clinics at government hospitals under NACP (National AIDS Control Programme).
- NACO STI helpline: 1097 (confidential)
- Condoms available free from ASHA workers and government hospitals.
- Multi-drug resistant gonorrhoea cases are being reported in India — underscoring the need to avoid self-medication.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Can gonorrhoea go away on its own without treatment?
A: No. Gonorrhoea does not resolve on its own. Without treatment, it causes serious complications (infertility, PID) and continues to spread to others.

Q: If I have no symptoms, do I still need to get tested?
A: Yes. Many people — especially women — have no symptoms. If you have had unprotected sex, testing is important. Untreated asymptomatic gonorrhoea still causes complications and spreads to partners.

Q: Is gonorrhoea the same as a urinary tract infection?
A: No. Both cause burning urination but have different causes and treatments. Gonorrhoea is a bacterial STI. UTIs are usually caused by gut bacteria (E. coli) and are not sexually transmitted. A doctor's test differentiates them.

Q: Will my doctor tell anyone I have gonorrhoea?
A: Gonorrhoea diagnosis is confidential. Healthcare providers may advise you to notify partners, but they will not disclose your identity to others.
