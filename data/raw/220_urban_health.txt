--- OVERVIEW ---
The rising noncommunicable disease burden, the persistent threat of infectious disease outbreaks and an increased risk of violence and injuries are key public health concerns in urban areas. This triple threat includes:

Injuries (including road traffic injury) and interpersonal violence particularly affect children, young adults, older people and the most marginalized groups as a result of poor working and living conditions and a lack of safe transport and infrastructure.

Infectious diseases like COVID-19, tuberculosis, dengue and diarrhoea thrive in poor and overcrowded environments and are closely related to unhealthy housing and poor sanitation and waste management. Poor urban waste management fuels transmission of diseases such as the Zika and Ebola viruses.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Over 55% of the world’s population live in urban areas and this is set to rise to 68% by 2050.
- Almost 40% of urban dwellers have no access to safely managed sanitation services and many lack access to adequate drinking water.
- An estimated 91% of people in urban areas breathe polluted air.
- Poorly designed urban transport systems create a range of threats including road traffic injuries, air and noise pollution and barriers to safe physical activity – all leading to higher levels of noncommunicable disease and injuries.
- Continued urbanization is expected to lead to cities becoming epicentres of disease transmission, including vector-borne diseases.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: What are the unique health challenges of living in Indian cities?
A: Urban Indians face: air pollution (major health risk), urban heat islands, mental health stress, sedentary lifestyles, traffic accident risk, poor housing and sanitation in slums, food insecurity, and NCDs (diabetes, hypertension, heart disease). Rural-to-urban migrants face additional barriers to healthcare access.

Q: Is healthcare easily accessible in Indian cities?
A: Access varies dramatically. Upper and middle-income urban residents generally have good access to private healthcare. Urban poor — particularly slum residents — face barriers including distance, cost, lack of documentation, and discrimination. Government primary health centres and dispensaries exist but are often overcrowded.

Q: What is an Urban Health Centre (UHC) in India?
A: Urban Health Centres (UHCs) are government-run primary healthcare facilities for urban populations, particularly urban poor and slum residents. They provide free outpatient care, maternal and child health services, immunisation, and NCDs screening. Look for your nearest UHC through your local municipal corporation.

Q: How does urban planning affect health in Indian cities?
A: Cities designed for cars rather than people reduce physical activity, increase pollution, and create heat islands. Green spaces improve mental health and air quality. Walkable neighbourhoods increase physical activity. Safe water and sanitation in slums prevent disease. Urban planning decisions are public health decisions.
