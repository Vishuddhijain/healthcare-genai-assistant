TOPIC: Gambling Disorder (Problem Gambling)  
CATEGORY: Mental Health
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/gambling
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Gambling disorder is a behavioural addiction characterised by persistent, recurrent gambling behaviour that causes significant impairment or distress. It is classified as a mental health disorder in the ICD-11 (International Classification of Diseases). With the rise of online gambling and gaming apps in India, gambling disorder is an emerging but underrecognised public health concern.

--- TYPES ---
1. Gambling disorder (ICD-11): Persistent gambling despite negative consequences, impaired control, and priority given to gambling over other activities.
2. Problem gambling: Gambling causing harm but not meeting full disorder criteria.
3. At-risk gambling: Gambling behaviour with early signs of harm.
Gambling forms: lotteries, cards, sports betting, online gambling, casino games, stock/crypto speculation (in some contexts).

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Approximately 1–3% of people who gamble develop a gambling disorder.
- Gambling disorder is more common in men, young adults, and people with other mental health conditions.
- Online gambling has significantly increased accessibility and risk, especially among young people.
- In India, gambling (except certain state-regulated lotteries and horse racing) is largely illegal, but online gambling and betting apps are widely accessible.
- Gambling disorder frequently co-occurs with depression, anxiety, and substance use disorders.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Behavioural signs:
- Preoccupied with gambling (constantly thinking about it, planning next session)
- Needing to gamble with increasingly larger amounts to achieve the same excitement
- Repeated unsuccessful efforts to control, reduce, or stop gambling
- Restless or irritable when trying to reduce gambling
- Gambling to escape problems or relieve feelings of helplessness, guilt, or anxiety
- Lying to family and friends to conceal gambling
- Relying on others to provide money to relieve desperate financial situations caused by gambling
- Jeopardising significant relationships, job, or education because of gambling
- Chasing losses — returning the next day to try to win back what was lost

Physical/Psychological signs:
- Sleep disturbance and fatigue
- Anxiety and depression
- Thoughts of suicide (elevated risk in gambling disorder)

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Male sex and young age
- Family history of gambling or addictions
- Mental health conditions (depression, ADHD, anxiety)
- Substance use disorders
- Impulsivity as a personality trait
- Easy access to gambling platforms (especially online)
- Financial stress
- Peer influence and social gambling environments

--- PREVENTION ---
- Awareness of gambling risks — gambling is not a reliable way to make money
- Setting and sticking to strict spending limits if gambling recreationally
- Parents should monitor children's access to online gaming with monetary elements (loot boxes, in-game purchases)
- Schools should include gambling awareness in life skills education
- Financial literacy education — understanding odds (the house always wins in the long run)

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- India's legal framework on gambling is complex — varies by state. Online gambling regulation is evolving.
- Parents should use parental controls on devices to limit children's access to gambling apps.
- Banks and payment apps can help set voluntary spending limits.

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- Diagnosis is clinical — based on DSM-5 or ICD-11 criteria (described in symptoms above)
- At least 4–5 symptoms present within a 12-month period
- A mental health professional (psychiatrist or psychologist) makes the diagnosis
- Screening tools: Problem Gambling Severity Index (PGSI)

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
Highly treatable with proper support:
- Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT): Most evidence-based treatment — addresses thoughts and behaviours driving gambling
- Motivational interviewing: Helps build motivation to change
- Support groups: Gamblers Anonymous (12-step programme)
- Medications: Some evidence for naltrexone, antidepressants — prescribed by psychiatrist
- Financial counselling to address debt
- Treating co-occurring disorders (depression, substance use) simultaneously

India resources:
- Tele MANAS: 14416 (free mental health support)
- iCall (TISS): 9152987821
- NIMHANS, AIIMS: De-addiction and behavioural addiction services

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
- Family member secretly borrowing or stealing money for unexplained reasons
- Mounting unexplained debts
- Selling personal possessions
- Extreme secrecy about finances or phone/computer use
- Mood swings related to wins and losses
- Suicidal thoughts — gambling disorder has elevated suicide risk

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- If gambling is causing financial, relationship, or occupational problems
- If you or a family member cannot stop gambling despite wanting to
- If thoughts of self-harm are present
- For any co-occurring mental health or substance use issue

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Online betting and fantasy sports apps (Dream11, etc.) are widely used in India.
- Cricket betting is extremely common; large illegal gambling networks operate.
- Gambling disorder awareness and treatment services are limited in India but growing.
- The Public Gambling Act 1867 (central law) bans most gambling; states have their own regulations.
- Cryptocurrency and stock trading can exhibit gambling-like patterns in susceptible individuals.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is gambling disorder a sign of weakness or moral failure?
A: No. Gambling disorder is a recognised mental health condition involving brain changes in reward and impulse control systems — similar to substance addictions. It is not a moral failure. Treatment helps.

Q: My family member gambles but says they can stop anytime. Is that a problem?
A: Claiming control while continuing despite consequences is a classic feature of gambling disorder. Denial is common. Encourage them to speak to a mental health professional.

Q: Can a young person develop gambling disorder?
A: Yes. Young adults are among the highest-risk groups, especially with online gambling accessibility. Early intervention is important.
