CATEGORY: Maternal and Child Health

--- OVERVIEW ---
Maternal mortality refers to the death of a woman during pregnancy, childbirth, or within 42 days of termination of pregnancy from causes related to or aggravated by the pregnancy. WHO estimates approximately 287,000 maternal deaths occurred globally in 2020 — about 800 women dying every day from preventable causes. India has made significant progress: Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR) declined from 130 (2014–16) to 97 per 100,000 live births (2018–20, SRS data), but disparities remain across states. Most maternal deaths are preventable with access to quality antenatal, delivery, and postnatal care.

--- TYPES / CAUSES OF MATERNAL DEATH ---
Direct causes (obstetric):
1. Severe bleeding (postpartum haemorrhage — PPH): Leading cause; can cause death within hours if untreated
2. Infections (puerperal sepsis): Usually after delivery
3. Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy: Pre-eclampsia and eclampsia (seizures)
4. Complications from delivery: Obstructed labour, uterine rupture
5. Unsafe abortion: Complications from unsafe termination of pregnancy

Indirect causes:
- Pre-existing conditions worsened by pregnancy: Anaemia, cardiac disease, diabetes, malaria, TB, HIV

--- SYMPTOMS / WARNING SIGNS (during pregnancy and postpartum) ---
EMERGENCY — seek immediate care:
- Heavy vaginal bleeding (soaking through pad in less than an hour) — at any time during pregnancy or after delivery
- Severe headache with blurred vision, swelling of face/hands, or seizures (pre-eclampsia/eclampsia)
- High fever with foul-smelling vaginal discharge (infection)
- Severe abdominal pain
- Reduced or absent fetal movements
- Convulsions/seizures
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe vomiting unable to keep fluids down

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Lack of access to skilled birth attendants and emergency obstetric care
- Home deliveries without trained personnel
- Adolescent pregnancy (<18 years) — higher risk of complications
- Anaemia in pregnancy (worsens haemorrhage outcomes — major India-specific risk factor)
- Pre-existing hypertension, diabetes, cardiac disease
- Multiple pregnancies (twins/triplets)
- Previous caesarean section or uterine surgery
- Short birth intervals
- Rural residence with limited access to emergency transport/referral

--- PREVENTION ---
- Antenatal Care (ANC): At least 4 ANC visits (WHO recommends 8 contacts); early registration of pregnancy
- Institutional delivery: Delivery at a health facility with skilled birth attendant
- Birth preparedness and complication readiness: Plan for transport, blood availability, emergency contacts
- Treat anaemia during pregnancy (IFA supplementation)
- Monitor and manage blood pressure (pre-eclampsia screening)
- Postnatal care: Check-ups within 48 hours, 1 week, 6 weeks after delivery
- Family planning/birth spacing: Reduces risk associated with repeated closely-spaced pregnancies
- Skilled management of labour complications (partograph use, timely referral)

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- Strengthening referral transport networks (ambulance services connecting villages to FRUs — First Referral Units)
- Blood storage units at CHCs/district hospitals for emergency transfusion
- Functional 24x7 delivery points at PHCs

--- VACCINATION ---
- Tetanus toxoid (TT/Td): 2 doses during pregnancy prevents maternal and neonatal tetanus
- Influenza vaccine recommended during pregnancy (any trimester)
- COVID-19 vaccination recommended during pregnancy per current guidelines

--- DIAGNOSIS / MONITORING ---
- Regular ANC check-ups: Blood pressure, haemoglobin, urine protein, blood sugar, fetal growth monitoring
- Ultrasound: Fetal growth, placental position, anomaly screening
- High-risk pregnancy identification and referral to higher centres

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- PPH: Active management of third stage of labour (AMTSL) with oxytocin; uterotonics; in severe cases — uterine balloon tamponade, surgery, blood transfusion — hospital setting
- Pre-eclampsia/eclampsia: Magnesium sulphate (for seizure prevention/control), blood pressure management, timely delivery — hospital setting
- Infections: Antibiotics — doctor prescribed
- All emergency obstetric complications require facility-based care — home management is dangerous

--- WHEN TO SEEK MEDICAL HELP ---
- Any of the warning signs listed above — IMMEDIATELY, do not wait
- Call 102/108 (ambulance services for pregnant women in many states)
- Plan delivery at a health facility, not at home, especially for high-risk pregnancies

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Janani Suraksha Yojana (JSY): Cash assistance for institutional delivery, especially for BPL/SC/ST women
- Janani Shishu Suraksha Karyakram (JSSK): Free delivery (including C-section), free drugs, diagnostics, blood, diet, and transport for pregnant women and sick newborns at government facilities
- Pradhan Mantri Surakshit Matritva Abhiyan (PMSMA): Free, fixed-day antenatal check-ups (including high-risk pregnancy identification) on 9th of every month at government facilities
- LaQshya programme: Improves quality of care in labour rooms and maternity OTs
- 102/108 ambulance services: Free transport for pregnant women to health facilities
- ASHA workers: Community-level support for ANC registration, institutional delivery promotion, postnatal visits

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Most maternal deaths are preventable with timely care. Any warning sign during pregnancy or after delivery requires IMMEDIATE medical attention — do not wait or attempt home remedies. India provides free maternal health services including transport, delivery, and emergency care under JSSK and related schemes.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: What is India's maternal mortality ratio and is it improving?
A: India's MMR (Maternal Mortality Ratio) has improved dramatically from 254 per 100,000 live births (2004-06) to 97 per 100,000 (2018-20) — a 60% reduction. However, India still falls short of the SDG target of below 70. Rural-urban and state disparities remain significant.

Q: What are the leading causes of maternal death in India?
A: Leading causes: haemorrhage (excessive bleeding after delivery), hypertensive disorders (pre-eclampsia, eclampsia), sepsis (infection), and complications of unsafe abortion. Most are preventable with skilled birth attendance and emergency obstetric care.

Q: How can I ensure a safe delivery in India?
A: Attend all antenatal check-ups (minimum 4 ANC visits), deliver in a hospital or health facility with skilled birth attendants, know the danger signs of pregnancy (heavy bleeding, severe headache, convulsions, reduced fetal movement), and have a birth preparedness plan (transport, blood donor, money).

Q: Is free maternity care available in India?
A: Yes. Janani Shishu Suraksha Karyakram (JSSK) provides completely free delivery (normal and caesarean), medicines, diet, diagnostics, and blood transfusion at government hospitals. Janani Suraksha Yojana (JSY) provides cash incentive for institutional delivery to eligible women.
