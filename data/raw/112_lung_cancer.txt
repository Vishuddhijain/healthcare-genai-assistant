TOPIC: Lung Cancer
CATEGORY: Non-Communicable Disease / Cancer
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/lung-cancer
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death worldwide and the second most commonly diagnosed cancer. Tobacco smoking is the primary cause, responsible for approximately 85% of cases. In India, lung cancer is among the top cancers in men and is increasingly seen in non-smokers, particularly women, linked to household air pollution (biomass fuel smoke), outdoor air pollution, and second-hand smoke. Lung cancer is often diagnosed at advanced stages because early symptoms are non-specific, leading to poor survival rates.

--- TYPES ---
1. Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (NSCLC): ~85% of cases; includes adenocarcinoma (most common, often in non-smokers), squamous cell carcinoma (strongly linked to smoking), large cell carcinoma
2. Small Cell Lung Cancer (SCLC): ~15% of cases; more aggressive, strongly linked to smoking, spreads faster

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Early stages: Often asymptomatic — a major reason for late diagnosis

As disease progresses:
- Persistent cough (new or worsening, especially if blood-tinged — haemoptysis)
- Chest pain (worse with deep breathing, coughing, laughing)
- Shortness of breath
- Hoarseness of voice
- Unexplained weight loss and loss of appetite
- Fatigue
- Recurrent respiratory infections (bronchitis, pneumonia)
- Wheezing

Advanced/metastatic symptoms: Bone pain (bone metastasis), headaches/neurological symptoms (brain metastasis), jaundice (liver metastasis), swollen lymph nodes

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
- Coughing up blood (haemoptysis) — always needs evaluation
- New persistent cough lasting more than 3 weeks, especially in smokers or ex-smokers
- Unexplained weight loss with respiratory symptoms
- Hoarseness lasting more than 2–3 weeks
- Chest pain with breathing difficulty

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Tobacco smoking (cigarettes, bidis, hookah) — the dominant risk factor; risk increases with duration and intensity
- Second-hand smoke (passive smoking) exposure
- Household air pollution (biomass fuel smoke — significant in Indian women, even non-smokers)
- Outdoor air pollution (PM2.5 exposure)
- Occupational exposures: Asbestos, radon, arsenic, chromium, nickel, diesel exhaust
- Family history of lung cancer
- Previous radiation therapy to the chest
- Pre-existing lung diseases (COPD, pulmonary fibrosis, prior TB)

--- PREVENTION ---
- Avoid tobacco in all forms — the single most effective prevention measure
- Avoid second-hand smoke exposure
- Reduce household air pollution: Transition to clean cooking fuels (LPG, electric)
- Reduce outdoor air pollution exposure where possible (masks during high pollution days)
- Occupational safety: PPE for workers exposed to asbestos, radon, industrial chemicals
- Radon testing in homes (in regions where relevant)

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- Workplace controls for asbestos, radon, and industrial carcinogens (ventilation, PPE, exposure limits)
- Reduction of ambient air pollution through emission controls, cleaner fuels
- National Tobacco Control Programme: Smoke-free public places under COTPA 2003

--- VACCINATION ---
- No vaccine for lung cancer; HPV and Hepatitis B vaccines prevent other cancers but not lung cancer

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- Chest X-ray: Initial screening but limited sensitivity for early disease
- CT scan (low-dose CT): Recommended for high-risk screening (heavy smokers, certain age groups) in some countries
- Biopsy (bronchoscopy, CT-guided needle biopsy): Confirms diagnosis and cancer type
- PET scan: Staging (extent of spread)
- Molecular testing: For NSCLC — identifies targetable mutations (EGFR, ALK, etc.) guiding targeted therapy

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- Surgery: For early-stage NSCLC (lobectomy or similar) — best outcomes when caught early
- Radiation therapy: Curative or palliative depending on stage
- Chemotherapy: Various regimens depending on cancer type and stage
- Targeted therapy: For NSCLC with specific mutations (EGFR inhibitors, ALK inhibitors, etc.)
- Immunotherapy: Checkpoint inhibitors for advanced NSCLC
- Palliative care: Symptom management for advanced disease — improves quality of life
- Treatment planning is individualised by an oncology team based on stage and molecular profile

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Persistent cough >3 weeks, especially with blood
- Unexplained weight loss with respiratory symptoms
- Hoarseness lasting >2–3 weeks
- Chest pain with breathing difficulty
- Heavy smokers or ex-smokers above 50 — discuss screening with doctor

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Lung cancer ranks among top cancers in Indian men; rising in non-smoking women due to household air pollution
- Tobacco Cessation Helpline: 1800-11-2356 (toll-free)
- National Tobacco Control Programme; COTPA 2003 bans smoking in public places, tobacco advertising
- Tata Memorial Centre, AIIMS, and National Cancer Grid hospitals provide specialised lung cancer care
- PM Ujjwala Yojana promotes clean cooking fuel to reduce household air pollution-linked lung cancer in women

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Lung cancer symptoms often appear late — any persistent respiratory symptom warrants medical evaluation, especially in smokers, ex-smokers, or those with significant air pollution exposure. Quitting tobacco at any stage reduces risk. Consult a qualified healthcare provider for diagnosis.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is lung cancer only caused by smoking?
A: No. While tobacco smoking (cigarettes and bidi) causes 80-85% of lung cancer cases, other causes include secondhand smoke, indoor air pollution (cooking on solid fuels), radon gas, asbestos exposure, occupational exposures (silica, chromium), and in some cases, no identifiable cause.

Q: What are the early warning signs of lung cancer?
A: Early lung cancer often has NO symptoms. Warning signs that appear later: persistent cough (lasting more than 3 weeks), coughing blood, shortness of breath, chest pain, hoarseness, unexplained weight loss, and recurrent chest infections. See a doctor for any of these symptoms.

Q: Is lung cancer treatable?
A: Yes, especially when detected early. Surgery, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and targeted therapies are available. Early-stage lung cancer has significantly better outcomes. PM-JAY covers lung cancer treatment at empanelled hospitals. Tata Memorial Centre and AIIMS provide comprehensive lung cancer care.

Q: Is low-dose CT screening for lung cancer available in India?
A: Low-dose CT screening is recommended for heavy smokers aged 50-80 years in many countries. In India, availability at government hospitals is limited, but is available at major government medical colleges and cancer centres. Discuss with a pulmonologist if you are a heavy smoker.
