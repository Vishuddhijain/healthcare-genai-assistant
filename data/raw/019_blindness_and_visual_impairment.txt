TOPIC: Blindness and Visual Impairment
CATEGORY: Sensory Health / Non-Communicable Disease

LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Vision is one of the most important senses. Visual impairment ranges from mild to complete blindness. An estimated 2.2 billion people globally have a near or distance vision impairment. The majority of vision impairment is preventable or treatable. In India, cataract is the leading cause of preventable blindness and is curable with a simple surgery.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- 2.2 billion people have near or distance vision impairment globally.
- At least 1 billion cases of vision impairment could be prevented or are yet to be addressed.
- India has approximately 12 million blind people — the largest blind population globally.
- Cataract is the leading cause of blindness in India (66.2% of all blindness).
- Most vision impairment disproportionately affects people over 50 years.
- Uncorrected refractive error (needing glasses) is the leading cause of vision impairment globally.

--- COMMON EYE CONDITIONS ---

1. CATARACT (Most common cause of blindness in India)
- Clouding of the lens of the eye causing blurry vision.
- Usually age-related; also caused by diabetes, injury, prolonged steroid use.
- Symptoms: Blurry or hazy vision, faded colours, glare, difficulty seeing at night.
- Treatment: Simple, safe surgical procedure (phacoemulsification or SICS) — restores vision. FREE at government hospitals under National Programme for Control of Blindness (NPCB).
- Do not delay surgery — cataract is curable.

2. GLAUCOMA (Silent blinding disease)
- Damage to the optic nerve, often caused by raised eye pressure.
- Often called the "silent thief of sight" — no symptoms until significant vision loss.
- Symptoms (when advanced): Gradual peripheral (side) vision loss, eye pain, halos around lights.
- Risk factors: Age >40, family history, diabetes, hypertension, prolonged steroid eye drops use.
- Treatment: Eye drops (to lower eye pressure), laser, surgery. Cannot restore lost vision but prevents further loss.
- Prevention: Regular eye checks for those at risk — early detection is key.

3. REFRACTIVE ERRORS (Most common cause of vision impairment)
- Near-sightedness (myopia): Difficulty seeing distant objects. Very common in children and young adults; rising rapidly.
- Far-sightedness (hyperopia): Difficulty seeing close objects.
- Astigmatism: Blurred vision due to irregular cornea shape.
- Presbyopia: Age-related difficulty reading close text (everyone gets this after 40).
- Treatment: Glasses, contact lenses, or corrective surgery. Free eye tests at government hospitals.

4. DIABETIC RETINOPATHY
- Damage to blood vessels of the retina due to prolonged diabetes.
- Leading cause of blindness in working-age adults.
- Often no symptoms until advanced stages — regular eye check-ups are essential for all diabetics.
- Prevention: Good blood sugar control is the most important step. Annual dilated eye exam for all diabetics.
- Treatment: Laser therapy, injections, or surgery (at specialised centres).

5. AGE-RELATED MACULAR DEGENERATION (AMD)
- Deterioration of the macula (central part of retina) with age.
- Affects central vision — reading, recognising faces.
- Risk factors: Age >50, smoking, family history.
- No cure but treatment can slow progression.

6. CONJUNCTIVITIS (Pink Eye)
- Inflammation of the thin layer covering the white of the eye.
- Viral (most common): Red eye, watery discharge, mild irritation. Highly contagious.
- Bacterial: Red eye, thick yellow-green discharge.
- Allergic: Itching, redness, watering — related to dust/pollen.
- Prevention: Wash hands frequently; do not touch eyes; do not share towels or eye drops.
- Treatment: Usually self-limiting (viral); antibiotic drops for bacterial (consult doctor).

--- EYE CARE HABITS ---
- Regular eye check-ups: Adults >40 every 2 years; diabetics annually; elderly annually.
- Screen time: Follow 20-20-20 rule — every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds.
- Protect from UV: Wear sunglasses outdoors (UV protection) to reduce cataract and AMD risk.
- Nutrition: Vitamin A (green leafy vegetables, carrots, milk) is essential for eye health.
- Vitamin A deficiency causes night blindness — common in Indian children. Free Vitamin A supplementation under UIP.
- Do not rub eyes vigorously.
- Remove contact lenses before sleeping; follow hygiene guidelines.

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC PROGRAMME ---
National Programme for Control of Blindness and Visual Impairment (NPCBVI):
- Free cataract surgery at all government district hospitals.
- Free spectacles to eligible patients.
- School eye screening programme.
- Blindness helpline: 1800-180-5025
Contact your District Blindness Control Society or government hospital eye department.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR IMMEDIATELY ---
- Sudden vision loss in one or both eyes
- Sudden onset of floaters (spots/cobwebs in vision) with flashes — possible retinal detachment (emergency)
- Eye injury
- Sudden eye pain with redness and nausea
- Double vision (diplopia)
- Vision loss after diabetes or hypertension — see an ophthalmologist promptly

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Can using screens too much permanently damage vision?
A: Extended screen use causes eye strain (dry eyes, fatigue, headache) but no permanent vision damage. Follow the 20-20-20 rule, ensure proper screen distance, and blink regularly.

Q: Can wearing glasses make vision worse?
A: No. Glasses correct refractive errors; they do not worsen the underlying eye condition. In children, wearing correct glasses is essential for proper visual development.

Q: Is cataract surgery safe?
A: Yes. Cataract surgery is one of the safest and most effective surgical procedures. It is performed millions of times a year in India with excellent outcomes. Free at government hospitals.

Q: My child squints. Should I be concerned?
A: Yes — any squinting (one eye turning inward or outward) in a child needs prompt evaluation by an ophthalmologist. Early treatment prevents permanent vision problems (amblyopia/lazy eye).

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
Regular eye examinations are key to preventing vision loss. Any sudden change in vision is a medical emergency. Seek care immediately. This information is for awareness only — diagnosis and treatment require a qualified ophthalmologist.
