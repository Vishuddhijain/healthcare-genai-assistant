--- OVERVIEW ---
Diabetes is a chronic disease that occurs either when the pancreas does not produce enough insulin, or when the body cannot effectively use the insulin it produces. Insulin is a hormone that regulates blood sugar. Uncontrolled diabetes leads to hyperglycaemia (raised blood sugar), which over time causes serious damage to the heart, blood vessels, eyes, kidneys, and nerves.

--- TYPES ---
1. Type 1 Diabetes: The pancreas produces little or no insulin by itself. It requires daily insulin administration. The cause is not fully known and currently cannot be prevented.
2. Type 2 Diabetes: The most common type. The body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't make enough. It is largely the result of excess body weight and physical inactivity. It is preventable through lifestyle changes.
3. Gestational Diabetes: Hyperglycaemia that develops during pregnancy with blood glucose levels above normal. It increases risk of complications during pregnancy and for both mother and child later in life.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Type 1 symptoms (may appear suddenly):
- Excessive urination (polyuria)
- Excessive thirst (polydipsia)
- Constant hunger
- Unexplained weight loss
- Vision changes
- Fatigue

Type 2 symptoms are similar but often less marked or absent for years. Many people are diagnosed late, after complications have already arisen.

--- RISK FACTORS (Type 2) ---
- Overweight or obesity
- Physical inactivity
- Unhealthy diet (high sugar, refined carbohydrates)
- Family history of diabetes
- Age (risk increases after 45)
- High blood pressure
- Abnormal cholesterol levels
- History of gestational diabetes

--- COMPLICATIONS (if uncontrolled) ---
- Heart disease and stroke (50% of people with diabetes die of cardiovascular disease)
- Blindness (diabetic retinopathy is a leading cause of blindness)
- Kidney failure (diabetes is among the leading causes)
- Nerve damage (neuropathy), especially in the feet
- Lower limb amputation
- Increased risk of infections

--- PREVENTION (Type 2) ---
- Achieve and maintain a healthy body weight
- Be physically active (at least 30 minutes of regular, moderate-intensity activity on most days)
- Eat a healthy diet — avoid sugar and saturated fats; increase intake of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
- Avoid tobacco use

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- If you experience frequent urination, unusual thirst, or unexplained weight loss, consult a doctor.
- Diabetes is diagnosed through blood tests. Early diagnosis prevents complications.
- People with diabetes should have regular check-ups for eyes, kidneys, and feet.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for awareness purposes only. Diabetes management, medication, and insulin dosage must be handled by a qualified medical professional. Do not self-diagnose or self-medicate.

Q: What is prediabetes and should I worry about it?
A: Prediabetes means blood sugar is higher than normal but not yet at diabetic levels. Without intervention, 15-30% of people with prediabetes develop Type 2 diabetes within 5 years. With lifestyle changes (weight loss, exercise, diet), prediabetes can be reversed. Take it seriously.

Q: Is free diabetes screening available in India?
A: Yes. Free blood sugar screening (fasting and post-meal) is available at all Ayushman Bharat Health and Wellness Centres for adults above 30 years. Government hospitals provide free HbA1c testing for diagnosed diabetics.

Q: What should a diabetic patient absolutely avoid eating?
A: No food is completely forbidden, but diabetics should strictly limit: white rice in large quantities, maida (refined flour) products, sugary drinks (soft drinks, fruit juice), sweets and mithai, deep-fried foods, and excessive fruit (especially high-sugar fruits like mango, banana). Consult a dietitian for a personalised plan.
