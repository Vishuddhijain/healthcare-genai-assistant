--- OVERVIEW ---
Suicide is a serious public health issue that claims approximately 703,000 lives worldwide every year. In India, suicide is a major public health concern — India accounts for approximately 17% of global suicide deaths. Behind every suicide death are many more people who experience suicidal thoughts or make attempts. Suicide is preventable. Understanding the warning signs, reducing stigma around mental health, and connecting people to support can save lives. This file is for awareness only — anyone in crisis should be connected to professional help immediately.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Approximately 703,000 people die by suicide globally each year — more deaths than from malaria, HIV/AIDS, breast cancer, or war in most years.
- In India, approximately 1.7 lakh (170,000) people die by suicide annually (NCRB 2022).
- Suicide is among the top three causes of death in the 15–39 age group in India.
- For every death by suicide, there are approximately 20 suicide attempts.
- 77% of global suicides occur in low- and middle-income countries.
- Suicide is preventable — with timely support and intervention, most suicidal crises can be resolved.
- Mental disorders (especially depression) account for up to 90% of suicide deaths where systematic studies are done.
- Means restriction (e.g., safe storage of pesticides, gun control) is one of the most effective suicide prevention strategies.
- NCRB data 2022: Farmers/agricultural workers and daily wage labourers are among highest at-risk occupational groups in India.

--- TYPES OF SUICIDAL BEHAVIOUR ---
1. Suicidal ideation: Thoughts of suicide — passive (wishing to die) or active (planning how)
2. Suicide attempt: A self-injurious act with some intent to die
3. Suicide: Death resulting from self-injurious behaviour with intent to die
4. Self-harm (non-suicidal): Deliberate self-injury without intent to die (but increases future risk)

Note: Non-suicidal self-harm should also be taken seriously and compassionately addressed.

--- WARNING SIGNS OF SUICIDE RISK ---
VERBAL SIGNS — Take seriously if a person says things like:
- "I want to die" / "I wish I were dead"
- "I want to kill myself"
- "Everyone would be better off without me"
- "I can't take it anymore"
- "Life is not worth living"
- "I won't be a problem for much longer"
- Talking or writing about death, dying, or suicide

BEHAVIOURAL SIGNS:
- Giving away valued possessions
- Saying goodbye to people in an unusual or final way
- Researching or acquiring means (medicines, pesticides, rope)
- Sudden calmness after a period of depression (may indicate a decision has been made)
- Withdrawing from friends, family, and usual activities
- Increased alcohol or substance use
- Reckless behaviour (driving dangerously, taking extreme risks)
- Changes in eating and sleep patterns
- Self-isolating and avoiding contact

EMOTIONAL SIGNS:
- Expressing hopelessness about the future
- Feeling like a burden to others
- Extreme sadness, anxiety, or agitation
- Expressing unbearable emotional pain
- Dramatic mood changes

--- RISK FACTORS ---
Note: Risk factors do not cause suicide — they increase vulnerability. Suicide is complex with multiple interacting factors.

Mental health conditions:
- Depression (strongest risk factor)
- Bipolar disorder
- Schizophrenia
- Substance use disorders (alcohol, drugs)
- Anxiety disorders
- Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
- Borderline personality disorder

Life circumstances:
- History of previous suicide attempt (strongest predictor)
- Family history of suicide
- Domestic violence, abuse, or trauma
- Chronic physical illness or pain
- Financial problems, debt, job loss
- Academic failure or exam pressure (a major driver in Indian context)
- Relationship breakdown, divorce, separation
- Bereavement (loss of a loved one)
- Social isolation and loneliness
- Access to means (pesticides — major method in rural India; medicines; weapons)

India-specific risk contexts:
- Examination pressure (students — 10th, 12th, competitive exams like JEE, NEET)
- Farmer distress and agricultural debt
- Dowry-related harassment of women
- LGBTQI+ discrimination and rejection
- Caste-based discrimination and violence
- Migration and social dislocation

--- PROTECTIVE FACTORS (REDUCE RISK) ---
- Strong social support and connections (family, friends, community)
- Access to mental health care
- Reasons for living (children, religious beliefs, personal values)
- Effective coping skills and problem-solving ability
- Sense of purpose and meaning
- Restricted access to lethal means
- Cultural, religious, and community beliefs that discourage suicide
- School and community connectedness

--- HOW TO HELP SOMEONE AT RISK ---
If you are concerned someone may be suicidal:

DO:
1. Ask directly: "Are you thinking about suicide?" — Asking DOES NOT plant the idea; it can open a life-saving conversation
2. Listen without judging: Let them express their feelings fully; do not dismiss their pain
3. Take all expressions of suicidal thoughts seriously — do not assume they are "just seeking attention"
4. Stay with the person if you are concerned they may act on their thoughts
5. Help remove access to potential means (medicines, pesticides, ropes) from the environment
6. Connect them with professional help: helpline, emergency services, doctor, psychiatrist
7. Follow up after initial contact — ongoing support matters

DO NOT:
- Do NOT promise to keep it a secret
- Do NOT argue that their life is good or they have no reason to feel suicidal
- Do NOT leave them alone if you believe they are in immediate danger
- Do NOT react with anger or shock — this can prevent the person from seeking further help
- Do NOT dismiss or minimise their feelings ("others have it worse")

CRISIS RESPONSE:
- If there is immediate danger: Call 112; stay with the person
- Take them to the nearest hospital emergency department
- Inform a family member or trusted adult

--- SAFE MESSAGING ON SUICIDE ---
For media, social media, teachers, community leaders:
- Avoid detailed descriptions of methods
- Avoid portraying suicide as glamorous, heroic, or inevitable
- Do NOT publish photos or notes
- Report on resources and warning signs
- Stories of hope and recovery are helpful
- Contagion effect (copycat suicides) is real — especially after celebrity deaths

--- PREVENTION STRATEGIES ---
Individual level:
- Mental health treatment (antidepressants, psychotherapy reduce suicide risk)
- Crisis counselling
- Safety planning (identifying warning signs, coping strategies, emergency contacts)
- Means restriction (locking away medicines/pesticides)

Community/societal level:
- Awareness campaigns to reduce stigma
- School mental health programmes
- Crisis helplines
- Training gatekeepers (teachers, ASHAs, family doctors, police) to identify and respond
- Pesticide restriction (WHO recommends restricting access to highly hazardous pesticides — major prevention measure in rural India)

--- WHEN TO SEEK PROFESSIONAL HELP ---
Seek immediate help if:
- Person expresses direct intent to die or has a plan
- Person has taken action to obtain means
- Person says goodbyes or gives away possessions
- Person is in acute mental health crisis

Seek help soon if:
- Person has been expressing hopelessness for days or weeks
- Depression, anxiety, or substance use is worsening
- Person has had previous attempts
- Significant life stressor occurred (job loss, relationship breakdown, exam failure)

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- NCRB data: India reports ~1.7 lakh suicide deaths annually (2022); states with highest numbers: Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, MP, West Bengal, Telangana
- Exam pressure: India loses thousands of students to suicide every year around board exam seasons (March) and competitive exam results (JEE, NEET, CA exams)
- Kota (Rajasthan): Major student suicide cluster city; intervention programmes ongoing
- Farmer suicides: Agricultural distress is a major driver — Maharashtra (Vidarbha), Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka have high rates
- Pesticide suicide: ~30% of global pesticide suicides occur in India — WHO advocates for highly hazardous pesticide restrictions
- LGBTQI+ youth: At significantly elevated risk due to discrimination, rejection, and Section 377 history
- NIMHANS: National reference centre for mental health and suicide prevention research
- Mental Healthcare Act 2017: Decriminalised attempted suicide in India (previously punishable under IPC Section 309)
- UMANG Programme: NIMHANS initiative for student mental health in schools

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Does asking about suicide increase the risk?
A: No. Research consistently shows that asking about suicide does not plant the idea or increase risk. In fact, it can provide relief and open communication. Always ask if you are concerned.

Q: Is suicide a sign of weakness or selfishness?
A: No. Suicide is associated with overwhelming psychological pain, mental illness, and sometimes neurobiological factors. Characterising it as weakness or selfishness prevents people from seeking help and is harmful to survivors.

Q: Is it true that people who talk about suicide don't actually do it?
A: This is a dangerous myth. Most people who die by suicide have communicated their intent beforehand. All statements about suicidal thoughts must be taken seriously.

Q: Was suicide decriminalised in India?
A: Yes. The Mental Healthcare Act 2017 effectively decriminalised suicide attempt in India. A person who attempts suicide is to be treated with care, not punishment.

Q: How can I help someone without being intrusive?
A: Be present, be consistent, and be honest. Say: "I've noticed you seem to be struggling and I care about you. Are you thinking about suicide?" Listening without judgment is one of the most helpful things you can do.
