TOPIC: Malaria
CATEGORY: Communicable Disease / Vector-Borne
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/malaria
LAST UPDATED: December 2025

--- OVERVIEW ---
Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites and spread to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. It is preventable and curable. Malaria does NOT spread person to person — it requires a mosquito as a carrier. It is most common in tropical and subtropical countries including parts of India.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Globally, there were an estimated 282 million malaria cases in 2024.
- 610,000 deaths occurred due to malaria in 2024 worldwide.
- Children under 5 years account for about 75% of all malaria deaths.
- India has made significant progress in reducing malaria cases but still reports a substantial burden.
- The WHO African Region bears 95% of the global malaria burden.

--- HOW IT SPREADS ---
- Bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito (primary route)
- Blood transfusion with infected blood (rare)
- Sharing contaminated needles

Malaria DOES NOT spread through:
- Casual contact, coughing, or sneezing
- Air or water

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Mild symptoms (appear 7-30 days after mosquito bite):
- Fever and chills
- Headache
- Muscle aches
- Nausea and vomiting
- Fatigue

Severe symptoms (require emergency care):
- High fever with confusion or altered consciousness
- Seizures
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe anemia
- Yellowing of skin/eyes (jaundice)
- Abnormal bleeding

NOTE: P. falciparum malaria (most dangerous type) can progress to severe illness and death within 24 hours if left untreated.

--- WHO IS AT HIGHER RISK ---
- Children under 5 years
- Pregnant women (can cause anemia, low birth weight, premature birth)
- People with HIV/AIDS
- Travelers visiting malaria-endemic regions without taking preventive medication
- People living in areas with poor mosquito control

--- PREVENTION ---
Personal protection:
- Sleep under insecticide-treated bed nets (ITNs)
- Use mosquito repellent (containing DEET) on exposed skin
- Wear long-sleeved clothes and full trousers in the evenings and early mornings
- Use mosquito coils or indoor sprays
- Ensure windows and doors have mesh screens

Environmental control:
- Eliminate standing water (in pots, tyres, coolers, flower pots) — breeding sites for mosquitoes
- Keep surroundings clean and dry
- Support community fogging and drainage efforts

For travelers: Anti-malarial medications may be recommended before visiting endemic areas — consult a doctor.

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
Malaria is diagnosed by blood tests — either a Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) or microscopy (blood smear). Early diagnosis and prompt treatment prevent complications and death. Free testing is available at government health centres.

--- TREATMENT ---
Malaria is treatable. Treatment depends on the type of malaria parasite and severity. Artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs) are the most effective treatment for P. falciparum malaria. Complete the full treatment course even if you feel better.

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC PROGRAMME ---
The National Vector Borne Disease Control Programme (NVBDCP) under the Ministry of Health manages malaria control in India. Free diagnosis and treatment are available at government facilities. Annual Mass Drug Administration (MDA) is conducted in high-burden areas.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
See a doctor immediately if:
- You have fever, chills, and headache after being in a malaria-endemic area
- Any child under 5 has high fever
- Symptoms include confusion, difficulty breathing, or seizures (emergency — call 112)

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This is awareness information only. Malaria treatment must be prescribed by a qualified doctor. Do not self-medicate with anti-malarials.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Can I get malaria from a vaccinated area?
A: Malaria is spread by mosquitoes, not from person to person. If you are in a malaria-endemic area, you are at risk regardless of your vaccination status (there is no widely available malaria vaccine in India yet). Use bed nets, repellent, and antimalarials if recommended by a doctor for high-risk areas.

Q: What is the difference between falciparum and vivax malaria?
A: P. falciparum is the most dangerous type — can cause cerebral malaria and death within 24-48 hours if untreated. P. vivax is less immediately dangerous but can cause relapses months or years later from dormant liver stages. Both require prompt treatment — falciparum is a medical emergency.

Q: Is free malaria testing available in India?
A: Yes. Rapid Diagnostic Tests (RDTs) and blood smear microscopy are available free at all government PHCs, CHCs, and hospitals, especially in endemic areas. ASHA workers in endemic areas carry RDT kits for doorstep testing. Do not delay testing if you have fever in a malaria area.

Q: Can malaria return after successful treatment?
A: P. vivax malaria can relapse after apparent cure because the parasite can remain dormant in the liver. Treatment with primaquine is given to prevent relapses. Primaquine must not be given to pregnant women or G6PD deficient individuals — a doctor will test first.
