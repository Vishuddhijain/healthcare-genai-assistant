TOPIC: Dementia and Alzheimer's Disease
CATEGORY: Mental Health
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/dementia
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Dementia is a syndrome — usually chronic or progressive — in which there is deterioration in memory, thinking, behaviour, and the ability to perform everyday activities. Dementia is NOT a normal part of ageing. It is caused by brain diseases. Alzheimer's disease is the most common form, accounting for 60–70% of cases. Currently, there is no cure, but care and support can significantly improve the quality of life for people with dementia and their caregivers.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Over 55 million people globally have dementia; this is expected to rise to 139 million by 2050.
- In India, approximately 8.8 million people have dementia (Dementia India Report).
- Alzheimer's disease causes 60–70% of all dementia cases.
- Dementia is one of the leading causes of disability and dependency among older adults.
- Women are disproportionately affected — both as patients and as caregivers.
- Up to 40% of dementia cases may be preventable by modifying risk factors.

--- TYPES OF DEMENTIA ---
1. Alzheimer's disease: Most common. Gradual memory loss, language problems, disorientation, behavioural changes.
2. Vascular dementia: Caused by strokes or reduced blood supply to the brain. Often step-like deterioration.
3. Lewy body dementia: Associated with abnormal protein deposits in the brain. Visual hallucinations and motor symptoms.
4. Frontotemporal dementia: Affects personality, behaviour, and language more than memory initially.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Early stage (often missed):
- Forgetting recent events or conversations
- Getting lost in familiar places
- Losing track of time
- Difficulty with complex tasks (managing finances, cooking)
- Repeating the same questions or stories

Middle stage:
- Increased memory loss — forgetting names, faces of family members
- Needing help with daily activities (dressing, bathing)
- Wandering and getting lost
- Behavioural changes: agitation, suspicion, depression
- Difficulty recognising family members

Late stage:
- Complete dependence on caregivers
- Difficulty eating and swallowing
- Bed-bound
- Vulnerability to infections

--- DIFFERENCE FROM NORMAL AGEING ---
Normal ageing: Occasionally forgetting where you put your keys, but remembering later.
Dementia: Forgetting what keys are for, or being unable to recognise family members.
If memory problems are interfering with daily life — see a doctor.

--- RISK FACTORS ---
Non-modifiable:
- Age (risk doubles every 5 years after 65)
- Genetics (family history — especially first-degree relatives with Alzheimer's)
- Down syndrome

Modifiable (reducing these can prevent up to 40% of cases):
- Hypertension (especially in midlife)
- Obesity
- Physical inactivity
- Diabetes
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Smoking
- Depression
- Social isolation
- Air pollution
- Low education levels
- Hearing loss (untreated)

--- PREVENTION ---
Based on current evidence:
- Be physically active (most strongly supported intervention)
- Manage cardiovascular risk factors: control blood pressure, blood sugar, cholesterol
- Do not smoke
- Limit alcohol
- Stay mentally active: reading, puzzles, learning new skills
- Stay socially engaged
- Treat depression and hearing loss
- Quality sleep (sleep deprivation increases amyloid plaque build-up in the brain)
- Eat a healthy diet (Mediterranean or MIND diet shows some evidence)

--- CARE FOR PEOPLE WITH DEMENTIA ---
Dementia care requires patience, compassion, and adaptability:
- Maintain routine — familiar structure is comforting
- Keep environment safe: remove trip hazards, install locks on doors to prevent wandering
- Communicate simply and calmly: short sentences, one idea at a time
- Redirect rather than argue when confused
- Allow independence in tasks they can still do
- Watch for signs of pain, as people with advanced dementia may not communicate it verbally

--- CAREGIVER SUPPORT ---
Caregiving for a person with dementia is demanding. Caregivers need support too:
- Alzheimer's and Related Disorders Society of India (ARDSI): ardsi.org — India's leading dementia support organisation
- Provides caregiver training, helplines, and day care centres
- ARDSI Dementia Helpline: 1800-425-0630 (free)
- Tele MANAS: 14416 (for caregiver mental health)

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is memory loss always dementia?
A: No. Many conditions cause memory problems including vitamin B12 deficiency, thyroid disorders, depression, and medication side effects — all treatable. Always see a doctor to rule out reversible causes.

Q: Can young people get dementia?
A: Yes. Early-onset dementia (before 65 years) accounts for about 9% of cases. It is less common but does occur.

Q: Is dementia hereditary?
A: Most dementia is not directly hereditary. However, having a first-degree relative (parent or sibling) with Alzheimer's does increase risk. Rare genetic forms (e.g., APOE-e4 gene) do exist.

Q: Should someone with dementia be put in a nursing home?
A: This depends on the individual's needs, family situation, and local resources. Many families in India care for dementia patients at home. ARDSI offers guidance and support for both options.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This is awareness information only. If you or a family member are experiencing memory or behavioural changes, please consult a neurologist or psychiatrist. Contact ARDSI (ardsi.org) or call 1800-425-0630 for support.
