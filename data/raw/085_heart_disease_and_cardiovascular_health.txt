--- OVERVIEW ---
Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are a group of disorders affecting the heart and blood vessels. They are the leading cause of death globally and in India. India accounts for approximately one-fifth of global CVD deaths, and Indians tend to develop heart disease a decade earlier than Western populations. CVD deaths in India are estimated at around 2.8–4 million annually. The most common CVDs are coronary artery disease (CAD/ischaemic heart disease), stroke, heart failure, and peripheral arterial disease.

--- TYPES ---
1. Coronary Artery Disease (CAD): Narrowing or blockage of coronary arteries due to plaque build-up (atherosclerosis). Leads to angina or heart attack (myocardial infarction).
2. Stroke: Occurs when blood supply to the brain is cut off (ischaemic stroke) or a blood vessel bursts (haemorrhagic stroke). Causes sudden neurological deficits.
3. Heart Failure: Heart is unable to pump blood efficiently. Can result from CAD, hypertension, or cardiomyopathy.
4. Hypertensive Heart Disease: Damage to the heart from chronic high blood pressure.
5. Rheumatic Heart Disease: Damage to heart valves from rheumatic fever (complication of streptococcal throat infection) — still significant in India.
6. Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD): Narrowing of arteries supplying blood to limbs.
7. Congenital Heart Disease: Heart defects present at birth.
8. Cardiomyopathy: Disease of the heart muscle affecting its pumping function.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Heart Attack — Warning Signs (Act within Golden Hour — first 60 minutes):
- Chest pain or discomfort (pressure, squeezing, fullness, or pain in centre/left side of chest)
- Pain radiating to the left arm, shoulder, neck, jaw, or back
- Shortness of breath (with or without chest pain)
- Cold sweat, nausea, or vomiting
- Lightheadedness or sudden dizziness
- NOTE: Women may have atypical symptoms — jaw pain, back pain, extreme fatigue without chest pain

Stroke — FAST mnemonic:
F — Face drooping (one side of face droops, numbness)
A — Arm weakness (one arm weak or numb)
S — Speech difficulty (slurred, strange, or unable to speak)
T — Time to call emergency services immediately (112 in India)

Other CVD symptoms:
- Palpitations (irregular, fast, or pounding heartbeat)
- Swelling in feet, ankles, or legs (heart failure)
- Fatigue and weakness on minimal exertion
- Persistent cough (sometimes with pink or white mucus — heart failure)
- Chest pain on exertion (angina) that resolves with rest

--- RISK FACTORS ---
Modifiable:
- Hypertension (high blood pressure) — single biggest risk factor
- Tobacco use (smoking, smokeless tobacco)
- High blood cholesterol (dyslipidaemia)
- Diabetes and insulin resistance
- Obesity and overweight
- Physical inactivity
- Unhealthy diet (high in salt, saturated fats, trans fats, sugar; low in fruits and vegetables)
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Stress and poor mental health
- Air pollution (indoor and outdoor)

Non-modifiable:
- Age (risk increases after 45 in men, after menopause in women)
- Family history of CVD
- South Asian ethnicity (Indians have genetically higher CVD risk)
- Previous heart attack or stroke

--- PREVENTION ---
Lifestyle changes (most impactful):
- Quit tobacco (smoking and smokeless) — reduces CVD risk substantially within a year
- Eat a heart-healthy diet: Reduce salt (less than 5 g/day), saturated and trans fats, added sugars; increase fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, fish
- Reduce refined oil usage; prefer healthier oils (mustard, groundnut, sunflower — in moderation)
- Be physically active: At least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week
- Maintain healthy weight (BMI 18.5–22.9 kg/m² for Indians; Indian cut-offs are lower than global)
- Control blood pressure, blood sugar, and cholesterol with regular monitoring and medical management
- Limit alcohol intake
- Manage stress through yoga, meditation, social support
- Regular check-ups after age 30 (or earlier with risk factors): Blood pressure, fasting blood sugar, lipid profile

--- KEY NUMBERS TO KNOW ---
- Blood Pressure: Target <130/80 mmHg (ideal <120/80 mmHg)
- Fasting Blood Sugar: <100 mg/dL (normal); 100–125 (pre-diabetes); >126 (diabetes)
- LDL Cholesterol: <100 mg/dL for general population; <70 mg/dL for high-risk individuals
- BMI (India): <23 kg/m² ideal; >25 overweight; >30 obese
- Waist circumference (India): Men <90 cm; Women <80 cm (central obesity cut-offs)

--- FIRST AID (Basic Awareness) ---
If someone is having a heart attack:
1. Call 108 (national ambulance) immediately
2. Have the person sit or lie down in a comfortable position
3. Loosen tight clothing
4. If the person is conscious and not allergic, give aspirin 325 mg (chew, not swallow) if available and trained to do so
5. Do NOT leave them alone

CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation):
- If the person is unresponsive and not breathing normally: Begin chest compressions (30 compressions at 100–120/min, 5–6 cm deep) and rescue breaths (if trained)
- If untrained: Hands-only CPR (continuous hard and fast chest compressions) until emergency services arrive

--- GOVERNMENT PROGRAMMES ---
- National Programme for Prevention and Control of Cancer, Diabetes, Cardiovascular Diseases and Stroke (NPCDCS): Free screening at government health centres
- Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY: Covers cardiac surgery and procedures for eligible beneficiaries
- HWCs (Health and Wellness Centres): Community-level CVD risk screening
- Emergency: 112 (national emergency), 108 (ambulance in most states)

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Any chest pain or discomfort — even if mild or transient
- Palpitations or feeling of irregular heartbeat
- Unexplained breathlessness
- Swollen ankles
- Dizziness or fainting
- If you have hypertension, diabetes, or high cholesterol — regular follow-up is essential
- Screening at age 30+ (or earlier with risk factors) for BP, blood sugar, cholesterol

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. A heart attack and stroke are medical emergencies — call 108/112 immediately. Do not drive yourself to the hospital during a suspected heart attack. Always follow your doctor's prescribed treatment for any cardiovascular condition. Do not self-medicate.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Can heart disease be prevented in young people?
A: Yes. 80% of premature heart attacks and strokes are preventable. Young people should avoid tobacco, exercise regularly, eat healthy, manage stress, and control blood pressure and blood sugar. Don't wait until middle age â the foundation is built in your 20s and 30s.

Q: What is the FASTEST way to tell if someone is having a heart attack?
A: Classic signs: chest pain or tightness (may radiate to left arm or jaw), sweating, shortness of breath, nausea. In women: unusual fatigue, jaw pain, nausea without chest pain. If in doubt â CALL 112 immediately. Time lost = heart muscle lost.

Q: Is ghee and coconut oil bad for the heart?
A: Ghee and coconut oil are high in saturated fats. Moderate consumption (1-2 tsp/day of ghee) is unlikely to harm a healthy person, but excessive use increases LDL cholesterol and heart disease risk. For those with existing heart disease, a cardiologist and dietitian should guide dietary fat choices.

Q: Is stress really as dangerous as physical risk factors for heart disease?
A: Yes. Chronic stress is an independent risk factor for heart disease â it raises blood pressure, promotes inflammation, disrupts sleep, and encourages unhealthy behaviours. Stress management through yoga, exercise, adequate sleep, and social connection is an important part of heart health.
