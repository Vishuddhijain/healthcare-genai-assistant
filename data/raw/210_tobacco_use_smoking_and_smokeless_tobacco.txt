TOPIC: Tobacco Use, Smoking, and Smokeless Tobacco
CATEGORY: Non-Communicable Disease / Lifestyle
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/tobacco | NHP India | MoHFW India | NTCP India
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Tobacco is the single largest preventable cause of death globally. It kills more than 8 million people each year — more than HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis, and malaria combined. In India, tobacco is used in many forms: cigarettes, bidis (hand-rolled), hookah (water pipe), gutka, paan masala, khaini, and snuff. India has over 268 million tobacco users — the second largest in the world. Tobacco causes cancer, heart disease, stroke, lung disease, and many other conditions. There is NO safe level of tobacco use.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Tobacco kills more than 8 million people globally per year; over 1.3 million of these are non-smokers exposed to secondhand smoke.
- In India, tobacco accounts for approximately 13.5 lakh (1.35 million) deaths per year.
- India has ~268 million tobacco users (GATS 2016–17) — about 29% of adults.
- India is unique: Smokeless tobacco use (gutka, khaini, paan masala) is more common than cigarette/bidi smoking.
- Bidis, not cigarettes, are the most commonly smoked tobacco product in India (77% of smokers).
- Tobacco causes 27% of all cancer deaths in India.
- The Indian government enacted Cigarettes and Other Tobacco Products Act (COTPA) 2003 to regulate tobacco.
- Quitting tobacco at any age provides health benefits — within 20 minutes of quitting, heart rate begins to drop.
- Free Tobacco Cessation services available at government hospitals under NTCP (National Tobacco Control Programme).

--- TYPES OF TOBACCO USE IN INDIA ---
SMOKED TOBACCO:
1. Bidis: Most common smoked tobacco in India; small hand-rolled cigarette in tendu/temburni leaf; more harmful than cigarettes in many ways (no filter, high tar/nicotine)
2. Cigarettes: Machine-manufactured; widely advertised; rising among youth
3. Hookah/Shisha (Water pipe): Perceived as less harmful — FALSE; causes same diseases as cigarettes; one session equivalent to 100 cigarettes in smoke volume
4. Cigars and pipes
5. Electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes): BANNED in India under PECA 2019; not a proven cessation aid

SMOKELESS TOBACCO:
6. Gutka: Crushed tobacco + areca nut + lime; packed in tiny sachets; major cause of oral cancer in India; banned in many states but widely available
7. Khaini: Raw tobacco + lime; chewed; major cause of oral cancer
8. Paan masala with tobacco: Chewed; oral cancer cause
9. Zarda: Sweetened tobacco with paan; chewed
10. Snuff (Nasal/Oral): Inhaled or placed in gum

ALL FORMS OF TOBACCO ARE HARMFUL — there is no safe tobacco product.

--- HEALTH EFFECTS OF TOBACCO ---
CANCER (tobacco causes at least 12 types):
- Lung cancer (most commonly associated; caused by smoking)
- Oral cavity cancer: India has one of highest rates globally — primarily due to gutka/smokeless tobacco
- Pharynx, larynx, oesophagus, stomach cancer
- Kidney, bladder, pancreatic cancer
- Cervical cancer (compounded by HPV)
- Acute myeloid leukaemia

HEART AND BLOOD VESSELS:
- Increases risk of heart attack by 2–4 times
- Stroke (doubles risk)
- Peripheral arterial disease (narrowing of arteries to limbs — can lead to amputation)
- Aneurysm

LUNGS:
- Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD): Emphysema and chronic bronchitis — leading cause of COPD in India
- Respiratory infections more frequent and severe
- Worsens asthma significantly

REPRODUCTIVE AND MATERNAL HEALTH:
- Reduces fertility in men and women
- During pregnancy: Low birth weight, premature birth, stillbirth
- Increased SIDS (sudden infant death syndrome) risk

OTHER:
- Diabetes complications worsened
- Immune system impairment
- Gum disease, tooth loss, staining
- Eye disease (cataracts, macular degeneration)
- Premature skin ageing
- Erectile dysfunction

--- SECONDHAND SMOKE (PASSIVE SMOKING) ---
- No safe level of secondhand smoke exposure exists
- Secondhand smoke contains 7,000+ chemicals; hundreds are toxic; ~70 cause cancer
- Adults exposed: Higher risk of heart disease, lung cancer, stroke
- Children exposed: Ear infections, severe asthma, pneumonia, SIDS, impaired lung development
- Exposure during pregnancy: Low birth weight, miscarriage
- India's COTPA bans smoking in public places, but enforcement varies

THIRDHAND SMOKE:
- Toxic residue from tobacco smoke that settles on surfaces and clothing
- Can harm infants and toddlers who crawl on or put things in mouths
- Difficult to remove; persists long after smoking stops

--- SYMPTOMS OF TOBACCO-RELATED ILLNESS (SEEK MEDICAL EVALUATION) ---
Warning symptoms that may indicate tobacco-related disease:
- Chronic cough (lasting >3 weeks) — may indicate COPD or lung cancer
- Coughing blood (haemoptysis) — seek immediate care
- Shortness of breath on exertion (COPD, heart disease)
- Unexplained weight loss with cough
- Change in voice/hoarseness (laryngeal cancer)
- White or red patches in mouth (leukoplakia/erythroplakia) — pre-cancerous from smokeless tobacco
- Difficulty swallowing (oesophageal cancer)
- Chest pain or tightness

--- WARNING SIGNS — SEEK IMMEDIATE CARE ---
- Coughing blood
- Severe chest pain
- Sudden difficulty breathing
- White patches in mouth that don't heal (leukoplakia — get biopsy)
- Lump in neck or jaw
- Unexplained weight loss + chronic cough

--- RISK FACTORS FOR TOBACCO ADDICTION ---
- Nicotine: Highly addictive — physical and psychological dependence
- Peer pressure (most tobacco use starts before age 18)
- Social norms and cultural acceptance (particularly gutka in rural India)
- Stress and mental health issues
- Advertising and marketing (especially to youth)
- Low cost and wide availability (bidis especially)
- Early age of first use

--- PREVENTION ---
1. Never start: Best prevention — especially crucial for children and adolescents
2. School education programmes: Tobacco-free school zones; NTCP includes school programmes
3. Tobacco taxation: Higher prices reduce consumption — COTPA tax provisions
4. Pictorial health warnings on tobacco products: Mandated in India (85% of packaging)
5. No advertising/promotion: Bans under COTPA
6. Smoke-free public spaces: COTPA mandates; strict enforcement needed
7. Peer education and social norms change

--- TOBACCO CESSATION (QUITTING) ---
Benefits of quitting:
- 20 minutes: Heart rate and blood pressure begin to drop
- 12 hours: Carbon monoxide in blood returns to normal
- 2 weeks: Circulation improves; lung function increases
- 1 month: Coughing and shortness of breath decrease
- 1 year: Heart disease risk halves
- 5 years: Stroke risk reduces to that of a non-smoker
- 10 years: Lung cancer risk halves; oral cancer risk significantly reduced
- 15 years: Heart disease risk equals that of a non-smoker

How to quit (evidence-based):
1. Set a quit date
2. Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT): Patches, gum, lozenges — reduces withdrawal symptoms
3. Prescription medications (under doctor's guidance): Varenicline (Champix), Bupropion
4. Behavioural support: Counselling, support groups, quitline
5. Combination therapy (NRT + counselling) most effective

India cessation resources:
- National Tobacco Cessation Quitline: 1800-11-2356 (FREE, toll-free)
- Tobacco Cessation Clinics (TCCs): At government district hospitals, medical colleges under NTCP
- mCessation programme: Free SMS-based cessation support — register by SMS "QUIT" to 011-22901701

--- VACCINATION ---
- HPV vaccination: Protects against cervical cancer (compounded by tobacco use in women)
- No vaccine against tobacco-related diseases; cessation is the intervention

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- For any warning symptoms listed above
- If you want help quitting tobacco — effective treatments are available FREE at government hospitals
- White patches or sores in mouth not healing within 2 weeks — dental/ENT evaluation urgently
- Annual oral cancer screening if you use smokeless tobacco — available at CHCs/district hospitals

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- National Tobacco Control Programme (NTCP): Tobacco Cessation Clinics at government hospitals; public awareness campaigns; quitline 1800-11-2356
- COTPA 2003 (Cigarettes and Other Tobacco Products Act): Bans advertising, mandates warnings, regulates sale to minors (<18); smoke-free public places
- PECA 2019: Prohibition of Electronic Cigarettes Act — e-cigarettes banned in India
- Tobacco and oral cancer: India accounts for ~1/3 of global oral cancer cases — largely due to gutka and smokeless tobacco use
- Gutka ban: Many states (Maharashtra, Kerala, Rajasthan, etc.) have banned gutka, but it circulates under different brand names
- Bidi workers: Millions of women who roll bidis are exposed to tobacco without smoking it — health risk
- GATS India 2 (2016–17): Official national data on tobacco use; next round planned
- Scheme for eradication of tobacco from rural areas: NHM health education component
- Khaini/Zarda: Dominant in Bihar, UP, Jharkhand — oral cancer belt of India

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is smokeless tobacco (gutka/khaini) safer than cigarettes?
A: No. Smokeless tobacco is NOT safer. It causes oral cancer, throat cancer, and oesophageal cancer, and is highly addictive. India has one of the world's highest rates of oral cancer, largely driven by smokeless tobacco use.

Q: Is hookah/shisha safe because the smoke is filtered through water?
A: No. Water does NOT filter harmful chemicals or reduce nicotine. A single hookah session exposes users to 100× the volume of smoke from a cigarette, with equivalent tar, carbon monoxide, and carcinogens.

Q: Are e-cigarettes a safe alternative?
A: E-cigarettes are NOT proven safe. They are banned in India (PECA 2019). While they may have fewer toxins than cigarettes, they are NOT harmless, particularly for adolescents whose developing lungs and brains are vulnerable. They are not a proven quit aid.

Q: Will quitting smoking damage my lungs because the toxins will come out?
A: No. This is a myth. Quitting tobacco has ONLY benefits — no harmful effects of quitting. Cough may temporarily increase as cilia (hair-like structures) in the airways begin recovering, which is a sign of healing.

Q: I've smoked for 30 years. Is it too late to quit?
A: Never too late. Even at 60–70 years, quitting reduces risk of heart attack, stroke, and cancer, and improves quality of life and breathing. Benefits begin within 20 minutes of the last cigarette.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
All forms of tobacco are harmful and addictive. Help is available for quitting — it is not just willpower. Call 1800-11-2356 (free) or visit a government Tobacco Cessation Clinic. If you have symptoms of tobacco-related illness (chronic cough, white mouth patches, shortness of breath), see a doctor immediately. Treatment is available at government hospitals. Tobacco kills — quitting is the single most impactful health decision you can make.
