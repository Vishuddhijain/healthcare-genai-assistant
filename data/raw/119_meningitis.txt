TOPIC: Meningitis
CATEGORY: Communicable Disease / Neurological
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/meningitis
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Meningitis is inflammation of the meninges (membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord), most commonly caused by bacterial or viral infections, but also by fungi or parasites. Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency with high mortality (up to 50% if untreated) and significant rates of long-term disability (hearing loss, learning difficulties, seizures) among survivors — affecting roughly 1 in 5 survivors. WHO's "Defeating Meningitis by 2030" roadmap targets elimination of bacterial meningitis epidemics. Major bacterial causes: Neisseria meningitidis (meningococcus), Streptococcus pneumoniae (pneumococcus), Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib), and Group B Streptococcus (in newborns).

--- TYPES ---
1. Bacterial meningitis: Most severe; medical emergency; requires immediate antibiotics
2. Viral meningitis: Usually milder, self-limiting (enteroviruses most common cause)
3. Fungal meningitis: Rare; occurs mainly in immunocompromised individuals (e.g. cryptococcal meningitis in advanced HIV)
4. Tuberculous meningitis: Caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis; subacute onset; significant burden in India
5. Neonatal meningitis: Group B Streptococcus, E. coli — affects newborns, high mortality/disability risk

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Classic triad: Fever + headache + neck stiffness (not always all present, especially in infants)

Additional symptoms:
- Photophobia (sensitivity to light)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Altered consciousness, confusion, drowsiness
- Seizures
- Petechial/purpuric rash (non-blanching — does not fade with pressure) — characteristic of meningococcal meningitis, a medical emergency

In infants (may lack classic signs):
- High-pitched cry, irritability, refusing feeds
- Bulging fontanelle (soft spot)
- Lethargy, poor tone (floppy baby)
- Fever or low temperature

--- WARNING SIGNS (EMERGENCY) ---
- Non-blanching rash (petechiae/purpura) with fever — meningococcal sepsis, call ambulance immediately
- Severe headache with neck stiffness and fever
- Altered consciousness or seizures with fever
- Bulging fontanelle in infant with fever
- Rapidly progressive symptoms

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Age: Infants and young children, elderly at higher risk
- Crowded living conditions (hostels, military camps, schools)
- Immunocompromised status (HIV, asplenia, complement deficiencies)
- Recent respiratory infection
- TB exposure (for tuberculous meningitis)
- Unvaccinated status (Hib, pneumococcal, meningococcal vaccines)
- Cochlear implants, CSF leaks (recurrent bacterial meningitis risk)

--- PREVENTION ---
Vaccination (most effective):
- Hib vaccine: Part of Pentavalent vaccine in UIP (6, 10, 14 weeks)
- Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine (PCV): Part of UIP (phased rollout)
- Meningococcal vaccine: Recommended for travellers to meningitis belt, Hajj/Umrah pilgrims, college dormitory residents, asplenic individuals
- BCG: Provides some protection against severe TB including TB meningitis in children

Other measures:
- Prompt treatment of ear/sinus infections (can spread to meninges)
- TB prevention and treatment (reduces TB meningitis risk)
- Avoid overcrowding; good ventilation in shared living spaces

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- Outbreak response: Chemoprophylaxis (rifampicin/ciprofloxacin) for close contacts of meningococcal meningitis cases
- Ventilation in crowded settings (hostels, camps)
- Surveillance for meningococcal disease clusters

--- VACCINATION ---
- Hib and PCV: Part of UIP for infants — free
- Meningococcal vaccine: For high-risk groups (Hajj pilgrims — mandatory; military recruits; asplenic patients) — available privately/through specific programmes
- BCG: At birth, under UIP

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- Lumbar puncture (CSF analysis): Definitive — cell count, glucose, protein, Gram stain, culture
- CSF PCR: Rapid pathogen identification
- Blood culture
- CT scan (sometimes done before LP to exclude raised intracranial pressure)
- For TB meningitis: CSF AFB smear/culture, GeneXpert, ADA levels, imaging (basal exudates on MRI)

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- Bacterial meningitis: IMMEDIATE IV antibiotics (empirical, then targeted) — every hour of delay increases mortality; hospital/ICU care
- Steroids (dexamethasone): Sometimes given with antibiotics to reduce inflammation/complications — doctor decision
- Viral meningitis: Usually supportive care (rest, fluids, pain relief); most resolve without specific antiviral
- TB meningitis: Prolonged anti-TB therapy (often longer than pulmonary TB) plus corticosteroids — specialist supervised
- Fungal meningitis: Antifungal therapy (amphotericin B, fluconazole) — specialist/hospital setting
- Chemoprophylaxis for close contacts of confirmed meningococcal cases

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- ANY suspected meningitis symptom (fever + headache + neck stiffness, or fever + non-blanching rash, or infant with bulging fontanelle/lethargy/poor feeding) — EMERGENCY, go to hospital immediately
- Do not wait — bacterial meningitis can be fatal within hours

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- TB meningitis remains a significant cause in India given high TB burden — high index of suspicion needed for subacute headache/fever with neurological signs
- Hib and PCV vaccines reduce bacterial meningitis burden under UIP
- Meningococcal vaccination mandatory for Hajj/Umrah pilgrims from India
- Government hospitals provide emergency meningitis management; do not delay seeking care due to cost concerns — treat as emergency

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency — any suspected case requires immediate hospital care. Do not delay seeking treatment. A non-blanching rash with fever in a child is a red flag requiring emergency assessment.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: What is the most dangerous type of meningitis?
A: Bacterial meningitis (caused by Neisseria meningitidis, Streptococcus pneumoniae, or Haemophilus influenzae) is the most dangerous — it can be fatal within 24 hours and cause permanent disability (deafness, brain damage) in survivors. Viral meningitis is usually less severe and self-limiting.

Q: How do I identify a meningitis rash?
A: A distinctive non-blanching rash (petechiae or purpura — red/purple spots that do NOT fade when pressed with a glass) indicates possible meningococcal septicaemia — a medical emergency. This is the glass test. Call 112 immediately if this rash is present.

Q: Are there vaccines for meningitis available in India?
A: PCV (Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine) — free under UIP — protects against pneumococcal meningitis. Hib vaccine — free under UIP — protects against Hib meningitis. Meningococcal vaccine is recommended for high-risk individuals and travellers to high-incidence areas — available at private clinics.

Q: Can meningitis be treated?
A: Bacterial meningitis requires emergency hospitalisation and intravenous antibiotics — every hour of delay increases disability and death risk. Viral meningitis is usually managed with supportive care. If meningitis is suspected — do not wait — go to emergency department or call 112.
