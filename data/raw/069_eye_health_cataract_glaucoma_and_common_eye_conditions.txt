--- OVERVIEW ---
India bears approximately 20% of the global blindness burden. As per NPCB data, there are an estimated 15 million blind individuals in India, of whom ~7 million are blind due to cataract alone — a largely preventable cause of blindness. Glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy, age-related macular degeneration, and refractive errors are leading causes of visual impairment. Most eye conditions are treatable or preventable with timely intervention; however, awareness, access, and affordability remain challenges.

--- CATARACT ---

Definition: A cataract is clouding of the eye's natural lens (located behind the iris and pupil), leading to blurred, hazy, or dimmed vision. It is the leading cause of blindness in India and the world.

Types:
- Age-related (senile) cataract: Most common; develops slowly with age (typically after 55)
- Congenital cataract: Present at birth; requires early treatment to prevent amblyopia (lazy eye)
- Secondary cataract: Due to other eye conditions (glaucoma, uveitis) or systemic disease (diabetes)
- Traumatic cataract: Following eye injury

Symptoms:
- Cloudy, blurry, or foggy vision
- Reduced night vision; glare and haloes around lights (especially headlights at night)
- Fading or yellowing of colours
- Frequent changes in spectacle/contact lens prescription
- Double vision in one eye
- Need for brighter light to read

Risk Factors:
- Age (most important)
- Diabetes
- Prolonged UV (sunlight) exposure without protection
- Smoking and alcohol use
- Prolonged use of corticosteroids (tablets or eye drops)
- Family history
- Previous eye injury or surgery
- Malnutrition (Vitamin C and antioxidant deficiency)

Prevention:
- Use UV-protective sunglasses outdoors
- Control blood sugar (in diabetics)
- Quit smoking
- Balanced diet rich in antioxidants (leafy greens, fruits, Vitamin C, E, lutein, zeaxanthin)
- Regular eye check-ups after age 40

Treatment:
- The only effective treatment is surgical removal of the cloudy lens and replacement with an artificial intraocular lens (IOL)
- Cataract surgery is one of the most successful, safe, and cost-effective surgeries in medicine
- Small incision cataract surgery (SICS) and phacoemulsification are standard techniques
- Recovery typically takes days to weeks with good visual outcomes
- In India: Free cataract surgery available at government hospitals and NPCB-supported camps

--- GLAUCOMA ---

Definition: A group of eye diseases causing progressive damage to the optic nerve, typically associated with elevated intraocular pressure (IOP), leading to gradual and irreversible loss of vision, usually starting with peripheral (side) vision. It is the leading cause of irreversible blindness globally.

Types:
1. Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG): Most common; painless and asymptomatic until advanced; pressure builds up gradually. Most common type globally.
2. Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma: Sudden, painful rise in eye pressure — EMERGENCY. Rapid vision loss, severe eye pain, headache, nausea, halos around lights.
3. Normal-Tension Glaucoma: Optic nerve damage despite normal IOP.
4. Secondary Glaucoma: Results from other eye conditions (trauma, steroid use, diabetes, uveitis).
5. Congenital Glaucoma: Present at birth; often detected by enlarged, cloudy eyes in infants.

Symptoms:
POAG (early — often none):
- Gradual loss of peripheral (side) vision
- Tunnel vision in advanced stages
- Often discovered only on routine eye examination

Acute Angle-Closure:
- Sudden severe eye pain
- Blurred vision and halos
- Headache, nausea, vomiting
- Redness of the eye

IMPORTANT: POAG is called the "silent thief of sight" because it has no symptoms until significant vision is lost. Regular eye pressure checks and optic nerve examination are essential.

Risk Factors:
- Age above 40
- Family history of glaucoma
- High intraocular pressure
- Thin central cornea
- High myopia (short-sightedness)
- Diabetes and hypertension
- Prolonged steroid use (eye drops, tablets) — a major preventable cause in India
- African/Asian ethnicity

Prevention:
- No proven way to prevent glaucoma, but early detection prevents blindness
- Do not use steroid eye drops without medical prescription — self-use of steroid drops for "red eye" is a major cause of steroid-induced glaucoma in India
- Regular eye check-ups after age 40, especially if risk factors are present

Treatment (Awareness Only):
- Medicated eye drops to reduce IOP — require lifelong use; must not be self-discontinued
- Laser treatment (trabeculoplasty or iridotomy)
- Surgery (trabeculectomy) in advanced cases
- Vision lost to glaucoma CANNOT be recovered — treatment prevents further loss

--- OTHER COMMON EYE CONDITIONS ---

Refractive Errors (Myopia, Hypermetropia, Astigmatism):
- Most common cause of visual impairment globally; easily corrected with spectacles, contact lenses, or refractive surgery
- India has hundreds of millions of uncorrected refractive errors — a major avoidable cause of vision loss
- Free spectacles available under NPCB school vision screening programmes

Diabetic Retinopathy:
- Progressive damage to retinal blood vessels due to poorly controlled diabetes
- No symptoms in early stages; can progress to blindness
- ALL diabetics should have an annual dilated retinal examination
- Controlled blood sugar, blood pressure, and lipids slow progression
- Laser photocoagulation and intravitreal injections (under specialist care) can prevent vision loss

Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD):
- Degeneration of the macula (central retina) causing loss of central vision
- Risk: Age, smoking, family history
- Wet AMD (treatable with injections); Dry AMD (no proven treatment; antioxidant supplements may slow progression)

Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye):
- Inflammation of the conjunctiva; causes redness, discharge, itching
- Viral: Self-limiting; highly contagious — avoid touching eyes, share no towels/pillowcases
- Bacterial: May need antibiotic eye drops (doctor prescribed)
- Allergic: Antihistamine drops; avoid triggers

Dry Eye Syndrome:
- Insufficient quantity or quality of tears; causes burning, grittiness, blurred vision
- Common with screen use, air conditioning, aging
- Artificial tear drops (lubricant eye drops) — many available OTC; consult optometrist/ophthalmologist for persistent symptoms

--- VISION HYGIENE AND SCREEN USE ---
- Follow 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes of screen use, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds
- Maintain adequate screen brightness and distance (arm's length)
- Use anti-glare screen protectors if needed
- Blink consciously while using screens
- Adequate lighting while reading

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR (EYE DOCTOR / OPHTHALMOLOGIST) ---
- Any sudden vision loss — emergency
- Severe eye pain — emergency
- Flashes of light or sudden increase in floaters — urgent (possible retinal detachment)
- Red eye with pain and light sensitivity
- Gradual blurring of vision, especially if progressive
- Halos around lights
- Difficulty driving at night
- Annual dilated eye exam for ALL diabetics
- Routine check at age 40 (even without symptoms) — especially with family history of glaucoma
- Children with squint, white reflex, or not following objects

--- GOVERNMENT PROGRAMMES ---
- National Programme for Control of Blindness and Visual Impairment (NPCB+VI): Free cataract surgeries, spectacles distribution, school vision screening, and support to eye banks
- District Blindness Control Societies (DBCS): Coordinate free eye camps and surgeries at district level
- Vision 2020: The Right to Sight — India: National initiative to eliminate avoidable blindness
- Free eye check-ups and surgeries at government medical college hospitals, district hospitals, and approved NGO/private partners

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Do not use any eye drops (especially steroid eye drops) without a doctor's prescription. For any sudden change in vision, severe eye pain, or injury — seek emergency eye care immediately. Regular eye examinations are the most important tool for detecting and preventing eye disease.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is cataract surgery free in India?
A: Yes. Cataract surgery is available free at all government district hospitals and medical colleges under the National Programme for Control of Blindness and Visual Impairment (NPCBVI). Contact your nearest government hospital eye department. Do not delay â cataract is curable.

Q: How do I know if I have glaucoma?
A: Glaucoma is called the silent thief of sight â it usually has NO symptoms until significant vision is lost. Risk factors: age over 40, family history, diabetes, hypertension. The only way to detect it early is through a regular eye pressure check and optic nerve examination by an ophthalmologist.

Q: Can using screens too much permanently damage my eyes?
A: Prolonged screen use causes eye strain (dry eyes, headache, blurred vision) but does not cause permanent damage. Follow the 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Blink regularly and ensure good screen ergonomics.

Q: My child squints one eye. Is it serious?
A: Yes â any squinting in a child needs prompt evaluation by an eye specialist (paediatric ophthalmologist). Untreated squint can cause amblyopia (lazy eye) with permanent vision loss in that eye. Early treatment (glasses, patching, surgery) prevents permanent damage.
