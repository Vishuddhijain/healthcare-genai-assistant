--- OVERVIEW ---
Dracunculiasis, also called Guinea-worm disease, is a parasitic disease transmitted through contaminated drinking water, causing a painful blister from which a worm slowly emerges. It results from ingesting the parasite Dracunculus medinensis . Although it rarely causes death, infected individuals are often incapacitated for weeks or months. This disease predominantly affects rural, underserved, and remote populations that rely on open, stagnant water sources (e.g., ponds) for drinking-water.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Dracunculiasis is a crippling parasitic disease on the verge of eradication, with 13 human cases reported in 2024 (provisional) and 14 in 2023.
- From the time infection occurs, it takes 10–14 months for the transmission cycle to complete, at which point a mature female worm emerges from the body.
- Transmission mostly involves drinking stagnant water contaminated by parasite-infected water fleas.
- Dracunculiasis was endemic in 20 countries (1) in the mid-1980s.

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- During the mid-1980s, approximately 3.5 million cases of dracunculiasis occurred in 20 countries worldwide, 17 located in Africa and the remaining 3 in Asia. Subsequent interventions reduced reported cases to fewer than 10 000 in 2007, then further down to 542 in 2012 and 126 in 2014. Since 2015, annual human cases have remained in double digits, declining from 22 in 2015 to 13 (provisional) in 2024. In that same year, 12 villages in Chad (7 cases) and South Sudan (6 cases) accounted for all known human infections. --- PREVENTION ---
- There is no vaccine or cure for dracunculiasis, but prevention and surveillance measures have brought the disease to the brink of eradication. Essential approaches include:
- Safe water access : Drilling protected wells or boreholes, promoting the use of cloth or pipe filters in the absence of safe drinking water, and discouraging contact with contaminated sources when worms emerge.
- Vector control : Regularly applying temephos (Abate®) to stagnant water to kill infected water fleas.
- Community education : Highlighting the importance of early case detection and reporting, alongside preventing animals from contaminating water, and proper management of aquatic animals’ waste.
- Interrupting transmission in animals requires enhanced surveillance, early containment of infected animals, proactive tethering, community education, and rigorous vector control.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is guinea worm disease present in India?
A: Guinea worm disease has been eradicated from India â India was certified guinea worm-free in 2000. It persists only in a handful of countries in sub-Saharan Africa. Global eradication efforts are ongoing and near-complete.

Q: How was guinea worm eliminated?
A: Eradication was achieved without any vaccine or medicine â solely through behaviour change (filtering drinking water) and surveillance. This makes guinea worm eradication one of the greatest public health achievements driven by community-level intervention.

Q: What are the symptoms of guinea worm disease?
A: One year after infection, a slow-growing blister forms on the skin (usually the foot or leg) as a long worm (up to 1 metre) emerges over weeks. The process is extremely painful and can cause secondary infections leading to disability.

Q: Can guinea worm come back to India?
A: Risk is very low but not zero given global travel. Maintaining drinking water safety and surveillance for imported cases is important. Any unusual skin lesion with a worm-like structure in a traveller from endemic areas should be reported to public health authorities.
