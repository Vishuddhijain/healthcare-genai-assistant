--- OVERVIEW ---
Obesity is a complex chronic condition defined by excessive body fat accumulation that poses a risk to health. India is the third most obese country in the world, with over 135 million obese individuals. India is undergoing a rapid "nutrition transition" — rising incomes, urbanisation, sedentary jobs, and ultra-processed food consumption have driven a dramatic increase in obesity alongside persistent undernutrition. Obesity is a major risk factor for heart disease, type 2 diabetes, certain cancers, hypertension, fatty liver disease, and sleep apnoea.

--- DEFINITIONS ---
- Overweight (BMI ≥ 23 kg/m² for Indians; global cut-off ≥ 25 kg/m²): Excess body weight for height
- Obesity (BMI ≥ 25 kg/m² for Indians; global cut-off ≥ 30 kg/m²): Significant excess fat
- NOTE: Indian and South Asian populations develop metabolic complications at lower BMI than Western populations — hence Indian-specific cut-offs are used
- Central (abdominal) obesity: More dangerous metabolically; defined as waist circumference >90 cm in Indian men, >80 cm in Indian women
- Body Mass Index (BMI) = weight (kg) ÷ height (m)²

--- ASSOCIATED LIFESTYLE DISEASES ---
Obesity increases risk of and often co-exists with:
1. Type 2 Diabetes: Insulin resistance driven by excess adipose tissue (especially visceral/abdominal fat)
2. Hypertension (high blood pressure): Extra body mass increases cardiac workload
3. Cardiovascular disease: Atherosclerosis, heart attack, stroke
4. Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD): Excess fat deposited in liver; can progress to cirrhosis
5. Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA): Excess tissue around airway causes breathing pauses during sleep
6. Dyslipidaemia: Elevated LDL, triglycerides; reduced HDL
7. Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS): Hormonal disorder affecting women; worsened by obesity
8. Certain cancers: Breast (post-menopausal), colon, endometrium, kidney, oesophageal
9. Osteoarthritis: Excess weight damages knee, hip, and lower back joints
10. Psychological impacts: Depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, social stigma

--- SYMPTOMS / SIGNS ---
Obesity itself:
- Increased body weight and BMI (above thresholds)
- Large waist circumference
- Shortness of breath on mild exertion
- Excessive sweating
- Fatigue and low energy
- Snoring or sleep disturbances
- Joint pain (knees, hips, lower back)
- Skin problems (skin folds, heat rash, fungal infections in skin folds)

Metabolic complications to watch for:
- Polyuria, polydipsia, fatigue (diabetes)
- Headaches, dizziness (hypertension)
- Chest pain or palpitations (cardiovascular)
- Fatigue, right upper abdominal discomfort (fatty liver)
- Excessive daytime sleepiness, witnessed apnoea (sleep apnoea)
- Irregular or absent periods, acne, facial hair (PCOS)

--- RISK FACTORS FOR OBESITY ---
Dietary:
- High consumption of ultra-processed foods (packaged snacks, instant noodles, fast food)
- Excessive sugar-sweetened beverages (sodas, packaged juices, chai with excess sugar)
- Large portion sizes
- Eating out frequently
- High consumption of refined carbohydrates (white rice, maida-based foods)

Activity:
- Sedentary occupation (desk work, IT sector)
- Screen time (TV, mobile, computer)
- Lack of physical activity or structured exercise
- Urban lifestyle with reduced incidental activity

Other factors:
- Genetic predisposition
- Hormonal conditions (hypothyroidism, Cushing's syndrome, PCOS)
- Certain medications (steroids, antidepressants, antipsychotics)
- Sleep deprivation (disrupts hunger hormones)
- Stress (increases cortisol and comfort eating)
- Childhood obesity (strong predictor of adult obesity)

--- PREVENTION AND MANAGEMENT ---
Diet:
- Eat a balanced diet: Plenty of vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes (dal, chana, rajma), lean protein
- Reduce refined carbohydrates and added sugars
- Limit ultra-processed and packaged foods
- Reduce consumption of sugar-sweetened beverages; drink water
- Control portion sizes — use smaller plates
- Limit eating out; prefer home-cooked meals
- Eat mindfully — slowly, without screens
- Traditional Indian foods (ragi, jowar, bajra rotis; dal; sabzi) are generally healthier than highly processed alternatives

Physical Activity:
- Adults: At least 150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week (brisk walking, cycling, swimming)
- Muscle-strengthening activities (bodyweight, yoga, resistance training): 2 or more days per week
- Reduce sedentary time: Take breaks from sitting every hour; walk when possible
- Children: At least 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity daily

Behaviour and Lifestyle:
- Sleep 7–9 hours per night
- Manage stress (yoga, meditation, physical activity, social connection)
- Set realistic weight loss goals (0.5–1 kg per week is sustainable)
- Seek support from family, community, or a registered dietitian/healthcare provider

Medical Management (doctor supervised):
- Dietary and physical activity counselling
- Pharmacotherapy: Certain medications available for obesity management under medical supervision
- Bariatric surgery: For severe obesity (BMI ≥32.5 kg/m² with metabolic disease in Indians) unresponsive to lifestyle changes — specialist decision

--- WAIST-TO-HEIGHT RATIO ---
Simple, widely validated screening tool:
- Waist circumference should be less than half of height
- Example: Person 160 cm tall should have waist under 80 cm

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- BMI above 23 kg/m² (overweight) or 25 kg/m² (obese) — Indian cut-offs
- Waist circumference above Indian cut-offs
- Symptoms of diabetes, hypertension, or other associated conditions
- Struggling to lose weight despite lifestyle changes
- Irregular periods, hair loss, or acne in women (PCOS)
- Excessive daytime sleepiness (sleep apnoea)
- Family history of metabolic disease

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- National Programme for NCD Prevention and Control (NPNCD): Free BMI/waist measurement and metabolic screening at government HWCs
- Eat Right India campaign (FSSAI): fssai.gov.in/eat-right-india
- Fit India Movement: fitindia.gov.in — promoting physical activity and lifestyle change
- Food safety labelling: FSSAI mandates nutritional labelling on packaged foods; reading labels helps make healthier choices

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Obesity is a medical condition — not a personal failure. Do not pursue crash diets or extreme weight loss measures without medical supervision, as they can be harmful. Always consult a qualified healthcare provider or registered dietitian for personalised guidance.
