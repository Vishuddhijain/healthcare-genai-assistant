--- OVERVIEW ---
Mental health is a state of well-being in which every individual realises their own potential, can cope with normal stresses of life, can work productively and fruitfully, and is able to make a contribution to their community. It is an integral part of health — there is no health without mental health. Mental health conditions are common, treatable, and nothing to be ashamed of.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- 1 in 8 people globally live with a mental disorder.
- Depression and anxiety are among the most common conditions.
- In India, the National Mental Health Survey (2016) found that about 10.6% of adults have a mental health condition.
- Suicide is the second leading cause of death among 15–29 year olds globally.
- 75% of people with mental health conditions in low and middle income countries receive no treatment.
- Mental health conditions are treatable — early support makes a significant difference.

--- COMMON MENTAL HEALTH CONDITIONS ---

1. DEPRESSION:
- Persistent sadness, loss of interest or pleasure in activities
- Changes in appetite and sleep
- Difficulty concentrating
- Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
- In severe cases, thoughts of self-harm or suicide
- Depression is NOT just sadness — it is a medical condition and is treatable.

2. ANXIETY DISORDERS:
- Persistent, excessive worry or fear
- Restlessness, racing heart, difficulty breathing
- Avoiding certain situations due to fear
- Includes panic disorder, social anxiety, phobias, and generalised anxiety disorder

3. Stress (Work / Academic / Life):
- Feeling overwhelmed or unable to cope
- Irritability, difficulty sleeping, difficulty concentrating
- Physical symptoms: headache, muscle tension, stomach issues
- Manageable with lifestyle changes and support

--- SIGNS SOMEONE MAY NEED MENTAL HEALTH SUPPORT ---
- Prolonged feelings of sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness
- Withdrawing from friends, family, or activities they used to enjoy
- Significant changes in sleep, appetite, or energy
- Difficulty performing daily tasks (school, work, self-care)
- Increased use of alcohol or substances
- Talking about wanting to die or harming oneself

--- WHAT TO DO ---
1. Talk to someone you trust — a friend, family member, teacher, or mentor.
2. Seek help from a mental health professional: psychiatrist, psychologist, or counsellor.
3. Government hospitals have free psychiatric outpatient services.
4. iCall helpline (TISS): 9152987821 — free counselling for students and young adults.
5. Vandrevala Foundation Helpline: 1860-2662-345 (24/7, free, pan-India).
6. NIMHANS (Bengaluru) offers specialised mental health services: nimhans.ac.in.

--- HOW TO SUPPORT A FRIEND ---
- Listen without judging
- Avoid saying "just cheer up" or "others have it worse"
- Encourage them to seek professional help
- Stay in touch and check in regularly
- In crisis situations, do not leave them alone — contact emergency services (112)

--- PREVENTIVE CARE FOR MENTAL HEALTH ---
- Regular physical activity — even 30 minutes of walking improves mood
- Adequate sleep (7-9 hours for adults)
- Healthy diet — reduces anxiety and improves mood
- Limiting alcohol and avoiding drugs
- Maintaining social connections
- Setting realistic goals and managing workload
- Practicing mindfulness, yoga, or meditation

--- MENTAL HEALTH IN INDIA: GOVERNMENT SCHEME ---
The National Mental Health Programme (NMHP) aims to ensure availability and accessibility of minimum mental health care for all in the foreseeable future.
District Mental Health Programme (DMHP) provides community mental health services at the district level including awareness, early detection, and treatment.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: How do I know if I need mental health help?
A: Consider seeking help if: you feel persistently sad, anxious, or empty for more than 2 weeks; you are unable to function at work, school, or in relationships; you are using alcohol or substances to cope; you have thoughts of harming yourself; or someone close to you has expressed concern about your mental health.

Q: Is taking mental health medication shameful?
A: Absolutely not. Mental health medication corrects chemical imbalances in the brain — just as insulin corrects diabetes or antihypertensives control blood pressure. Needing medication for mental health is not weakness or something to be ashamed of. Follow your doctor's advice.

Q: Can yoga and meditation replace mental health treatment?
A: Yoga, meditation, and mindfulness are valuable complementary tools that reduce stress and improve mental wellbeing. However, they cannot replace professional treatment for clinical conditions like severe depression, bipolar disorder, or schizophrenia. Use them alongside, not instead of, professional care.

Q: How can I support a family member with a mental health condition?
A: Listen without judgment, educate yourself about their condition, encourage and accompany them to professional help, maintain regular contact, do not dismiss their experience as weakness. Avoid saying 'just cheer up' or 'it's all in your mind.' Family support is a key factor in recovery.
