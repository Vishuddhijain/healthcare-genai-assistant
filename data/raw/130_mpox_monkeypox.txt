TOPIC: Mpox (Monkeypox)
CATEGORY: Communicable Disease / Zoonotic
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/mpox | MoHFW India | NCDC
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Mpox (formerly called monkeypox) is a viral disease caused by the monkeypox virus, a member of the Orthopoxvirus genus (same family as smallpox). It was first identified in humans in 1970 in the Democratic Republic of Congo. In 2022, a global outbreak of mpox occurred across multiple countries that had not previously reported cases. In 2024, a new Clade Ib variant caused another outbreak centred in Africa, prompting WHO to declare mpox a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) again. India has reported mpox cases and maintains active surveillance.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Mpox is caused by monkeypox virus; two clades (genetic groups): Clade I (Central Africa — more severe) and Clade II (West Africa — less severe; caused 2022 global outbreak).
- The disease is usually self-limiting but can be severe, especially in children, pregnant women, and immunocompromised individuals.
- Case fatality rate: ~3–6% with Clade I; <1% with Clade II in non-endemic settings.
- Transmission: Through close physical contact with infected person, animal, or contaminated materials.
- NOT primarily airborne — close skin-to-skin contact is the main route.
- India detected its first mpox case in 2022; has maintained surveillance since.
- Mpox vaccination is available; smallpox vaccines offer some cross-protection.
- The name was changed from "monkeypox" to "mpox" by WHO in 2022 to avoid stigma.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Incubation period: 5–21 days (usually 6–13 days)

Phase 1 — Prodrome (first 1–5 days):
- Fever (sudden onset)
- Intense headache
- Swollen lymph nodes (lymphadenopathy — a KEY distinguishing feature from smallpox and chickenpox)
- Muscle aches (myalgia)
- Back pain
- Fatigue
- Chills

Phase 2 — Rash (begins 1–3 days after fever):
- Rash typically starts on face, then spreads to arms, legs, palms, and soles
- Rash progression: Flat spots (macules) → raised bumps (papules) → fluid-filled blisters (vesicles) → pus-filled lesions (pustules) → scabs → healing
- Lesions are deep-seated, firm, and all at the same stage in the same area (unlike chickenpox)
- Lesions may also appear in mouth, genitals, eyes, and anal region
- Duration: Rash typically lasts 2–4 weeks

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
Seek medical attention immediately if:
- Severe rash spreading rapidly
- Difficulty swallowing or breathing (lesions in throat)
- Eye pain or vision changes (ocular involvement)
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Signs of secondary bacterial infection (increasing redness, warmth, pus, pain)
- Symptoms in children under 8, pregnant women, or immunocompromised individuals (higher risk of severe disease)
- Rash on genitals or anal area — also see a doctor confidentially

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Close physical contact with a confirmed mpox case
- Direct contact with skin lesions, rash, scabs, or body fluids of infected person
- Contact with contaminated bedding, towels, clothing
- Healthcare workers without appropriate PPE
- People with multiple sexual partners (in 2022 outbreak, most cases were among men who have sex with men — MSM — though mpox is NOT exclusively an STI)
- Unvaccinated younger generations (smallpox vaccination — which offered cross-protection — was discontinued after 1980 global eradication)
- Hunters/bushmeat handlers in endemic regions (animal-to-human transmission)
- Immunocompromised individuals (HIV+, cancer patients)
- Children in areas with Clade I (endemic Africa)

--- TRANSMISSION ---
Person-to-person:
- Close physical/skin-to-skin contact with rash, lesions, or scabs
- Contact with body fluids (saliva, respiratory secretions during prolonged face-to-face contact)
- Handling contaminated materials (bedding, clothing)
- During 2022 outbreak: sexual contact was a major mode among adults

Animal-to-person:
- Bites or scratches from infected animals (rodents, primates)
- Handling/eating undercooked meat of infected animals (bushmeat)
- NOT primarily airborne — but respiratory droplet transmission during prolonged close contact is possible

Does NOT spread via:
- Casual contact (passing someone in a hallway)
- Touching unaffected skin of infected person
- Through water

--- PREVENTION ---
1. Avoid close skin contact with anyone who has mpox symptoms (rash, lesions)
2. Avoid contact with wild animals, especially rodents and primates in endemic regions
3. Wash hands frequently with soap and water
4. Use appropriate PPE when caring for mpox patients
5. Safe sex practices (use condoms, reduce number of partners) during outbreaks
6. Do not share bedding, towels, or clothing with a known case
7. Cook all animal products (especially meat) thoroughly
8. Vaccination: Recommended for high-risk contacts and healthcare workers

--- VACCINATION ---
- Smallpox vaccines (ACAM2000, JYNNEOS/Imvanex/Imvamune): Provide cross-protection against mpox
- JYNNEOS (Bavarian Nordic): Specifically licensed for mpox prevention; safer (non-replicating virus)
- India: Government has procured/evaluated mpox vaccines for high-risk groups and healthcare workers
- Vaccination recommended as post-exposure prophylaxis within 4 days of exposure
- Not universally available at present — consult NCDC or state health authorities

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- PCR testing of skin lesion swabs (vesicle fluid, crust, or lesion roof): Most reliable
- Blood tests (PCR from blood in severe cases)
- All suspected cases in India to be reported to state health authority and NCDC
- Testing available at NCDC Delhi, NIV Pune, and designated state labs
- Do NOT self-diagnose — multiple conditions can cause skin rashes; clinical + lab confirmation required

--- TREATMENT (AWARENESS ONLY) ---
- Most cases resolve on their own within 2–4 weeks with supportive care
- Supportive treatment: Keep rash clean and dry, pain management, maintain hydration
- Antiviral tecovirimat (TPOXX): Used in severe cases; under WHO access protocols
- Secondary bacterial skin infections: Treated with antibiotics if confirmed
- Isolation is important to prevent transmission while symptomatic

--- ISOLATION GUIDELINES ---
- Isolate at home until all skin lesions have healed completely and new skin has formed
- Avoid close contact with family members — use separate rooms if possible
- Cover all lesions if sharing space
- Avoid contact with pets (animals can be infected)
- Wash bedding and clothing separately with hot water

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Unexplained rash with fever, especially if you have had recent contact with a confirmed or suspected mpox case
- Rash that looks like clustered blisters at same stage of development
- Return from a country with active mpox outbreak
- Rash on genitals with or without lymph node swelling
- Any child or pregnant woman with unexplained blistering rash

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- India reported its first mpox case in July 2022 (Kerala)
- NCDC (National Centre for Disease Control) coordinates surveillance; IDSP collects data
- MoHFW issued guidelines for surveillance, diagnosis, and management
- Designated isolation facilities at AIIMS, government medical colleges
- Helpline: State health helplines + NCDC for case reporting guidance
- India does not have endemic mpox; all cases have been travel-related or contact-traced
- Stigma: Mpox is NOT a disease only of any specific group; anyone with close contact with a case is at risk
- Report suspected mpox cases to the National Centre for Disease Control (NCDC): 011-23921401. National Health Helpline: 104. Emergency: 112.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is mpox the same as smallpox?
A: No. Mpox is related but distinct. Smallpox was eradicated in 1980. Mpox is milder than smallpox. However, swollen lymph nodes (not seen in smallpox) help differentiate mpox.

Q: Is mpox only transmitted sexually?
A: No. While sexual contact was a major transmission route in the 2022 global outbreak, mpox can spread through any close skin-to-skin contact, contaminated materials, or animal bites. It is not classified as a sexually transmitted infection (STI) exclusively.

Q: Can I get mpox from my pet?
A: Animals can in theory be infected by mpox from humans or the environment. Avoid contact with your pets while you are symptomatic with mpox.

Q: How is mpox different from chickenpox?
A: Mpox: Lymph node swelling, deep lesions all at same stage, rash starts on face before body, palms and soles affected. Chickenpox: No lymph node swelling, lesions at different stages, starts on trunk, rarely on palms/soles.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
Mpox is usually mild but can be serious. If you have an unexplained rash with fever, consult a doctor promptly and mention any potential exposures. Isolation helps prevent spread. India has established surveillance — do not hide symptoms. All care at government hospitals is free for confirmed cases. Do not stigmatise affected individuals.
