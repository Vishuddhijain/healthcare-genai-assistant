TOPIC: Falls and Unintentional Injuries
CATEGORY: Injury Prevention / Public Health
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/falls
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Falls are the second leading cause of unintentional injury deaths globally (after road traffic injuries). They are especially dangerous for elderly and young children. The majority of fall-related injuries and deaths are preventable. Road traffic injuries, drowning, burns, and poisoning are other major causes of unintentional injury in India.

--- KEY FACTS ON FALLS ---
- An estimated 684,000 fatal falls occur globally each year.
- 37.3 million non-fatal falls requiring medical attention occur annually.
- Adults over 60 years suffer the greatest number of fatal falls.
- Children under 5 and adults over 65 are at highest risk.
- In India, falls are a leading cause of disability in the elderly.
- Fall-related hip fractures in elderly are associated with significant mortality within 1 year.

--- WHO IS AT RISK ---
High risk groups:
- Elderly (over 65): Reduced balance, muscle weakness, vision problems, medications causing dizziness
- Young children: Developmental curiosity + limited awareness of danger
- People with neurological conditions (Parkinson's, stroke, epilepsy)
- People with vision or hearing impairment
- People taking certain medications (sedatives, blood pressure drugs, diuretics)
- People with osteoporosis (fractures from minor falls)

--- PREVENTING FALLS IN ELDERLY ---
Home modifications:
- Install grab bars near toilet and in bathroom (most falls happen in bathroom)
- Use non-slip mats in bathroom and kitchen
- Ensure all areas are well-lit, especially pathways to bathroom at night
- Remove loose rugs, clutter, cords from walkways
- Keep frequently used items at accessible height (avoid climbing)
- Install railings on all staircases

Lifestyle:
- Regular exercise: balance and strength training (yoga, tai chi, walking)
- Vitamin D and calcium supplementation (improves bone and muscle health — consult doctor)
- Eye check-ups: uncorrected vision significantly increases fall risk
- Hearing aids if needed
- Footwear: wear non-slip, well-fitting shoes; avoid walking barefoot on smooth floors
- Review medications with doctor — reduce polypharmacy where possible
- Use walking aids (stick, walker) if doctor recommends

--- PREVENTING FALLS IN CHILDREN ---
- Never leave infants unattended on high surfaces (tables, beds, changing tables)
- Install safety gates at staircase tops and bottoms
- Use window guards — falls from windows are a serious cause of child injury
- Supervise children on playground equipment
- Teach children safe behaviour around edges, stairs, and climbing
- Use properly fitted helmets for cycling, skating, sports

--- ROAD TRAFFIC INJURIES ---
India has one of the highest road traffic fatality rates in the world.
- 1.5 lakh people die in road accidents in India every year.
- Young people (15–29 years) are most affected.

Prevention:
- Always wear a helmet on two-wheelers (reduces head injury risk by 70%)
- Always wear a seatbelt in cars (front and back seats)
- Never use a mobile phone while driving — it increases accident risk by 4 times
- Never drink and drive
- Follow speed limits
- Obey traffic signals
- Ensure vehicle is roadworthy
- Children under 12 should sit in the back seat

--- DROWNING ---
Drowning is a leading cause of unintentional injury death in India, especially in children.
- Most drownings occur in natural bodies of water (ponds, rivers, wells) — especially in rural India.
- Children under 5 are at highest risk.

Prevention:
- Supervise children near all water bodies at all times
- Cover or fence wells and ponds near homes
- Teach children to swim at an early age
- Never swim alone
- Avoid alcohol near water
- Use life jackets in boats

First aid for drowning:
- Remove person from water safely (don't put yourself at risk)
- Call 112 immediately
- Begin CPR if person is not breathing and you are trained

--- BURNS AND FIRE SAFETY ---
Burns are particularly common in Indian households due to cooking on open flames, LPG accidents, and kerosene use.
- Children and women cooking on open stoves are at highest risk.

Prevention:
- Keep children away from kitchen during cooking
- Do not leave cooking unattended
- Keep flammable materials away from cooking areas
- Check LPG connections for leaks regularly
- Install a fire extinguisher in kitchen if possible
- Keep a bucket of sand or water nearby for small fires

First aid for burns: (see 09_first_aid.txt)
- Cool with running water for 10 minutes
- Never apply ice, butter, or toothpaste

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: What should I do if an elderly person falls?
A: Don't rush to move them. Ask if they are in pain. If they cannot get up or complain of hip/back pain, do not try to lift them — call 112. If they can get up safely with help, move slowly and carefully.

Q: Are walking sticks/walkers really necessary?
A: If a doctor recommends one, yes — absolutely. Pride about using a walking aid is not worth a broken hip. Walking aids significantly reduce fall risk.

Q: What is the most important thing to prevent falls in elderly at home?
A: Bathroom safety (grab bars + non-slip mat) and removing trip hazards (rugs, clutter) are the highest-impact interventions.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
Fall injury prevention is about awareness and action. For injuries involving suspected fractures, head injuries, or inability to bear weight — call 112 and do not move the person unnecessarily. This information is for awareness and prevention only.
