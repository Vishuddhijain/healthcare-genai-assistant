TOPIC: Epilepsy
CATEGORY: Non-Communicable Disease / Neurological

--- OVERVIEW ---
Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder characterised by recurrent, unprovoked seizures. A seizure is a sudden, uncontrolled burst of electrical activity in the brain that temporarily affects movement, behaviour, sensation, or consciousness. Epilepsy is one of the most common neurological conditions globally. It is manageable — 70% of people with epilepsy can live seizure-free with proper treatment.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Epilepsy affects approximately 50 million people globally — one of the most common neurological diseases.
- In India, approximately 12 million people have epilepsy — one of the highest burdens globally.
- 80% of people with epilepsy live in low- and middle-income countries.
- Up to 70% of people with epilepsy could live seizure-free if diagnosed and treated.
- In India, significant treatment gap exists — only 15–40% of people with epilepsy receive treatment.
- Epilepsy is associated with significant stigma — leading to social exclusion, lack of education and employment.
- Three in four people with epilepsy in low-income countries receive no treatment at all.

--- TYPES OF SEIZURES ---
1. Focal (partial) seizures: Originate in one area of the brain.
   - Focal aware: Person remains conscious. May experience unusual sensations, emotions, or automatic movements.
   - Focal impaired awareness: Consciousness affected. Person may stare blankly, make repetitive movements, be unresponsive.

2. Generalised seizures: Involve entire brain.
   - Tonic-clonic (grand mal): Most recognisable — loss of consciousness, stiffening (tonic phase), then jerking (clonic phase). May last 1–3 minutes.
   - Absence (petit mal): Brief staring, loss of awareness for a few seconds. Common in children.
   - Atonic (drop attacks): Sudden loss of muscle tone — person falls.
   - Myoclonic: Brief, sudden jerks of limbs.

--- CAUSES ---
In many cases, the cause is unknown. Known causes include:
- Brain injuries (head trauma, birth injury)
- Infections: Meningitis, encephalitis, neurocysticercosis (tapeworm in brain — very common cause in India, linked to eating undercooked pork)
- Stroke (leading cause in adults over 35)
- Brain tumours
- Genetic conditions
- Developmental problems (in children)
- Neurocysticercosis is estimated to cause 30% of epilepsy cases in India

--- FIRST AID FOR A SEIZURE ---
What TO DO:
- Stay calm and stay with the person
- Note the time the seizure starts
- Clear away nearby hard or sharp objects to prevent injury
- Cushion the person's head with something soft
- Gently turn the person onto their side if possible (recovery position) — prevents choking
- Time the seizure — if it lasts more than 5 minutes, call 112
- Once the seizure ends, stay with the person as they recover (may be confused/drowsy — this is normal)

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT hold the person down or restrain them
- Do NOT put anything in their mouth — it is impossible to swallow your tongue during a seizure
- Do NOT give water or medicines during the seizure
- Do NOT leave the person alone until fully recovered

Call 112 if:
- Seizure lasts more than 5 minutes
- Person does not regain consciousness after seizure ends
- Person is injured during the seizure
- Person is pregnant
- Person does not have a known epilepsy diagnosis
- This is the person's first seizure

--- TREATMENT ---
- Anti-epileptic drugs (AEDs): First-line treatment. Prescribed by a neurologist. Control seizures in ~70% of people.
- AEDs must be taken every day as prescribed — missing doses can trigger seizures.
- Many AEDs are available free at government hospitals.
- Surgery: For selected patients where seizures originate from a specific brain area.
- Ketogenic diet: High-fat, low-carbohydrate diet used in children with drug-resistant epilepsy (under medical supervision only).
- Never stop AEDs without doctor's advice — sudden stopping can cause life-threatening status epilepticus.

--- LIVING WITH EPILEPSY ---
Safety precautions:
- Do not swim alone or in open water
- Do not cook alone over open flames until seizure-free for significant time
- Avoid operating heavy machinery if seizures are not fully controlled
- Do not drive until seizure-free for the period specified by law (1 year in India)
- Inform close contacts about epilepsy and first aid

Triggers to avoid (in susceptible individuals):
- Sleep deprivation
- Missed medication
- Alcohol and drug use
- Flashing lights (in photosensitive epilepsy)
- Fever
- Stress

--- STIGMA AND RIGHTS ---
- Epilepsy carries serious social stigma in India, particularly in rural areas — false beliefs that it is contagious or caused by evil spirits.
- Epilepsy is NOT contagious and is NOT caused by supernatural forces.
- The Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act (RPWD 2016) protects people with epilepsy in India.
- People with well-controlled epilepsy can attend school, work, and lead normal lives.

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC ---
- National Programme for Treatment of Epilepsy: Provides free AEDs at government hospitals.
- National Trust: Supports persons with neurological conditions including epilepsy.
- Epilepsy India: epilepsyindia.org — information and support.
- Neurocysticercosis prevention: Cook pork thoroughly, wash vegetables, improve sanitation.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is epilepsy hereditary?
A: Some forms of epilepsy have genetic components, but most cases are not directly inherited. Having epilepsy in the family does not mean you will get it.

Q: Can a person die from a seizure?
A: Most seizures are not life-threatening. However, prolonged seizures (status epilepticus) and SUDEP (Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy) are rare but real risks. Proper treatment significantly reduces these risks.

Q: Can children with epilepsy go to school?
A: Yes. With proper seizure control, children with epilepsy should attend mainstream schools. Teachers should be informed about first aid for seizures.

Q: Is it true you shouldn't put a spoon in someone's mouth during a seizure?
A: Correct — NEVER put anything in someone's mouth during a seizure. The "swallowing tongue" belief is a myth. Putting objects in the mouth causes injury.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
Epilepsy treatment requires a qualified neurologist. Never start, stop, or change AEDs without medical advice. For a prolonged seizure or first-time seizure, call 112 immediately.
