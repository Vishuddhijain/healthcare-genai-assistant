--- OVERVIEW ---
Despite significant progress over the past two decades, India still accounts for a substantial share of global under-5 child deaths. The three leading causes of death in children under 5 in India are pneumonia, diarrhoeal diseases, and malnutrition (which underlies ~45% of all child deaths globally). These three conditions are closely linked — malnutrition weakens immunity, increasing susceptibility to infection; infections worsen nutritional status; diarrhoea and pneumonia together account for over a third of under-5 deaths globally.

--- SECTION 1: DIARRHOEA IN CHILDREN ---

Definition: Passage of 3 or more loose or watery stools per day. Acute diarrhoea lasts <14 days; persistent diarrhoea lasts 14 days or more; dysentery involves blood in stool.

Common Causes:
- Rotavirus (most common cause of severe diarrhoea in young children)
- E. coli, Shigella, Salmonella (bacterial)
- Cryptosporidium, Giardia (parasitic)
- Contaminated water or food
- Poor hygiene and sanitation

Dehydration — Key Danger:
Diarrhoea kills primarily by dehydration. Recognise signs:
- Mild: Thirsty, slightly less active
- Moderate: Dry mouth, sunken eyes, reduced skin elasticity (skin pinch goes back slowly), less urine, no tears when crying, irritable
- Severe: Very sunken eyes, very dry mouth, skin pinch goes back very slowly, unable to drink, lethargic/unconscious — MEDICAL EMERGENCY

ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution):
- First-line treatment for preventing and treating dehydration from diarrhoea
- WHO/UNICEF low-osmolarity ORS: 1 packet in 1 litre of clean water
- If ORS packet unavailable: Home-made solution — 1 litre clean water + 6 level teaspoons sugar + half level teaspoon salt
- Give sips frequently; do NOT stop feeds — continue breastfeeding

Zinc Supplementation:
- Zinc 20 mg/day for 14 days (10 mg/day for infants under 6 months) — reduces diarrhoea duration and severity and prevents recurrence
- Government health centres provide ORS and zinc free under IMNCI programme

When to Seek Medical Help (diarrhoea):
- Blood in stool
- High fever
- Persistent vomiting — cannot keep ORS down
- Not improving after 2–3 days
- Signs of moderate or severe dehydration
- Child is very young (<6 months)

Prevention of Diarrhoea:
- Exclusive breastfeeding for first 6 months (protects against infections)
- Safe drinking water (boiled or purified)
- Hand washing with soap — before preparing food, before feeding child, after using toilet/changing nappies
- Safe food handling and storage
- Rotavirus vaccine: Available under UIP (Universal Immunisation Programme) in India — 2 or 3 doses at 6–10–14 weeks
- Improved sanitation and use of toilets (Swachh Bharat Mission)

--- SECTION 2: PNEUMONIA IN CHILDREN ---

Definition: Infection of the lungs (alveoli) caused by bacteria, viruses, or fungi. Bacterial pneumonia (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae) is most severe. Viral pneumonia (RSV, influenza) is more common.

Symptoms:
- Fast breathing (most important sign): >60 breaths/min in infants <2 months; >50 in 2–11 months; >40 in 1–5 years
- Chest in-drawing (lower chest wall draws inward with each breath)
- Wheezing
- High fever
- Cough
- Difficulty feeding or lethargy
- Cyanosis (bluish lips/nails) — EMERGENCY

Classification (WHO):
- Pneumonia: Fast breathing, no chest in-drawing → treat with oral antibiotics (amoxicillin first line)
- Severe pneumonia: Chest in-drawing → hospital admission required
- Very severe: Cyanosis, unable to feed, convulsions → emergency

Prevention:
- Vaccination: Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine (PCV) — under UIP phase-in; protects against S. pneumoniae (leading bacterial cause)
- Hib vaccine (Pentavalent vaccine): Protects against H. influenzae type b
- Exclusive breastfeeding (first 6 months)
- Reduce indoor air pollution (biomass fuel smoke)
- Adequate nutrition (malnutrition increases pneumonia risk significantly)
- Zinc supplementation (reduces pneumonia incidence in children)
- Hand washing with soap

When to Seek Medical Help (pneumonia):
- Fast breathing
- Chest in-drawing
- Difficulty breathing or noisy breathing
- High persistent fever
- Unable to drink/feed
- Lethargy or drowsiness
- Any of above in infant <2 months — seek care urgently

--- SECTION 3: MALNUTRITION IN CHILDREN ---

Definitions:
- Stunting: Low height-for-age (chronic undernutrition over a long period) — 35% of Indian children under 5 are stunted (NFHS-5)
- Wasting: Low weight-for-height (acute undernutrition) — 19% of Indian children under 5 are wasted
- Underweight: Low weight-for-age — 32% of Indian children under 5 are underweight
- Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM): Very low weight-for-height with or without oedema; life-threatening
- Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM): Moderate weight-for-height deficit
- Micronutrient deficiency: Lacks Vitamin A, iron, zinc, iodine — "hidden hunger"

Signs of Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM):
- Visible severe wasting (very thin arms and legs, prominent ribs)
- Bilateral pitting oedema (swelling in both feet/legs — sign of kwashiorkor)
- MUAC (Mid-Upper Arm Circumference) < 11.5 cm (red zone) — screening tool used by health workers
- Very poor appetite
- Marasmus: Extreme wasting, very low weight, wrinkled skin
- Kwashiorkor: Oedema, swollen belly, skin lesions, hair changes

Causes of Malnutrition in India:
- Inadequate dietary intake (quality and quantity)
- Poor infant and young child feeding practices
- Recurrent infections (diarrhoea, pneumonia) creating vicious cycle
- Poverty and food insecurity
- Maternal malnutrition (low birth weight babies)
- Poor WASH (Water, Sanitation, Hygiene) practices
- Early marriage and adolescent pregnancy

Prevention:
- Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months; continued breastfeeding up to 2 years
- Timely complementary feeding from 6 months (nutritious semi-solid foods alongside breastfeeding)
- Dietary diversity (cereals, pulses, vegetables, fruits, dairy/animal foods)
- Vitamin A supplementation (Vitamin A National Supplementation Programme — doses every 6 months for children 9 months–5 years)
- Iron and Folic Acid (IFA) supplementation — through ICDS and health system
- Deworming: National Deworming Day (NDD) — albendazole twice yearly for children 1–19 years
- Growth monitoring at Anganwadi centres (monthly weighing)

SAM Management:
- Therapeutic feeding at Nutrition Rehabilitation Centres (NRCs) — located in government district hospitals and CHCs
- Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF): Peanut-based energy-dense product for SAM management at home
- Treatment of infections accompanying SAM

--- GOVERNMENT PROGRAMMES ---
- ICDS (Integrated Child Development Services): Anganwadi centres provide supplementary nutrition, growth monitoring, immunisation, health check-ups, and referrals
- POSHAN Abhiyaan (National Nutrition Mission): Targets reduction of stunting, underweight, anaemia, and low birth weight
- MAA Programme (Mothers Absolute Affection): Promotes breastfeeding
- NRC (Nutrition Rehabilitation Centres): SAM management at government hospitals
- Universal Immunisation Programme (UIP): Rotavirus, PCV, Pentavalent (Hib), Measles-Rubella, BCG, IPV, OPV vaccines — free at government centres

Helplines:
- POSHAN Abhiyaan portal: poshan.gov.in
- Child Helpline: 1098 (Childline India)
- ICDS via Anganwadi: Reach nearest Anganwadi centre

--- WHEN TO SEEK MEDICAL HELP ---
- Diarrhoea with signs of dehydration or blood in stool
- Fast breathing, chest in-drawing, or difficulty breathing
- Child unable to eat/drink or is extremely lethargic
- Child looks severely thin or has bilateral leg swelling
- Child has MUAC less than 11.5 cm (red tape on MUAC measuring tape)
- Persistent fever (>3 days)
- Any serious illness in an infant under 2 months

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Diarrhoea, pneumonia, and malnutrition in children — especially infants — can become life-threatening rapidly. Always seek timely care from a qualified healthcare provider. Do not delay if warning signs are present. Free care is available at government Primary Health Centres, Community Health Centres, and district hospitals.
