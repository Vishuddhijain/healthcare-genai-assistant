TOPIC: Fragility Fractures and Osteoporosis
CATEGORY: Non-Communicable Disease / Bone Health / Musculoskeletal
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/fragility-fractures | International Osteoporosis Foundation
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
A fragility fracture is a bone fracture that occurs from minimal trauma — a fall from standing height or less — that would not normally break a healthy bone. Fragility fractures are primarily caused by osteoporosis (low bone density). They are a major public health concern globally and in India, especially among older adults and post-menopausal women. Hip fractures — the most severe fragility fracture — are associated with significant disability and death.

--- TYPES ---
Common fragility fracture sites:
1. Hip fracture: Most serious — often requires surgery; 20–30% of patients die within 1 year; 50% never regain previous mobility.
2. Vertebral (spine) fracture: Most common fragility fracture — may cause height loss, back pain, stooped posture (kyphosis/dowager's hump); many are silent (no pain).
3. Wrist (Colles') fracture: Common after outstretched hand fall; often the first sign of osteoporosis.
4. Humerus (upper arm) fracture.
5. Pelvis fracture.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Worldwide, 1 in 3 women and 1 in 5 men over age 50 will experience an osteoporotic fracture.
- In India, an estimated 50 million people have osteoporosis.
- A hip fracture occurs approximately every 3 seconds globally.
- Indian women develop osteoporosis at a younger age than Western women — partly due to lower calcium intake and Vitamin D deficiency.
- Once one fragility fracture has occurred, the risk of a second fracture doubles.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Fragility fractures:
- Sudden pain after minor fall or movement
- Inability to bear weight (hip, spine, leg fractures)
- Visible deformity at fracture site
- Swelling and bruising

Silent vertebral fractures (often unnoticed):
- Gradual height loss (>2 cm over time)
- Progressive back pain
- Stooped posture (kyphosis)
- Reduced rib-to-hip distance

Osteoporosis itself has NO symptoms — it is only detected by bone density testing or after a fracture.

--- RISK FACTORS ---
Non-modifiable:
- Age >50 (especially post-menopausal women)
- Female sex (bone loss accelerates after menopause)
- Family history of hip fracture or osteoporosis
- Ethnic factors (South Asians have lower bone density on average)
- Previous fragility fracture

Modifiable:
- Low calcium and Vitamin D intake
- Physical inactivity / sedentary lifestyle
- Tobacco use (reduces bone density)
- Excessive alcohol use
- Prolonged use of steroids (corticosteroids) — major drug-induced cause
- Low body weight / undernutrition
- Falls risk (see 40_falls_injury.txt)

Medical conditions increasing risk:
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Hyperparathyroidism
- Coeliac disease
- Chronic kidney disease
- Hyperthyroidism

--- PREVENTION ---
Building bone density (most effective in youth — bone bank concept):
- Adequate calcium: Adults need 1000–1200 mg/day. Sources: milk, curd, paneer, ragi, sesame, fish with bones.
- Adequate Vitamin D: 600–800 IU/day. Sources: 15–20 min morning sunlight, oily fish, fortified milk, eggs. Supplements if deficient (doctor-advised).
- Regular weight-bearing exercise: Walking, jogging, dancing, yoga — builds bone strength.
- Resistance training: Improves muscle strength and bone density.
- Quit smoking and limit alcohol.
- Avoid prolonged steroid use (only with medical supervision).
- Fall prevention in elderly (see 40_falls_injury.txt) — since most fragility fractures result from falls.

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- Home modifications to prevent falls (grab bars, non-slip mats, adequate lighting) are critical.
- Ensure elderly family members have regular bone density checks.
- Community awareness to reduce "normal ageing" misconception — fractures are not inevitable.

--- VACCINATION ---
No specific vaccine for osteoporosis. However:
- Influenza and pneumococcal vaccines reduce hospitalisations that lead to immobility and bone loss.

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- DXA (Dual-energy X-ray Absorptiometry) scan: Gold standard for bone density measurement; T-score below -2.5 = osteoporosis.
- FRAX score: WHO tool to calculate 10-year fracture risk (available online at shef.ac.uk/FRAX).
- Blood tests: Calcium, Vitamin D, parathyroid hormone, thyroid function (to rule out secondary causes).
- X-ray: Detects fractures; may show vertebral compression.
DXA scanning available at government medical colleges and district hospitals in India.

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- Calcium and Vitamin D supplementation (doctor-prescribed)
- Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate) — reduce fracture risk; doctor-prescribed
- Denosumab, teriparatide — for severe osteoporosis; specialist-prescribed
- Hip fracture: Usually requires surgical fixation (hemiarthroplasty or total hip replacement) — available at government hospitals
- Post-fracture rehabilitation: Physiotherapy is essential for recovery
- Liaison Fracture Service: Hospitals identify osteoporosis after any fragility fracture and initiate treatment

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
- Sudden, severe back pain in a person over 60 — possible vertebral fracture
- Pain and inability to bear weight after a minor fall — possible hip fracture
- Progressive height loss and stooped posture — possible vertebral fractures

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Women at menopause — bone density check
- Anyone over 50 with a fracture from minimal trauma — needs osteoporosis workup
- Long-term steroid users — regular bone density monitoring
- Any person with height loss >2 cm or new back pain after 60
- Hip/spine fracture: Emergency — call 112, do not move the person

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Vitamin D deficiency is almost universal in India despite abundant sunshine — largely due to indoor lifestyles, clothing, and skin pigmentation.
- Calcium intake in India is far below recommended levels — average Indian diet provides only 300–400 mg/day vs. recommended 1000+ mg.
- DXA scanning is available in government medical college hospitals; private sector availability has improved.
- Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY covers hip fracture surgery at empanelled hospitals for eligible patients.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is osteoporosis only a problem for women?
A: No. 1 in 5 men over 50 will have an osteoporotic fracture. Men are often underdiagnosed because osteoporosis is mistakenly seen as a women's disease.

Q: Can I reverse osteoporosis?
A: Bone density can be increased modestly with treatment and lifestyle changes. Significant reversal is limited, but treatment can prevent further loss and substantially reduce fracture risk.

Q: Does milk really help bones?
A: Yes. Dairy is the richest dietary calcium source. Adequate calcium from youth builds peak bone mass. However, Vitamin D is equally important for calcium absorption — often deficient in India.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This is awareness information only. Osteoporosis diagnosis and treatment require medical supervision. Hip and spine fractures are emergencies — call 112 and do not attempt to move the person without medical guidance.
