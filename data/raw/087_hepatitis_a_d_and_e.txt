--- OVERVIEW ---
Viral hepatitis infections A, D, and E are distinct from Hepatitis B and C (covered in 42_hepatitis_bc.txt). All cause liver inflammation but differ in transmission, severity, and management. Hepatitis A and E are primarily waterborne/foodborne and usually self-limiting. Hepatitis D only occurs in people already infected with Hepatitis B and can cause severe disease.

---
--- HEPATITIS A ---

--- TYPES ---
Hepatitis A virus (HAV): Single type. Fecal-oral transmission. Usually self-limiting (no chronic form).

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Hepatitis A affects approximately 1.5 million people clinically each year globally.
- India has a high prevalence — most Indians are exposed in childhood (often asymptomatically).
- Hepatitis A is vaccine-preventable.
- In areas with poor sanitation, children are commonly infected early and develop lifelong immunity.
- Hepatitis A does NOT cause chronic liver disease.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Incubation: 14–28 days after exposure.
- Fever
- Malaise (feeling unwell)
- Loss of appetite
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain (right upper quadrant)
- Dark urine (tea-coloured)
- Jaundice — yellowing of skin and eyes (appears 1–2 weeks after first symptoms)
- Clay-coloured stool
Children under 6: Often have no symptoms. Older children and adults typically have more severe symptoms.
Fulminant hepatitis A (rare but serious): Rapid liver failure — requires hospitalisation.

--- TRANSMISSION ---
- Faecal-oral route: Contaminated water or food
- Raw or undercooked shellfish from contaminated water
- Close contact with infected person
- Poor hand hygiene after toilet use

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Drinking untreated water or water from unknown sources
- Eating raw shellfish (oysters, clams) from polluted water
- Poor sanitation and hygiene
- Travelling to endemic areas without vaccination
- People who inject drugs

--- PREVENTION ---
- Safe water: Drink boiled or treated water; avoid ice of unknown origin.
- Food safety: Wash hands; cook shellfish thoroughly; avoid raw food from unknown sources.
- Handwashing: Wash hands with soap after toilet and before meals.
- Vaccination: Hepatitis A vaccine (2-dose schedule) highly effective. Recommended for travellers to endemic areas, children in high-risk areas, people with chronic liver disease. Available at private clinics; selective availability at government hospitals.

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- Outbreak control: Report hepatitis A clusters to local health authorities.
- Sewage treatment and clean water supply — the long-term solution.

--- VACCINATION ---
- Hepatitis A vaccine: 2 doses (0 and 6–12 months). Highly effective (>94% protection). Available at private vaccination centres in India.
- Post-exposure prophylaxis: Hepatitis A vaccine within 2 weeks of exposure may prevent illness.

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- Blood test: Anti-HAV IgM antibody (confirms acute infection).
- Liver function tests: Elevated bilirubin and liver enzymes.

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- No specific antiviral treatment for Hepatitis A.
- Supportive care: Rest, adequate nutrition, fluids.
- Avoid alcohol and hepatotoxic medications.
- Most patients recover fully within 2 months.
- Hospitalisation for severe cases or fulminant hepatitis.

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
- Prolonged jaundice (more than 4 weeks)
- Severe vomiting, inability to eat or drink
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Severe abdominal pain
→ These may indicate fulminant hepatitis — seek emergency care.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Any yellowing of eyes or skin (jaundice) — especially with fever and dark urine
- Dark urine without fever — possible hepatitis
- Worsening symptoms after initial diagnosis

--- HEPATITIS D ---

--- OVERVIEW ---
Hepatitis D (delta hepatitis) is caused by the Hepatitis D virus (HDV), a defective virus that requires Hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate. HDV only infects people who already have Hepatitis B.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Approximately 5% of people with Hepatitis B are co-infected with HDV — about 15 million people globally.
- HDV co-infection accelerates liver disease — dramatically increases risk of cirrhosis and liver cancer.
- Preventing Hepatitis B (through vaccination) also prevents Hepatitis D.

--- TRANSMISSION ---
- Same routes as Hepatitis B: Blood, sexual contact, mother to child.

--- PREVENTION ---
- Hepatitis B vaccination prevents both HBV and HDV infection — the Hepatitis B vaccine is free under India's UIP.
- Hepatitis B-infected individuals: Avoid sharing needles, use condoms, do not share personal items.

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- Pegylated interferon alpha (limited effectiveness) — specialist management at government hospitals.
- Prevention through HBV vaccination remains the key approach.

--- HEPATITIS E ---

--- OVERVIEW ---
Hepatitis E is caused by the Hepatitis E virus (HEV). It is primarily waterborne, particularly common after floods and in areas with contaminated water supplies. It is usually self-limiting but poses serious risk to pregnant women.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Approximately 20 million HEV infections occur annually; 44,000 deaths.
- India has the second highest Hepatitis E burden globally.
- India regularly experiences Hepatitis E outbreaks — commonly after floods or water supply contamination.
- Hepatitis E is PARTICULARLY DANGEROUS IN PREGNANCY — maternal mortality can reach 20–25% in third trimester.
- No chronic form in immunocompetent individuals (unlike B and C).

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Same as Hepatitis A: fever, jaundice, dark urine, abdominal pain. Incubation: 2–9 weeks.

--- TRANSMISSION ---
- Drinking contaminated water (primary route in India)
- Eating undercooked pork or wild boar (zoonotic strains in developed countries)
- Person to person (less common)

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Floods and natural disasters contaminating water supply
- Poor sanitation
- Pregnancy (high risk of severe disease)

--- PREVENTION ---
- Safe drinking water — boil or treat water, especially after floods.
- Good sanitation and hand hygiene.
- Vaccination: An HEV vaccine (HEV 239) is available in China but not yet globally licensed. Not available in India currently.
- Post-flood health precautions: Only drink boiled/treated water; avoid street food.

--- WARNING SIGNS (especially in pregnancy) ---
- Jaundice in a pregnant woman — emergency — seek immediate care.
- Hepatitis E in pregnancy can cause acute liver failure with very high mortality.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Any jaundice — especially in pregnant women or after flood/water contamination exposure.
- Hepatitis E outbreaks: Report to local health authorities for investigation of water supply.

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Hepatitis E outbreaks are commonly reported after floods in India (Bihar, Assam, Odisha, etc.).
- NVHCP (National Viral Hepatitis Control Programme) includes Hepatitis A and E surveillance.
- Post-flood public health response always includes hepatitis surveillance and safe water provision.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: How is Hepatitis A different from Hepatitis E — both seem the same?
A: Both are waterborne and cause jaundice. Key differences: Hepatitis E is especially dangerous in pregnancy (very high mortality); Hepatitis A vaccine exists and is effective; Hepatitis A is most common in children in India while Hepatitis E affects all ages.

Q: Can I get Hepatitis D without Hepatitis B?
A: No. Hepatitis D virus cannot replicate without Hepatitis B. Getting vaccinated against Hepatitis B fully prevents Hepatitis D.

Q: I was jaundiced 2 years ago. Am I immune to Hepatitis A now?
A: If confirmed Hepatitis A infection, you likely have lifelong immunity to Hepatitis A specifically. You are not immune to other forms of hepatitis. Blood test for anti-HAV IgG confirms past immunity.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
Jaundice (yellow eyes/skin), dark urine, and fever require prompt medical evaluation. Hepatitis E in pregnancy is a medical emergency. Seek care immediately. This information is for awareness only.

--- TREATMENT (AWARENESS ONLY) ---
- Treatment details are for awareness only. Consult a qualified healthcare professional.
