--- OVERVIEW ---
Safe drinking water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) are fundamental to public health. Lack of access to safe water and adequate sanitation is a root cause of numerous communicable diseases — diarrhoea, typhoid, cholera, hepatitis A, polio, leptospirosis, and worm infestations. In India, water-borne diseases remain a leading cause of illness and death, especially among children. India has made significant progress through Swachh Bharat Mission (ODF status) and Jal Jeevan Mission (piped water), but gaps remain — particularly in ensuring safe water quality, sustained sanitation practices, and hygiene behaviour.

--- WATERBORNE DISEASES LINKED TO UNSAFE WATER/SANITATION ---
- Diarrhoeal diseases (cholera, E. coli, Shigella, rotavirus)
- Typhoid and paratyphoid fever (Salmonella typhi)
- Hepatitis A (fecal-oral route)
- Polio (fecal-oral; near-elimination in India, but vigilance needed)
- Leptospirosis (contact with water contaminated by animal urine)
- Intestinal worm infestations (Ascaris, hookworm, whipworm — soil-transmitted helminths)
- Cryptosporidiosis and Giardiasis
- Fluorosis (endemic in high-fluoride groundwater areas)
- Arsenicosis (arsenic contamination in groundwater — West Bengal, Bihar, Jharkhand, UP, Assam)

--- SAFE DRINKING WATER ---

Water Safety Hierarchy (in order of reliability):
1. Piped, treated municipal supply that is regularly tested (ideal)
2. Borewell/handpump water tested for safety
3. Packaged bottled water (safety depends on brand and storage)
4. Household-level treatment (if above options unavailable)

Household Water Treatment (Point-of-Use):
When safe piped water is unavailable, the following methods make water safe:

a) Boiling:
- Bring water to a rolling boil for 1 minute (at altitudes above 2000m, boil for 3 minutes)
- Most effective against bacteria, viruses, and parasites
- Allow to cool naturally; store in clean, covered container
- Does NOT remove chemical contamination (fluoride, arsenic, nitrates)

b) Chlorination:
- Add 2 drops of household bleach (5% chlorine) per litre of clear water; 4 drops if turbid
- Wait 30 minutes before drinking
- Effective against bacteria and viruses; less effective against Cryptosporidium
- Free chlorination tablets sometimes distributed by government health workers

c) Filtration:
- Ceramic candle filters, biosand filters: Remove bacteria and turbidity; require cleaning and maintenance
- RO (Reverse Osmosis) filters: Remove dissolved salts, heavy metals (fluoride, arsenic, nitrates), and microbes; water quality depends on membrane maintenance
- UV purifiers: Effective against microbes; does not remove chemical contaminants
- Combination (UF + UV + activated carbon): Best for comprehensive treatment

d) Solar Disinfection (SODIS):
- Fill clear PET bottles with clear water; expose to direct sunlight for 6 hours (or 2 days if cloudy)
- Low-cost; effective for E. coli and most waterborne pathogens

Storage of treated water:
- Store in a clean, narrow-mouth, covered container
- Do not dip hands/utensils into the container; use a clean ladle or tap
- Use treated water within 24 hours (boiled water) to 3 days (chlorinated)

Key contaminants in Indian groundwater to be aware of:
- Fluoride: Rajasthan, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Gujarat, Karnataka, UP, MP — causes fluorosis
- Arsenic: West Bengal, Bihar, Jharkhand, UP, Assam, Manipur — causes arsenicosis (skin changes, cancer risk)
- Nitrates: Agricultural areas with heavy fertiliser use — causes methaemoglobinaemia in infants ("blue baby syndrome")
- Iron: Common in groundwater — not directly harmful but affects taste and promotes microbial growth
- If in doubt about water quality: Get water tested at a government PHED/water quality testing lab

--- SANITATION ---

Open Defecation Free (ODF):
- Open defecation (OD) contaminates water sources, soil, and food — a major fecal-oral disease pathway
- Swachh Bharat Mission (SBM) declared India ODF in 2019; SBM 2.0 focuses on sustainability, ODF-Plus, and solid/liquid waste management
- Use of household or community toilets is essential; never defecate near water sources

Safe Sanitation Practices:
- Use a toilet or latrine for defecation
- Safely dispose of child faeces (diapers, wrap in plastic, use toilet/latrine)
- Keep toilets clean; regular disinfection reduces disease transmission
- Ensure no animal waste near water sources or cooking areas

Wastewater and Drainage:
- Ensure wastewater from kitchens and bathrooms drains away from drinking water sources
- Do not allow stagnant water near homes (mosquito breeding + water contamination risk)

--- HAND HYGIENE ---
Hand washing with soap is the single most effective personal hygiene practice to prevent infection.

Critical times to wash hands:
1. Before preparing food
2. Before eating
3. Before feeding a child
4. After using the toilet
5. After cleaning a child's bottom or changing nappies
6. After handling animals or animal waste
7. After handling garbage
8. Before and after caring for a sick person

Correct hand washing technique (WHO 6-step method):
1. Wet hands with water
2. Apply enough soap to cover all surfaces
3. Rub palms together
4. Right palm over back of left hand and interlace fingers; repeat
5. Palm to palm, interlaced
6. Backs of fingers to opposing palms (interlocked)
7. Rotational rubbing of left thumb clasped in right palm; repeat
8. Rotational rubbing with clasped fingers of right hand in left palm; repeat
9. Rinse with water
10. Dry with clean cloth or air dry
Total hand washing time: At least 20–40 seconds

When soap is unavailable:
- Ash rubbing is traditionally used but less effective; soap is strongly preferred
- Alcohol-based hand sanitizer (60%+ alcohol) is effective but does not remove visible dirt or certain pathogens (Clostridioides difficile, norovirus)

--- FOOD SAFETY ---
WASH is closely linked to food safety — contaminated water and unhygienic handling are primary causes of foodborne illness.
- Wash raw vegetables and fruits with clean water
- Cook food thoroughly (especially meat, eggs, seafood)
- Avoid raw milk; use pasteurised or boiled milk
- Store cooked food safely; reheat thoroughly
- Avoid eating at stalls with visible hygiene issues (unwashed utensils, flies, stagnant water nearby)
- FSSAI guidelines: fssai.gov.in

--- MENSTRUAL HYGIENE ---
- Access to clean water and private sanitation is essential for menstrual hygiene management (MHM)
- Use clean, sanitary materials (disposable pads, cloth pads — if cloth, wash and dry in sun thoroughly)
- Wash hands before and after changing pads
- Dispose of used pads hygienically (wrap and bin; do not flush — blocks drains)
- Reusable menstrual cups and cloth pads are eco-friendly and cost-effective alternatives
- Government provides free sanitary napkins at Anganwadis and health centres in some states
- Kishori Scheme / Sabla Scheme: MHM education for adolescent girls in schools

--- GOVERNMENT PROGRAMMES ---
- Jal Jeevan Mission (JJM): jaljeevan.gov.in — piped tap water connection to every rural household by 2024
- Swachh Bharat Mission (SBM): swachhbharat.mygov.in — ODF and ODF-Plus; solid/liquid waste management
- Nal Se Jal: Urban water supply mission
- AMRUT (Atal Mission for Rejuvenation and Urban Transformation): Urban WASH infrastructure
- National Water Quality Sub-Mission: Testing and treatment of contaminated groundwater (fluoride, arsenic, heavy metals) in identified districts
- National Deworming Day (NDD): Annual albendazole deworming in schools linked to WASH promotion

Helplines:
- Jal Jeevan Mission: 1916 (water helpline in many states)
- Local Panchayat or PHC for water quality testing referral

--- WHEN TO SEEK MEDICAL HELP ---
- Diarrhoea, especially with blood or signs of dehydration
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes) — possible hepatitis A, leptospirosis
- Prolonged fever with abdominal symptoms — typhoid, leptospirosis
- Dental problems in areas with high fluoride (mottled teeth, joint pain — consider fluorosis)
- Skin changes, darkening, or nodules — possible arsenicosis in high-arsenic areas
- Persistent abdominal pain or worm segments in stool — helminthiasis

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Safe water and sanitation are rights, not luxuries. If your community lacks safe water access, contact your local Panchayat, PHC, or district PHED/water authority. For waterborne illness, seek medical care promptly — do not delay when dehydration or severe symptoms are present.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is tap water in Indian cities safe to drink directly?
A: In most Indian cities, municipal tap water is treated but not consistently safe to drink without further treatment at the point of use, due to distribution system contamination (pipe leaks, old infrastructure). Boiling or using an RO/UV purifier is recommended for drinking water in urban areas.

Q: How effective is boiling water for purification?
A: Boiling water for 1 minute kills all pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites). It is the most reliable, low-cost water purification method. Cool boiled water in a covered container to prevent recontamination. Boiling does not remove chemical contamination (fluoride, arsenic, heavy metals).

Q: Is ODF (Open Defecation Free) status verified in my village?
A: ODF status is verified through community surveys and government assessments under Swachh Bharat Mission. Check with your gram panchayat or district administration. ODF certification requires 100% of the population to use toilets — it is a community achievement, not just infrastructure.

Q: What is the Swachh Bharat Mission and what does it achieve for health?
A: Swachh Bharat Mission has constructed 100+ million toilets, dramatically reducing open defecation. Studies show it has significantly reduced diarrhoea, cholera, stunting in children, and maternal anaemia in rural India. Clean India directly means healthier India.
