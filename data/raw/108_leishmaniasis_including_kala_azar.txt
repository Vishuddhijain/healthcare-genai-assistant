TOPIC: Leishmaniasis (including Kala-Azar)
CATEGORY: Communicable Disease (Vector-Borne / Parasitic NTD)
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/leishmaniasis
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Leishmaniasis is caused by Leishmania parasites transmitted by infected female sandfly bites. India has one of the highest burdens of visceral leishmaniasis (kala-azar), endemic in Bihar, Jharkhand, West Bengal, and eastern UP. India, with Nepal and Bangladesh, is targeting kala-azar elimination (<1 case per 10,000 population per block).

--- TYPES ---
1. Visceral Leishmaniasis (Kala-Azar): Most severe; affects liver, spleen, bone marrow; fatal if untreated; caused by L. donovani in India
2. Cutaneous Leishmaniasis: Skin sores/ulcers; most common form globally
3. Mucocutaneous Leishmaniasis: Destroys mucous membranes of nose/mouth/throat
4. Post-Kala-Azar Dermal Leishmaniasis (PKDL): Skin rash after kala-azar treatment; acts as disease reservoir

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Kala-Azar (Visceral):
- Prolonged irregular fever (weeks to months)
- Massive splenomegaly and hepatomegaly
- Progressive weight loss, weakness, anaemia, low platelets (bleeding tendency)
- Darkening of skin ("kala-azar" = black fever)

Cutaneous: Painless or painful skin ulcers/nodules that heal slowly, leaving scars

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
- Prolonged fever (>2 weeks) with abdominal swelling (enlarged spleen) in endemic area
- Skin rash after completed kala-azar treatment (possible PKDL — needs re-treatment)

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Living in endemic districts (Bihar, Jharkhand, WB, eastern UP)
- Houses with cracked mud walls (sandfly breeding sites)
- Outdoor exposure at dusk/night without protection
- Malnutrition, HIV co-infection (increases risk and severity)

--- PREVENTION ---
- Indoor residual spraying (IRS) with DDT/synthetic pyrethroids
- Use of fine-mesh insecticide-treated bed nets (sandflies are tiny)
- Plastering cracked walls to eliminate sandfly breeding sites
- Avoid outdoor exposure at dusk and night in endemic areas
- Early detection and treatment of PKDL to remove disease reservoir

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- National Kala-Azar Elimination Programme: IRS campaigns in endemic blocks
- House improvement (plastering walls, eliminating cattle sheds attached to homes)
- Vector surveillance for Phlebotomus argentipes sandfly

--- VACCINATION ---
- No licensed vaccine currently available

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- rK39 rapid diagnostic test (antibody-based; widely used in India)
- Splenic/bone marrow aspirate microscopy (definitive but invasive)
- PCR for confirmation in select cases

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- Liposomal Amphotericin B: First-line; single-dose regimen; FREE at government health facilities in endemic states
- Miltefosine (oral): Alternative; specialist supervised
- PKDL: Extended treatment course required
- All treatment under National Kala-Azar Elimination Programme — free at PHC/CHC/district hospitals in endemic areas

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Fever lasting more than 2 weeks with abdominal swelling — get tested for kala-azar if in/from endemic area
- Non-healing skin ulcers after travel to endemic regions
- Skin rash following kala-azar treatment

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- National Kala-Azar Elimination Programme (NHM): Free diagnosis (rK39) and treatment (Liposomal Amphotericin B) at government facilities
- Endemic districts: Bihar (highest burden), Jharkhand, West Bengal, eastern UP
- India nearing elimination target in most blocks as of recent years
- PKDL surveillance is key to sustaining elimination

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Kala-azar treatment is free at government facilities in endemic states. Prolonged fever with abdominal swelling should be evaluated promptly — early treatment prevents fatality.
