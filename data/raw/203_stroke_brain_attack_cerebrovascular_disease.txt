TOPIC: Stroke (Brain Attack / Cerebrovascular Disease)
CATEGORY: Non-Communicable Disease / Neurological Emergency / Cardiovascular Disease
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/the-top-10-causes-of-death | NHP India | Indian Stroke Association | CDC
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
A stroke occurs when the blood supply to the brain is suddenly interrupted (ischaemic stroke) or when a blood vessel in the brain ruptures and bleeds (haemorrhagic stroke). Without blood, brain cells begin dying within minutes — making stroke one of the most time-critical medical emergencies. Stroke is the second leading cause of death and third leading cause of disability globally. In India, stroke accounts for approximately 7% of all deaths and is a leading cause of disability. The key message for stroke: ACT FAST — every second counts.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Stroke kills approximately 5.5 million people globally each year.
- In India, approximately 1.8 million new strokes occur each year; stroke is responsible for about 7% of all deaths.
- India has over 12 million stroke survivors — many with permanent disability.
- 80% of strokes are PREVENTABLE through controlling risk factors.
- A stroke occurs every 40 seconds in India.
- Time is brain: Each minute a large stroke goes untreated, about 1.9 million brain cells die.
- With clot-busting treatment (tPA/thrombolysis), outcomes dramatically improve IF given within 4.5 hours.
- India National Stroke Programme: Designated stroke centres in government hospitals.

--- TYPES OF STROKE ---
1. Ischaemic Stroke (most common, ~87% of all strokes):
   - Caused by a clot blocking a blood vessel in the brain
   - Two types:
     - Thrombotic: Clot forms directly in a brain artery (usually due to atherosclerosis)
     - Embolic: Clot forms elsewhere (heart, large artery) and travels to the brain
   - Treatable with clot-busting drugs (thrombolysis) within 4.5 hours

2. Haemorrhagic Stroke (~13% of strokes):
   - Caused by a ruptured blood vessel bleeding into the brain
   - Two types:
     - Intracerebral haemorrhage: Bleeding within brain tissue (most common; often due to hypertension)
     - Subarachnoid haemorrhage: Bleeding between brain and surrounding membrane (often due to aneurysm rupture)
   - Higher mortality than ischaemic stroke

3. Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA) — "Mini-Stroke":
   - Same symptoms as stroke but last only minutes to 24 hours; no permanent damage
   - WARNING: TIA is a major warning sign — 10–15% of TIA patients have a full stroke within 3 months
   - Requires IMMEDIATE medical evaluation to prevent major stroke

--- SYMPTOMS — USE THE F.A.S.T. TEST ---

F — FACE: Facial drooping or numbness — especially on one side; ask person to smile — does one side droop?
A — ARM: Arm weakness — is one arm weak or numb? Ask to raise both arms — does one drift downward?
S — SPEECH: Slurred, garbled, or inability to speak; or difficulty understanding speech
T — TIME: Call 112 IMMEDIATELY — time is critical

Additional stroke symptoms (BE-FAST adds these):
B — BALANCE: Sudden loss of balance, coordination, or difficulty walking
E — EYES: Sudden vision problems in one or both eyes; double vision; sudden blindness in one eye

Other symptoms:
- Sudden severe headache with no known cause ("thunderclap headache" — worst headache of your life) — sign of subarachnoid haemorrhage
- Sudden numbness or weakness of face, arm, or leg, especially on one side
- Sudden confusion
- Sudden dizziness

KEY POINT: ANY ONE of these symptoms appearing suddenly = stroke. Act immediately.

--- WARNING SIGNS --- 
Call 112 for ANY sudden onset:
- Facial droop (especially one-sided)
- Arm or leg weakness/paralysis (especially one-sided)
- Speech difficulty (slurred, garbled, or absent)
- Sudden severe headache (worst ever)
- Sudden vision loss or double vision
- Loss of balance or coordination

DO NOT WAIT: Stroke symptoms require immediate emergency response. Do NOT wait to see if symptoms improve. Do NOT drive yourself — call 112.

--- RISK FACTORS ---
Non-modifiable:
- Age: Risk doubles every 10 years after age 55
- Gender: Men have higher stroke rates; women live longer so more strokes in women overall
- Family history of stroke
- Previous stroke or TIA

Modifiable (can be controlled):
- Hypertension (high blood pressure): Single most important risk factor (causes 50%+ of strokes)
- Atrial fibrillation (irregular heartbeat): Increases stroke risk 5-fold
- Diabetes mellitus
- High cholesterol (dyslipidaemia)
- Smoking: Doubles stroke risk
- Obesity
- Physical inactivity
- Excessive alcohol use
- Illicit drug use (cocaine, amphetamines)
- Stress and depression (emerging evidence)

India-specific high-risk:
- Young strokes: India has unusually high rates of stroke in young adults (20–45 years) — linked to rheumatic heart disease, uncontrolled hypertension, and genetic factors

--- PREVENTION ---
PRIMARY PREVENTION (before a stroke):
1. Control blood pressure: Target <130/80 mmHg — take medicines as prescribed; monitor regularly
2. Manage diabetes: Keep HbA1c within target
3. Treat atrial fibrillation: Blood-thinning medications (anticoagulants) under doctor's guidance
4. Stop smoking: Risk of stroke falls dramatically after quitting
5. Eat healthy: Low salt (reduces BP), more fruits/vegetables, less saturated fat
6. Regular physical activity: 150 min/week moderate exercise
7. Limit alcohol: Heavy drinking raises BP and stroke risk
8. Maintain healthy weight

SECONDARY PREVENTION (after TIA or stroke):
- Antiplatelet drugs (aspirin, clopidogrel): Prescribed by doctors to prevent recurrence
- Anticoagulants: For atrial fibrillation-related stroke
- Blood pressure and cholesterol control
- Lifestyle changes (same as above, often more urgently needed)

--- TREATMENT (AWARENESS ONLY) ---
For Ischaemic Stroke:
- IV tPA (tissue plasminogen activator/thrombolysis): Clot-busting drug; must be given within 4.5 hours of symptom onset
- Mechanical thrombectomy: Catheter procedure to remove large clots; up to 24 hours window at specialised centres
- Requires CT scan to confirm type before treatment
- Aspirin (325 mg): Given within 48 hours if thrombolysis not possible

For Haemorrhagic Stroke:
- Blood pressure control
- Surgery: For some cases (clot evacuation, aneurysm clipping/coiling)
- Reversal of blood thinners if applicable

Post-stroke rehabilitation:
- Physiotherapy: Regain movement
- Speech therapy: For speech and swallowing problems
- Occupational therapy: Regain daily living skills
- Available at government hospitals and rehabilitation centres (NIMHANS, regional referral hospitals)

CRITICAL TIME WINDOWS:
- tPA: Must be given within 4.5 hours — ideally within 60 minutes of arriving at hospital
- Thrombectomy: Up to 24 hours at designated centres
- EVERY MINUTE MATTERS

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- IMMEDIATELY — call 112 for any sudden F.A.S.T. or BE-FAST symptoms
- Even if symptoms resolve (TIA) — go to hospital within hours; do not wait until next day
- Do not drive — call emergency services

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Indian Stroke Association: isastroke.com — guidelines, patient resources
- National Programme for Prevention and Control of NCDs (NP-NCD): Stroke prevention through BP, DM control
- Government hospitals increasingly equipped with stroke units and CT scanners in cities
- Golden hour/Time: The window for thrombolysis is often missed in India due to delayed presentation — public awareness critical
- Young stroke burden: India has one of highest rates of stroke in 20–45 age group globally (rheumatic heart disease, uncontrolled BP, drug use)
- Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY: Covers stroke treatment (thrombolysis and surgery) at empanelled hospitals
- AIIMS, PGI, state referral hospitals: Designated comprehensive stroke centres
- Telemedicine: Telestroke networks being developed in some states (Karnataka, Kerala, AP) to enable remote stroke diagnosis in rural areas
- Smoking + tobacco: Significant contributor in India — stopping tobacco is key stroke prevention

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: What is the difference between a stroke and a heart attack?
A: A heart attack occurs when blood supply to the heart muscle is blocked. A stroke occurs when blood supply to the brain is blocked or when a vessel in the brain ruptures. Both are medical emergencies but involve different organs.

Q: Can young people get strokes?
A: Yes. India has unusually high rates of stroke in young adults (20–45). Causes include rheumatic heart disease, uncontrolled blood pressure, smoking, illicit drug use, and genetic clotting disorders.

Q: If stroke symptoms go away, do I still need to go to hospital?
A: YES, absolutely. Temporary symptoms (TIA — transient ischaemic attack) are a warning that a major stroke may be imminent, often within hours or days. Immediate evaluation and treatment can prevent a larger stroke.

Q: Can stroke patients fully recover?
A: Recovery varies widely. Some recover fully; many have lasting disabilities. Early treatment (within 4.5 hours) dramatically improves recovery. Intensive rehabilitation (physiotherapy, speech therapy) helps maximise recovery.

Q: Does taking aspirin during a stroke help?
A: Do NOT self-administer aspirin during a suspected stroke without medical advice. If the stroke is haemorrhagic (bleeding), aspirin makes it worse. Only take aspirin if directed by emergency personnel or a doctor.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
Stroke is a medical emergency. Use F.A.S.T. (Face, Arms, Speech, Time) to recognise symptoms. Call 112 immediately — do not drive yourself or wait for symptoms to improve. The sooner treatment is given, the better the outcome. Stroke treatment is available at government hospitals. Stroke is largely preventable — control your blood pressure, don't smoke, and manage diabetes.
