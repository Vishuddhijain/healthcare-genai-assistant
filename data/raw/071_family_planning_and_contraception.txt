TOPIC: Family Planning and Contraception
CATEGORY: Reproductive Health
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/family-planning-contraception | MoHFW India
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Family planning allows people and couples to plan whether and when to have children. Access to safe, voluntary, and effective contraception is a fundamental human right and a key component of public health. In India, family planning services are provided free at all government health facilities. Unintended pregnancy is a major health risk — especially for young women and those with health conditions.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- An estimated 257 million women globally have an unmet need for family planning.
- Safe contraception prevents unintended pregnancies, reduces maternal mortality, and improves child health.
- India's National Family Planning Programme provides free contraceptives at all government health centres.
- Contraceptive use has increased significantly in India, but unmet need remains especially in rural areas.
- All methods have different effectiveness, benefits, and side effects — a healthcare provider should guide choice.

--- CONTRACEPTIVE METHODS (AWARENESS OVERVIEW) ---

BARRIER METHODS:
1. Male condom:
   - Also protects against STIs (the only contraceptive that does this)
   - Available FREE at government hospitals, PHCs, and through ASHA workers
   - Correct and consistent use is essential for effectiveness
   - Available also at pharmacies

2. Female condom:
   - Worn inside the vagina; also protects against STIs
   - Available at some government facilities

HORMONAL METHODS (require medical consultation):
3. Oral contraceptive pills (OCPs):
   - Taken daily; highly effective when taken correctly
   - Free at government PHCs
   - Not recommended for women who smoke and are over 35, or those with certain health conditions
   - Need medical guidance for appropriate type and dosage

4. Injectable contraceptive (DMPA/Antara):
   - Injection given every 3 months by a health worker
   - Free at government health centres under Antara Programme
   - No daily pill to remember; confidential

5. Implant (Implanon):
   - Small rod inserted under skin of upper arm; lasts 3 years
   - Highly effective; reversible
   - Available at selected government hospitals

INTRAUTERINE DEVICES (IUDs):
6. Copper-T (Cu-IUD):
   - Small T-shaped device placed inside the uterus by a trained provider
   - Highly effective for 5–10 years; reversible
   - FREE at government hospitals
   - Also works as emergency contraception if inserted within 5 days of unprotected sex

7. Hormonal IUS (Mirena):
   - Available in private sector

EMERGENCY CONTRACEPTION:
8. Emergency contraceptive pill (ECP):
   - Taken within 72 hours of unprotected sex (the sooner, the more effective)
   - Available over the counter at pharmacies (no prescription needed)
   - NOT a regular method of contraception — does not protect against STIs
   - Also known as "morning-after pill" or iPill/Unwanted 72

PERMANENT METHODS (Sterilisation):
9. Female sterilisation (tubectomy/laparoscopy):
   - Permanent; suitable for women who have completed their family
   - FREE at government hospitals; compensation provided under Mission Parivar Vikas
   
10. Male sterilisation (vasectomy):
    - Safe, simple, minor surgical procedure
    - FREE at government hospitals
    - Less invasive and faster recovery than female sterilisation
    - Significantly underutilised in India compared to female sterilisation

--- NATURAL METHODS ---
- Fertility awareness methods (tracking menstrual cycle): Require careful training; less reliable
- Not recommended as primary method for those who want to avoid pregnancy reliably

--- CHOOSING A METHOD ---
The right method depends on:
- Whether pregnancy is desired in the future (reversibility needed?)
- Frequency of sexual activity
- Health conditions and medications
- Tolerance of hormonal methods
- Whether STI protection is also needed (only condoms protect against STIs)
Consult an ASHA worker, ANM (Auxiliary Nurse Midwife), or doctor for personalised guidance.

--- MISSION PARIVAR VIKAS ---
India's flagship family planning programme for high-fertility districts:
- Free contraceptives at all levels of health system
- Cash incentive for sterilisation to compensate for loss of wages
- ASHA workers provide doorstep counselling and contraceptive supply

--- UNINTENDED PREGNANCY AND ABORTION ---
If pregnant unintentionally:
- Medical Termination of Pregnancy (MTP) Act 2021: Legal abortion in India up to 20 weeks (24 weeks in special cases — rape, fetal abnormality, disability).
- Safe abortion services are available at government hospitals — free for eligible patients.
- Unsafe abortions cause significant maternal mortality. Always use registered medical facilities.
- Consult a doctor promptly for unintended pregnancy.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- To choose a contraceptive method
- If experiencing side effects from a current method
- If missed pills or concerns about contraceptive failure
- Emergency contraception needed
- Planning pregnancy — pre-conception counselling

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is the morning-after pill an abortion pill?
A: No. Emergency contraceptive pills prevent fertilisation or implantation — they do not terminate an existing pregnancy. They are most effective when taken as soon as possible after unprotected sex.

Q: Does using contraception affect future fertility?
A: No. All reversible methods (pills, condoms, IUDs, injections) — fertility returns after stopping. Even after long-term use.

Q: Are condoms 100% effective?
A: No contraceptive is 100% effective. Condoms used correctly and consistently are about 98% effective. They are the only method that also protects against STIs.

Q: Is vasectomy better or worse than tubectomy?
A: Vasectomy is simpler, safer, faster, and has fewer complications. Both are equally effective. Men are encouraged to consider vasectomy — it should be a shared responsibility.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
All contraceptive methods, especially hormonal methods and IUDs, should be selected with guidance from a healthcare provider. This information is for awareness only. Free counselling and services are available at government health centres.
