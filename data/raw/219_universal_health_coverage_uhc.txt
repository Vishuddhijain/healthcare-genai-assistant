--- OVERVIEW ---
Universal health coverage (UHC) means that all people have access to the full range of quality health services they need, when and where they need them, without financial hardship. It covers the full continuum of essential health services, from health promotion to prevention, treatment, rehabilitation and palliative care across the life course.

Achieving UHC is one of the targets the nations of the world set when they adopted the 2030 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) in 2015. At the United Nations General Assembly High Level Meeting on UHC in 2019, countries reaffirmed that health is a precondition for and an outcome and indicator of the social, economic and environmental dimensions of sustainable development. In 2025, the United Nations Statistical Commission approved proposals for revisions of the SDG UHC indicators, as part of a comprehensive review of all SDG indicators. The revised global UHC monitoring framework uses the following two indicators: SDG indicator 3.8.1: UHC service coverage index and SDG indicator 3.8.2 : proportion of the population facing financial hardship in health.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Consult a qualified healthcare professional if symptoms are severe, persistent, recurrent, worsening, or affecting daily life.
- Seek urgent care for red-flag symptoms such as breathing difficulty, chest pain, heavy bleeding, severe dehydration, confusion, fainting, seizures, pregnancy danger signs, suspected poisoning/exposure, or signs of severe infection.
- For prevention, screening, vaccination, sexual/reproductive health, child health, mental health, disability support, or chronic-condition concerns, use a government health facility, PHC/CHC, district hospital, or registered clinician.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: What is Universal Health Coverage (UHC) and does India have it?
A: UHC means all people can access quality health services without financial hardship. India has made significant progress with PM-JAY, HWCs, and government hospitals, but full UHC is not yet achieved — gaps remain in coverage, quality, and financial protection, especially for the middle class and informal sector.

Q: What should I do if I cannot afford healthcare in India?
A: Check PM-JAY eligibility at pmjay.gov.in (covers poorest 40%). Use government hospitals (free basic care). Jan Aushadhi Kendras for cheap medicines. Rashtriya Arogya Nidhi for serious illness in BPL patients. State government schemes (Aarogyasri, Karunya, Biju Swasthya Kalyan Yojana). Call 104 for health guidance.

Q: Is the out-of-pocket expenditure for healthcare high in India?
A: Yes, significantly. India has one of the highest out-of-pocket health expenditure rates in the world — approximately 55% of total health expenditure is paid directly by patients. This causes financial catastrophe for 5-7% of Indian households annually. PM-JAY and UHC expansion aim to reduce this.

Q: How does India compare to other countries on UHC?
A: India's UHC Service Coverage Index (SCI) has improved significantly but remains below the global median. Countries like Thailand and Sri Lanka (with lower GDPs) have achieved stronger UHC. India's healthcare spending as % of GDP (1.2-1.5%) is among the lowest — increased public investment is essential for UHC.
