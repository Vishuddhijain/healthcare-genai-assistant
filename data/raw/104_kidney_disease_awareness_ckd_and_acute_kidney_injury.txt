CATEGORY: Non-Communicable Disease (Nephrology)

--- OVERVIEW ---
Kidney (renal) disease is a growing public health crisis in India. The kidneys perform essential functions: filtering waste and excess fluid from blood, regulating blood pressure, balancing electrolytes, and producing hormones for red blood cell production and bone health. Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) — defined as reduced kidney function or structural damage for >3 months — affects an estimated 17% of adults in India (SEEK study). India adds approximately 220,000 new end-stage renal disease (ESRD) patients annually, of whom only a fraction receive dialysis or transplantation due to cost and access barriers. Diabetes and hypertension are the top two causes of CKD in India and globally.

--- TYPES ---

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD):
- Progressive, irreversible decline in kidney function over months to years
- Staged by GFR (Glomerular Filtration Rate): G1 (>90, normal/high) → G5 (<15, kidney failure)
- Often silent until late stages — called the "silent killer"

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI):
- Sudden decline in kidney function over hours to days
- Potentially reversible if identified and treated promptly
- Causes in India: Dehydration (diarrhoea, heatstroke), severe infections (sepsis, leptospirosis, malaria, dengue), NSAIDs/painkillers, contrast dye, snake bite, crush injury

End-Stage Kidney Disease (ESKD) / Kidney Failure (CKD G5):
- Requires renal replacement therapy: dialysis (haemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis) or kidney transplantation
- Kidney transplantation is the best treatment for quality of life and survival

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Early CKD — often NO symptoms. Detected only through blood/urine tests.

As CKD progresses:
- Fatigue and weakness
- Decreased urine output or changes in urine (frothy/foamy urine = proteinuria, blood in urine = haematuria, frequent night urination)
- Swelling of feet, ankles, and legs (oedema)
- Puffiness around eyes (especially in the morning)
- High blood pressure that is difficult to control
- Nausea and vomiting
- Loss of appetite
- Itching (pruritus)
- Muscle cramps
- Shortness of breath (fluid in lungs)
- Difficulty concentrating
- Pale skin and anaemia (kidneys produce less erythropoietin)

AKI symptoms:
- Decreased or absent urine output
- Swelling
- Confusion
- Nausea and fatigue
- May be part of a broader severe illness (sepsis, dengue, snakebite)

--- RISK FACTORS ---
Primary causes (leading to CKD):
1. Diabetes mellitus — the leading cause in India; affects ~40% of CKD patients
2. Hypertension (high blood pressure) — second leading cause; both cause and effect of CKD
3. Glomerulonephritis (immune-mediated kidney inflammation) — more common in India than in Western countries; often linked to infections (streptococcal, hepatitis B, malaria, HIV)
4. Obstruction: Kidney stones, enlarged prostate — can cause CKD if prolonged
5. Chronic recurrent infections: Repeated UTIs, tuberculosis of kidney
6. Polycystic kidney disease: Inherited; multiple cysts destroy kidney tissue
7. Lupus and autoimmune conditions

Other risk factors:
- Prolonged NSAID use (ibuprofen, diclofenac, aspirin) — extremely common and under-recognised in India; painkillers are nephrotoxic
- Ayurvedic/herbal medications with heavy metals (lead, mercury, arsenic) — documented cause of CKD in India
- Occupational exposure to heavy metals (chromium, lead, cadmium)
- Dehydration (manual labourers in heat — linked to CKD of unknown cause / CKDu)
- Recurrent kidney stones
- Family history of kidney disease
- Smoking and obesity

--- PREVENTION ---
Control primary risk factors:
- Maintain blood sugar within target range (HbA1c <7% for most diabetics)
- Control blood pressure (target <130/80 mmHg; or <125/75 mmHg in CKD with proteinuria)
- Annual kidney function testing (serum creatinine, eGFR, urine albumin-creatinine ratio) for all diabetics and hypertensives — mandatory for early detection

Medication safety:
- Avoid self-medication with NSAIDs/painkillers (ibuprofen, diclofenac, ketorolac) — use sparingly and under medical supervision
- Be cautious with Ayurvedic/herbal medicines — ensure they are certified metal-free
- Always inform doctors of ALL medications (including OTC and herbal) when kidney disease is a concern

Hydration:
- Adequate fluid intake (approx. 2–3 litres/day for healthy adults) — helps prevent kidney stones and UTIs
- Avoid severe dehydration (manual labour in heat; during illness)

Diet (for those at risk — consult doctor for individualised advice):
- Reduce salt intake (<5 g/day)
- Moderate protein intake (excessive protein may stress damaged kidneys)
- Maintain healthy weight
- Avoid high-oxalate foods if history of calcium oxalate stones (spinach, nuts, tea, chocolate — in moderation)

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- Serum creatinine and eGFR (estimated GFR): Measures filtering capacity
- Urine albumin-creatinine ratio (ACR) / dipstick for protein: Detects early kidney damage
- Urine routine and microscopy: Detects blood, infection, casts
- Kidney ultrasound: Assesses size, structure, stones, obstruction
- Renal biopsy: Definitive diagnosis of glomerulonephritis and other specific diseases

Screening recommendation:
- All adults with diabetes or hypertension: Annual creatinine/eGFR and urine protein test
- Any adult with family history of CKD: Baseline screening

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
CKD management:
- Treat underlying cause (diabetes, hypertension management is paramount)
- ACE inhibitors or ARBs (BP medications): Reduce proteinuria and slow CKD progression — doctor prescribed
- SGLT2 inhibitors (newer diabetes drugs): Shown to protect kidneys — doctor prescribed
- Diet modification: Lower salt, controlled protein (nephrologist-guided)
- Treat complications: Anaemia (erythropoietin injections), bone disease, fluid overload
- Avoid nephrotoxic drugs
- CKD G5 (kidney failure): Dialysis or kidney transplantation

Dialysis in India:
- Haemodialysis: 3× per week, 3–4 hours per session at a dialysis centre
- Pradhan Mantri National Dialysis Programme (PMNDP): Free/subsidised dialysis at government hospitals for BPL beneficiaries
- PMJAY (Ayushman Bharat): Covers dialysis and kidney transplantation for eligible beneficiaries

--- KIDNEY STONES ---
- Hard deposits of minerals and salts inside kidneys
- Common in India (hot climate, dietary factors, dehydration)
- Types: Calcium oxalate (most common), uric acid, struvite, cystine
- Symptoms: Severe flank/back pain, blood in urine, nausea, frequent urination
- Small stones often pass on their own with adequate hydration
- Larger stones require medical intervention (lithotripsy, ureteroscopy, surgery)
- Prevention: High fluid intake (>2.5 litres/day), dietary modifications as advised by doctor

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Frothy/foamy or blood-tinged urine
- Persistent swelling of face or legs
- Unexplained fatigue or anaemia
- Difficult-to-control high blood pressure
- Decreased urine output
- Severe flank/back pain (kidney stone)
- Diabetic or hypertensive? Get annual kidney function tests even without symptoms
- Prolonged/regular NSAID use — get kidney function checked

--- GOVERNMENT PROGRAMMES ---

- Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY: Covers kidney transplantation (listed procedure)
- National Organ and Tissue Transplant Organisation (NOTTO): Facilitates deceased donor organ donation; notto.mohfw.gov.in
- To register as organ donor: notto.mohfw.gov.in/index.php/donor-registration

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Kidney disease is often asymptomatic in early stages — regular screening is the only way to detect it early. Do not self-medicate with painkillers or herbal medicines without medical advice. Kidney function can deteriorate rapidly without warning in some conditions — always consult a qualified nephrologist or physician for diagnosis and management.
