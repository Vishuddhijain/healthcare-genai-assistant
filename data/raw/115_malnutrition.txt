TOPIC: Malnutrition
CATEGORY: Public Health / Nutrition
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet - https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/malnutrition
LAST UPDATED: 2024

--- OVERVIEW ---
Malnutrition refers to deficiencies, excesses, or imbalances in a person's intake of energy and/or nutrients. It encompasses both undernutrition (stunting, wasting, underweight, micronutrient deficiencies) and overnutrition (overweight, obesity, diet-related NCDs). India faces a "double burden" — high rates of child undernutrition coexisting with rising obesity and lifestyle diseases. NFHS-5 data shows 35% of Indian children under 5 are stunted, 19% wasted, and 32% underweight, while adult obesity is rising rapidly.

--- TYPES ---
1. Undernutrition:
   - Stunting (low height-for-age): Chronic undernutrition
   - Wasting (low weight-for-height): Acute undernutrition
   - Underweight (low weight-for-age): Combination
   - Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM): Life-threatening; requires therapeutic feeding
2. Micronutrient-related malnutrition ("hidden hunger"): Deficiencies in iron, Vitamin A, iodine, zinc, folate
3. Overweight and obesity: Excess body fat with NCD risk
4. Diet-related NCDs: Conditions linked to poor diet (diabetes, CVD, certain cancers)

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Undernutrition:
- Visible wasting (thin limbs, prominent ribs)
- Stunted growth (short for age)
- Bilateral pitting oedema (kwashiorkor)
- Fatigue, weakness, poor concentration
- Frequent infections (weakened immunity)
- Delayed wound healing
- Hair and skin changes (kwashiorkor)

Micronutrient deficiencies:
- Iron deficiency: Fatigue, pallor (see Anaemia)
- Vitamin A deficiency: Night blindness, dry eyes, increased infection susceptibility
- Iodine deficiency: Goitre, developmental delays (cretinism in severe maternal deficiency)
- Zinc deficiency: Poor growth, impaired immunity, delayed wound healing

Overnutrition: See Obesity topic for symptoms

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
- MUAC (Mid-Upper Arm Circumference) <11.5 cm in children 6–59 months — Severe Acute Malnutrition, needs immediate referral
- Bilateral pitting oedema in a child — SAM, medical emergency
- Rapid, unexplained weight loss in any age group
- Child not meeting growth milestones on Anganwadi growth chart

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Poverty and food insecurity
- Poor infant and young child feeding practices
- Recurrent infections (diarrhoea, pneumonia, worm infestations)
- Inadequate maternal nutrition (low birth weight babies)
- Poor sanitation and hygiene (WASH)
- Early marriage/adolescent pregnancy
- Lack of dietary diversity

--- PREVENTION ---
- Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months; continued breastfeeding to 2 years
- Timely, adequate, safe, and diverse complementary feeding from 6 months
- Micronutrient supplementation: Vitamin A, IFA (Iron-Folic Acid), zinc for diarrhoea
- Deworming (National Deworming Day): Albendazole for children 1–19 years, twice yearly
- Growth monitoring at Anganwadi centres
- Dietary diversity: Include cereals, pulses, vegetables, fruits, dairy, and where possible eggs/meat

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- WASH improvements (safe water, sanitation, hygiene) reduce infection-related malnutrition
- Food fortification: Iodised salt, fortified rice/wheat flour, fortified edible oil (Vitamin A/D)

--- VACCINATION ---
- Full immunisation reduces infections that worsen malnutrition
- Measles vaccination particularly important (measles severely worsens nutritional status)

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- Anthropometry: Weight, height/length, MUAC compared to WHO growth standards
- Z-scores: Weight-for-age, height-for-age, weight-for-height
- Clinical signs: Bilateral pitting oedema, visible severe wasting
- Blood tests: Haemoglobin (anaemia), micronutrient levels where indicated

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
- SAM: Therapeutic feeding at Nutrition Rehabilitation Centres (NRCs); Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF) for community-based management
- MAM: Supplementary feeding through ICDS/Anganwadi
- Micronutrient deficiencies: Targeted supplementation (Vitamin A, IFA, zinc) under medical/programme guidance
- Treatment of underlying infections accompanying malnutrition

--- WHEN TO SEEK MEDICAL/PROGRAMME HELP ---
- Child not gaining weight on Anganwadi growth chart
- Visible wasting or leg swelling in a child
- MUAC measurement in red zone
- Any child appearing significantly smaller/thinner than peers of same age

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- POSHAN Abhiyaan (National Nutrition Mission): Targets reduction of stunting, underweight, anaemia, low birth weight
- ICDS/Anganwadi centres: Growth monitoring, supplementary nutrition, referral for SAM
- Nutrition Rehabilitation Centres (NRCs): Government facilities for SAM management
- National Food Security Act: PDS (ration shops) provide subsidised food grains
- Mid-Day Meal Scheme (PM POSHAN): School nutrition for children

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for health awareness only. Severe Acute Malnutrition is a medical emergency requiring immediate referral to a Nutrition Rehabilitation Centre. Regular growth monitoring through Anganwadi centres helps catch malnutrition early. Consult ASHA, ANM, or Anganwadi worker for nutrition support.

--- TREATMENT (AWARENESS ONLY) ---
- Treatment details are for awareness only. Consult a qualified healthcare professional.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Seek medical care for severe, persistent, worsening, or red-flag symptoms. Use emergency number 112 for life-threatening situations.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: What is the difference between stunting, wasting, and underweight in children?
A: Stunting (low height for age) reflects chronic, long-term malnutrition. Wasting (low weight for height) reflects acute, recent malnutrition and is immediately dangerous. Underweight (low weight for age) reflects a combination. Wasting requires urgent treatment. Check growth at Anganwadi centres monthly.

Q: Is malnutrition only a problem in poor families?
A: No. Hidden hunger (micronutrient deficiency) affects people across income levels. Families who eat calorie-sufficient but nutritionally poor diets (mostly refined grains, little fruit/vegetable diversity) can have children with micronutrient deficiencies. Urban malnutrition is a growing concern in India.

Q: What free nutrition services are available for children in India?
A: Anganwadi centres provide free supplementary nutrition (khichdi, take-home rations), growth monitoring, Vitamin A supplementation, deworming, and nutrition counselling under ICDS and POSHAN Abhiyaan. These are for children 0-6 years and pregnant/lactating women. Visit your nearest Anganwadi.

Q: How do I know if my child is growing well?
A: Monthly growth monitoring at Anganwadi centres uses a growth chart. The red zone (below -3 SD) indicates severe acute malnutrition. The yellow zone indicates moderate malnutrition. A child in the red zone needs immediate medical attention at a Nutrition Rehabilitation Centre (NRC) — free treatment.
