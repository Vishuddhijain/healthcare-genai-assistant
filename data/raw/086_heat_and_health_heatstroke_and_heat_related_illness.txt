--- OVERVIEW ---
Extreme heat is one of the deadliest weather phenomena and a growing public health threat due to climate change. India experiences severe heat waves annually, particularly in April–June. Heat-related illnesses range from heat cramps and heat exhaustion to life-threatening heatstroke. Vulnerable populations — elderly, outdoor workers, infants, and those with chronic illness — are at greatest risk. Heat illness is almost entirely preventable.

--- TYPES ---
1. Heat cramps: Painful muscle spasms, usually in legs or abdomen, during or after exertion in heat.
2. Heat exhaustion: Heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness, nausea, headache, cool/pale/moist skin, rapid weak pulse. Body temperature normal or slightly elevated. Can progress to heatstroke.
3. Heatstroke: Medical emergency. Body temperature ≥40°C (104°F). Hot, dry skin (classic) or sweating (exertional). Confusion, loss of consciousness, seizures. Organ failure can occur. FATAL if untreated.
4. Heat syncope: Sudden fainting due to reduced blood flow to brain in heat.
5. Heat oedema: Swelling of hands, feet, ankles in heat.
6. Prickly heat (miliaria): Skin rash from blocked sweat glands.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Heat is the leading cause of weather-related death globally.
- India has experienced increasingly severe heat waves — temperatures above 45°C recorded annually in northern and central India.
- Over 6,500 people died in heat waves in India between 1992–2015 (official data; actual number likely much higher).
- Climate change is making heat waves more frequent, longer, and more intense.
- Outdoor labourers, farmers, the elderly, and people without access to fans/AC are most at risk.
- Heatstroke mortality can exceed 50% if treatment is delayed.

--- SYMPTOMS ---
Heat Exhaustion:
- Heavy sweating
- Cold, pale, clammy skin
- Weakness and fatigue
- Dizziness or fainting
- Nausea or vomiting
- Headache
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Body temperature may be normal or up to 39°C

Heatstroke (EMERGENCY):
- High body temperature ≥40°C (104°F)
- Hot skin — may be dry (classic heatstroke) or moist (exertional)
- Confusion, altered mental status, slurred speech
- Unconsciousness or seizures
- Rapid, strong pulse
- Nausea and vomiting
→ CALL 112 IMMEDIATELY — heatstroke is life-threatening

--- RISK FACTORS ---
- Outdoor work in direct sun (farmers, construction workers, MNREGA workers)
- Elderly age (reduced ability to regulate body temperature)
- Infants and young children
- Chronic illness (heart disease, diabetes, kidney disease)
- Obesity
- Dehydration
- Alcohol use (impairs thermoregulation)
- Certain medications (diuretics, beta-blockers, antipsychotics)
- Poor housing (no fan or cooler, tin/asbestos roofs)
- Urban heat island effect (cities are hotter than rural areas)

--- PREVENTION ---
Personal protection:
- Stay hydrated: Drink water regularly even if not thirsty — at least 1 glass every hour during heat.
- Wear lightweight, loose, light-coloured cotton clothing.
- Stay out of the sun during peak hours (11 AM – 4 PM).
- Use umbrella, hat, or dupatta when going out.
- Stay in cool, shaded, or air-conditioned places during peak heat.
- Use wet cloth on neck, wrists, and forehead to cool down.
- Avoid strenuous physical activity outdoors during peak heat.
- Never leave children, elderly, or pets in a parked vehicle.

Drinks and diet:
- Drink ORS, lassi, buttermilk, nimbu pani, coconut water — replenish electrolytes.
- Avoid alcohol and caffeinated drinks during heat (cause dehydration).
- Eat light, cooling foods — cucumber, curd, watermelon, raw mango (kairi) panna.

At-risk population protection:
- Check on elderly neighbours and relatives during heat waves.
- Ensure outdoor workers have adequate water, rest, and shade (legal obligation of employers).
- Cool infants with tepid sponging; avoid bundling in heat.

--- ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL ---
- National Action Plan on Heat-Related Illness: India's NDMA has guidelines for heat action plans.
- State Heat Action Plans: Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Gujarat, Odisha, and other states operate heat response systems.
- Cool rooms/cooling centres: Government institutions (hospitals, schools) open as cooling centres during heat waves.
- IMD (India Meteorological Department) issues heat wave alerts — follow official alerts.

--- VACCINATION ---
No vaccine for heat illness. Influenza vaccine is recommended for vulnerable populations.

--- DIAGNOSIS ---
- Heatstroke: Clinical diagnosis — high core body temperature (rectal temperature preferred) with neurological symptoms.
- Blood tests: Electrolytes, kidney function, liver function, clotting — assess organ damage.
- ECG: Assess cardiac involvement.

--- TREATMENT (Awareness Only) ---
Heat Exhaustion:
- Move to cool place immediately
- Remove excess clothing
- Cool with cool water, fan, wet cloth
- Give cool water or ORS to drink if conscious
- If no improvement in 30 minutes — go to hospital

Heatstroke (EMERGENCY — call 112):
- Move immediately to cool place
- Remove clothing
- Apply ice packs to neck, armpits, and groin
- Fan while spraying cool water on body
- Do NOT give fluids to an unconscious person (aspiration risk)
- Rapid cooling is the life-saving intervention — don't wait for the hospital

--- WARNING SIGNS ---
- Confusion, altered behaviour in someone who has been in the heat
- Collapse
- Seizures
- Hot, dry skin (dangerous heatstroke sign)
→ All of these = CALL 112 IMMEDIATELY

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Any symptoms of heat exhaustion not resolving with rest and cooling
- All cases of suspected heatstroke — emergency
- Elderly person unwell during heat wave — lower threshold to seek care

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC CONTEXT ---
- Heat Action Plans operational in: Ahmedabad, Nagpur, Pune, Bhubaneswar, Lucknow, and other cities.
- NDMA Heat Wave Guidelines: ndma.gov.in
- IMD heat wave alerts: imd.gov.in or Mausam app
- Loo (hot, dry wind): Extremely dangerous dust-laden wind in North India during summer (April–June) — avoid direct exposure; stay indoors.
- MNREGA workers and daily wage labourers are among the most vulnerable — employers must provide shade, water, rest periods.
- NIMHANS summer heat awareness: Heat also worsens mental health conditions.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: How much water should I drink during a heat wave?
A: At least 8–10 glasses (2–2.5 litres) per day, more if working outdoors or sweating heavily. Drink before you feel thirsty. Add a pinch of salt and sugar to water if electrolytes are lost.

Q: Is raw mango panna good for heat?
A: Yes. Kairi (raw mango) panna is a traditional Indian cooling drink that helps replace electrolytes and prevent heat exhaustion. It is widely recommended by health authorities in India.

Q: What is the difference between heat exhaustion and heatstroke?
A: Heat exhaustion: heavy sweating, weakness, normal or slightly elevated temperature — manageable with cooling and hydration. Heatstroke: temperature ≥40°C, confusion or unconsciousness, hot dry skin — life-threatening emergency requiring 112.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
Heatstroke is a life-threatening emergency. Call 112 immediately. Begin cooling while waiting for help. This information is for public awareness and first-line response only.
