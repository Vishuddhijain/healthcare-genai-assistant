--- OVERVIEW ---
Health literacy means being able to access, understand, appraise and use information and services in ways that promote and maintain good health and well-being.

Health literacy means more than being able to access web sites, read pamphlets and follow prescribed health-seeking behaviours. It includes the ability to think critically about, as well as the ability to interact and express personal and societal needs for promoting health.

WHO defines health literacy as “representing the personal knowledge and competencies that accumulate through daily activities, social interactions and across generations. Personal knowledge and competencies are mediated by the organizational structures and availability of resources that enable people to access, understand, appraise, and use information and services in ways that promote and maintain good health and well-being for themselves and those around them.”

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Health literacy represents the personal knowledge and competencies that accumulate through daily activities, social interactions and across generations.
- Health literacy is mediated by the organizational structures and availability of resources that enable people to access, understand, appraise and use information and services in ways that promote and maintain good health and well-being for themselves and those around them – often described as organizational health literacy.
- In the United States of America, for example, health literacy is a stronger predictor of an individual’s health status than income, employment status, education level and racial or ethnic group.
- Even in economically advanced countries in Europe , many children, adolescents and adults have limited health literacy skills.
- According to surveys in the WHO European Region , population health literacy follows a social gradient and can further reinforce existing inequalities.
- Health literacy is a determinant of health.

--- WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR ---
- Consult a qualified healthcare professional if symptoms are severe, persistent, recurrent, worsening, or affecting daily life.
- Seek urgent care for red-flag symptoms such as breathing difficulty, chest pain, heavy bleeding, severe dehydration, confusion, fainting, seizures, pregnancy danger signs, suspected poisoning/exposure, or signs of severe infection.
- For prevention, screening, vaccination, sexual/reproductive health, child health, mental health, disability support, or chronic-condition concerns, use a government health facility, PHC/CHC, district hospital, or registered clinician.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: What is health literacy and why does it matter?
A: Health literacy is the ability to understand, evaluate, and use health information to make good decisions. Low health literacy leads to poor medication adherence, delayed care-seeking, misunderstanding of diagnoses, and worse health outcomes. High health literacy empowers people to manage their health effectively.

Q: How can I improve my own health literacy?
A: Ask your doctor to explain in simple language. Write down instructions. Ask 'What do I do if...?' questions. Use reliable sources: nhp.gov.in, who.int, and mohfw.gov.in. Be sceptical of health claims on social media. Ask a pharmacist to explain medicines.

Q: How can I spot health misinformation?
A: Warning signs: promises of miraculous cures, claims that doctors hide the truth, extreme urgency to share, no credible source cited, appeals to conspiracy theories. Always cross-check with government or WHO sources. If in doubt, ask a qualified healthcare professional.
