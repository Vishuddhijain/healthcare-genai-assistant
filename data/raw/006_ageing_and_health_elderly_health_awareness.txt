--- OVERVIEW ---
Ageing is a natural process. By 2030, 1 in 6 people in the world will be aged 60 or over. In India, the elderly population (60+) is projected to reach 194 million by 2031. While ageing brings challenges, healthy ageing — maintaining functional ability and quality of life into older age — is achievable with the right care, lifestyle, and support.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- By 2030, 1 in 6 people globally will be aged 60+.
- India's elderly population (60+) is approximately 140 million currently and growing rapidly.
- Most elderly deaths are from non-communicable diseases: heart disease, stroke, diabetes, COPD, cancer.
- Falls are the second leading cause of unintentional injury deaths globally — most common in elderly.
- Dementia affects 55 million people globally; Alzheimer's disease is the most common form.
- Many health conditions in the elderly are preventable or manageable with timely care.

--- COMMON HEALTH CONDITIONS IN ELDERLY ---
1. Heart disease and stroke (see 16_cardiovascular_health.txt)
2. Diabetes (see 01_diabetes.txt)
3. Hypertension (see 02_hypertension.txt)
4. Arthritis: Joint pain and inflammation. Very common. Exercise, healthy weight, and doctor-prescribed treatment help.
5. Osteoporosis and fractures (see 28_bone_health.txt)
6. Dementia (see 31_dementia.txt)
7. Depression and loneliness: Very common but often undiagnosed in elderly. Social connection is vital.
8. COPD and respiratory diseases (see 17_asthma_copd.txt)
9. Visual impairment (cataract, glaucoma) (see 22_eye_health.txt)
10. Hearing loss (see 36_deafness_hearing_loss.txt)
11. Malnutrition: Common in elderly due to reduced appetite, poor dental health, or limited food access.
12. Urinary incontinence: Affects quality of life. Treatable — discuss with doctor.

--- FALLS PREVENTION ---
Falls are extremely common and dangerous in elderly. A fall can cause hip fracture leading to prolonged disability or death.
Prevention:
- Keep home well-lit, especially pathways at night
- Remove trip hazards: rugs, loose tiles, clutter on floors
- Install grab bars near toilet and bathtub
- Wear non-slip footwear (avoid walking barefoot or in socks on smooth floors)
- Exercise regularly to improve strength and balance (yoga, walking)
- Have eyes and hearing checked regularly
- Review medications with doctor — some cause dizziness
- Use walking aids (stick, walker) if recommended by doctor

--- NUTRITION FOR ELDERLY ---
- Adequate protein: Prevents muscle loss (sarcopenia). Eat dal, eggs, curd, fish, paneer.
- Calcium and Vitamin D: Critical for bones. Milk, curd, ragi, 15 min morning sunlight.
- Fibre: Prevents constipation. Fruits, vegetables, whole grains.
- Hydration: Elderly often don't feel thirst — drink water regularly even without feeling thirsty.
- Reduce salt: Reduces hypertension risk.
- Smaller, more frequent meals if appetite is reduced.

--- PHYSICAL ACTIVITY FOR ELDERLY ---
Even light activity significantly improves health:
- Walking 30 minutes daily
- Chair-based exercises if mobility is limited
- Yoga and balance exercises (reduces fall risk)
- Swimming if accessible
- Consult a doctor before starting a new exercise programme

--- MENTAL HEALTH AND SOCIAL CONNECTION ---
- Loneliness and social isolation are serious health risks in the elderly — comparable to smoking.
- Stay connected with family, friends, neighbours.
- Engage in hobbies, community activities, or religious/spiritual activities.
- Depression in elderly is common but treatable. Signs: persistent sadness, withdrawal, loss of appetite, sleep changes. Tell a doctor.
- Mental activity: Reading, puzzles, learning new skills — helps maintain cognitive function.

--- MEDICATION SAFETY FOR ELDERLY ---
- Many elderly take multiple medications (polypharmacy) — increases risk of side effects and interactions.
- Maintain a list of all medications and show to every doctor.
- Never stop or change doses without doctor's advice.
- Avoid buying medicines without prescription.
- Some medicines cause dizziness or falls — review with doctor regularly.

--- INDIA-SPECIFIC SCHEMES FOR ELDERLY ---
1. National Programme for Health Care of the Elderly (NPHCE): Free healthcare for elderly at government hospitals.
2. Pradhan Mantri Vaya Vandana Yojana (PMVVY): Pension scheme for senior citizens.
3. Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY: Covers hospital costs for eligible elderly.
4. Senior Citizens Savings Scheme: Financial security for elderly.
5. Indira Gandhi National Old Age Pension Scheme (IGNOAPS): Monthly pension for BPL elderly.
Free health check-ups for elderly at Ayushman Bharat Health and Wellness Centres.

--- CAREGIVING TIPS ---
- Involve elderly in daily decisions — preserves dignity and independence.
- Monitor for signs of depression, confusion, or memory loss.
- Ensure regular medication compliance.
- Schedule regular doctor visits — at least every 3–6 months for those with chronic conditions.
- Watch for signs of elder abuse (physical, financial, emotional) — report to local authorities.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: Is memory loss normal with ageing?
A: Some mild forgetfulness (e.g., occasionally misplacing items) is normal. But significant memory loss, confusion, getting lost in familiar places, or personality changes could indicate dementia. Consult a doctor.

Q: Should elderly people avoid all exercise?
A: No. Regular moderate activity (walking, yoga) is one of the best things an elderly person can do for their health. Rest is only needed after injury or illness as advised by a doctor.

Q: How often should an elderly person see a doctor?
A: Ideally every 3–6 months for those with chronic conditions. At minimum annually for blood pressure, blood sugar, and eye checks. More frequently if symptoms arise.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
This information is for awareness only. Health conditions in elderly should be managed by a qualified doctor. Emergency symptoms (chest pain, stroke signs, falls with injury) require calling 112 immediately.
