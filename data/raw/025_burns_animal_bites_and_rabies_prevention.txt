--- SECTION 1: BURNS ---

--- OVERVIEW ---
Burns are injuries to the skin and underlying tissues caused by heat, chemicals, electricity, radiation, or friction. In India, burns are a significant public health problem — especially among women cooking on open fires and children in households using solid fuels. Burns are largely preventable.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Burns cause approximately 180,000 deaths globally each year.
- India accounts for a significant proportion — over 1 million people suffer moderate to severe burns annually.
- Women and children are disproportionately affected due to cooking-related injuries.
- Kerosene and LPG incidents are a major cause of severe burns in India.
- 90% of burns occur in low- and middle-income countries.

--- CLASSIFICATION OF BURNS ---
1. First-degree (superficial): Only outer skin (epidermis). Redness, pain, no blisters. Heals in 3–5 days.
   Example: Mild sunburn.
2. Second-degree (partial thickness): Epidermis + part of dermis. Blisters, intense pain, red/moist skin.
   Requires medical care.
3. Third-degree (full thickness): All layers of skin destroyed. White, brown, or black charred appearance. Often painless (nerve damage). Always requires hospitalisation.

--- FIRST AID FOR BURNS ---
For minor burns (small area, first or second degree):
- COOL immediately with cool (not cold/ice) running water for at least 10–20 minutes
- Remove jewellery or tight clothing near the burn (before swelling begins)
- Cover loosely with a clean, non-fluffy bandage or cloth
- Give paracetamol for pain if needed (consult pharmacist)
- See a doctor for blisters or burns larger than palm size

DO NOT:
- Apply ice — causes frostbite and worsens injury
- Apply butter, oil, toothpaste, aloe vera (fresh), egg white — all increase infection risk
- Burst blisters — protects against infection
- Use fluffy/cotton wool directly on wound — fibres stick

Seek EMERGENCY care immediately for:
- Burns covering more than 10% of body surface area
- Burns on face, hands, feet, genitals, or over major joints
- All third-degree burns
- Chemical or electrical burns
- Burns in children, elderly, or pregnant women
- Burns with difficulty breathing (smoke inhalation)

--- CHEMICAL BURNS ---
- Flush with large amounts of water for 20 minutes — do NOT use neutralising agents (vinegar, baking soda) — makes it worse
- Remove contaminated clothing while flushing (protect yourself from secondary exposure)
- Call 112 and go to emergency immediately

--- BURN PREVENTION ---
- Switch to LPG or electric cooking (Ujjwala Yojana provides free LPG connection to BPL households)
- Keep children away from cooking areas
- Never leave cooking unattended
- Wear fitted cotton clothing while cooking (not synthetic — melts and sticks)
- Handle firecrackers with extreme caution, use long sparklers for children
- Install smoke alarms in homes
- Check LPG pipes and cylinders for leaks regularly

--- SECTION 2: ANIMAL BITES AND RABIES ---

--- OVERVIEW ---
Animal bites — especially dog bites — are a major public health concern in India. India has the highest burden of human rabies in the world, accounting for approximately 36% of global rabies deaths. Rabies is fatal once symptoms appear but is 100% preventable with prompt post-exposure treatment.

--- KEY FACTS ---
- Approximately 18,000–20,000 people die of rabies in India every year.
- Dog bites are responsible for up to 96% of human rabies cases.
- India has the world's largest stray dog population.
- Children (5–15 years) are disproportionately affected — they play with animals and may not report bites.
- Once symptoms appear, rabies is nearly 100% fatal. Prevention is critical.
- Rabies is 100% preventable with proper wound care + post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP).

--- WHAT TO DO AFTER AN ANIMAL BITE ---
Immediate first aid (within seconds — do not wait):
1. Wash the wound immediately and thoroughly with soap and water for at least 15 minutes (running water best).
2. Apply antiseptic (povidone-iodine or 70% alcohol) after washing.
3. Do NOT suture the wound immediately (increases rabies risk).
4. Go to a hospital or PHC IMMEDIATELY — even if the animal appears healthy.

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP):
- PEP consists of: rabies vaccine doses + Rabies Immunoglobulin (RIG) for deep bites.
- PEP is FREE at all government hospitals and district hospitals in India.
- PEP must be started as soon as possible after the bite — ideally within 24 hours.
- Even if bitten days ago: Go to a hospital — PEP can still work before symptoms appear.
- Never delay PEP thinking the animal "looked healthy" — infected animals can appear normal.

Categories of exposure (WHO):
- Category I (touching or feeding animal, licks on intact skin): No PEP needed. Wash hands.
- Category II (nibbling, minor scratches, licks on broken skin): Wound washing + vaccine.
- Category III (deep bites, scratches drawing blood, licks on mucous membranes, contact with bat): Wound washing + full vaccine course + RIG.

The animal that bit you:
- If it's your pet and vaccinated: Still go to a hospital — doctor will advise.
- Observe the animal for 10 days if possible (under veterinary supervision).
- Do NOT kill the animal immediately — it prevents observation.

--- OTHER ANIMAL BITES ---
Snake bites: See 09_first_aid.txt — keep calm, immobilise, rush to hospital for antivenom.
Monkey bites: Common in India near temples. Treat like dog bites — wash thoroughly and get PEP.
Rat bites: Wash wound thoroughly. Risk of rat bite fever and leptospirosis. See a doctor.
Stray cat bites: Cat scratch disease and tetanus risk. Wash thoroughly, see a doctor for tetanus.

--- PREVENTION ---
- Vaccinate pet dogs and cats — this is the most effective intervention to eliminate rabies.
- Teach children to avoid stray animals and not to provoke them.
- Do not try to handle or rescue injured or sick wild animals with bare hands.
- Support community dog vaccination programmes.
- Ensure your pet dog's rabies vaccination is up to date.

--- FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ---
Q: A dog bit me but the skin didn't break. Should I still get PEP?
A: If skin is unbroken (Category I), PEP is not needed. Wash the area thoroughly. If there is any uncertainty about whether skin was broken — consult a doctor.

Q: Can I get rabies from a vaccinated dog?
A: Risk is very low, but not zero. The dog may not be fully protected, or the vaccine may have been administered incorrectly. Always consult a doctor after any animal bite.

Q: I was bitten 3 weeks ago and didn't do anything. Is it too late?
A: No! Go to a hospital immediately. PEP can still prevent rabies before symptoms appear, regardless of how long ago the bite occurred.

Q: If someone has rabies symptoms, can they be saved?
A: Unfortunately, once symptoms appear, rabies is almost universally fatal. This is why immediate post-exposure treatment is critical.

--- IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE ---
All animal bites should be evaluated by a doctor. Rabies PEP is free at government hospitals. For severe bites, heavy bleeding, or suspected rabies exposure — call 112 immediately.
